## MEAN FOR AN UNGROUPED FREQUENCY DISTRIBUTION (DIRECT METHOD)

Let $n$ observations consist of values $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ of a variable $x$, occurring with frequencies $f_{1}, f_{2}, \ldots, f_{n}$ respectively. Then, the mean, $\bar{x}$, of these observations is given by

$$
\bar{x}=\frac{\left(f_{1} x_{1}+f_{2} x_{2}+\ldots+f_{n} x_{n}\right)}{\left(f_{1}+f_{2}+\ldots+f_{n}\right)}=\frac{\sum\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\sum f_{i}} .
$$

## SOLVED EXAMPLES

EXAMPLE 1 The following table shows the weights of 12 persons of a health club:

| Weight (in kg ) | 67 | 70 | 72 | 73 | 75 |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Number of persons | 4 | 3 | 2 | 2 | 1 |

Find the mean weight per person.
solution For calculating the mean, we prepare the table given below:

| Weight (in kg) $x_{i}$ | Number of persons $f_{i}$ | ( $f_{i} \times x_{i}$ ) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 67 | 4 | 268 |
| 70 | 3 | 210 |
| 72 | 2 | 144 |
| 73 | 2 | 146 |
| 75 | 1 | 75 |
|  | $\Sigma f_{i}=12$ | $\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)=843$ |

$$
\therefore \quad \text { mean, } \bar{x}=\frac{\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\Sigma f_{i}}=\frac{843}{12}=70.25 .
$$

Hence, the mean weight is 70.250 kg .
EXAMPLE 2 The heights of 100 plants in a garden are given below:

| Height (in cm ) <br> $x_{i}$ | 60 | 64 | 68 | 72 | 76 |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Number of plants <br> $f_{i}$ | 5 | 16 | 40 | 30 | 9 |

Find the mean height per plant.
SOLUTION
For calculating the mean, we prepare the table given below:

| Height (in cm) $x_{i}$ | Number of plants $f_{i}$ | ( $f_{i} \times x_{i}$ ) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 60 | 5 | 300 |
| 64 | 16 | 1024 |
| 68 | 40 | 2720 |
| 72 | 30 | 2160 |
| 76 | 9 | 684 |
|  | $\Sigma f_{i}=100$ | $\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)=6888$ |

$\therefore \quad$ mean, $\bar{x}=\frac{\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\Sigma f_{i}}=\frac{6888}{100}=68.88$.
Hence, the mean height per plant is 68.88 cm .
EXAMPLE 3 If the mean of the following data is 18.75 , find the value of $p$.

| $x_{i}$ | 10 | 15 | $p$ | 25 | 30 |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| $f_{i}$ | 5 | 10 | 7 | 8 | 2 |

We may prepare the frequency table as given below:

| $x_{i}$ | $f_{i}$ | $f_{i} \times x_{i}$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 10 | 5 | 50 |
| 15 | 10 | 150 |
| $p$ | 7 | $7 p$ |
| 25 | 8 | 200 |
| 30 | 2 | 60 |
|  | $\Sigma f_{i}=32$ | $\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)=460+7 p$ |

$$
\therefore \quad \text { mean }=\frac{\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\Sigma f_{i}}=\frac{460+7 p}{32} .
$$

But, mean $=18.75$ (given).

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \frac{460+7 p}{32}=18.75 & \Rightarrow 460+7 p=18.75 \times 32 \\
& \Rightarrow 460+7 p=600 \\
& \Rightarrow 7 p=140 \Rightarrow p=20 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $p=20$.
EXAMPLE 4 The mean of the following data is 21.6. Find the value of $p$.

| $x_{i}$ | 6 | 12 | 18 | 24 | 30 | 36 |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| $f_{i}$ | 5 | 4 | $p$ | 6 | 4 | 6 |

For calculating the mean, we prepare the table given below:

| $x_{i}$ | $f_{i}$ | $f_{i} \times x_{i}$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 6 | 5 | 30 |
| 12 | 4 | 48 |
| 18 | $p$ | $18 p$ |
| 24 | 6 | 144 |
| 30 | 4 | 120 |
| 36 | 6 | 216 |
|  | $\Sigma f_{i}=25+p$ | $\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)=558+18 p$ |

$$
\therefore \quad \text { mean }=\frac{\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\Sigma f_{i}}=\frac{558+18 p}{25+p} .
$$

But, mean $=21.6$ (given).

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \frac{558+18 p}{25+p}=21.6 & \Rightarrow 558+18 p=540+21.6 p \\
& \Rightarrow 3.6 p=18 \\
& \Rightarrow p=\left(\frac{18}{3.6}\right)=\left(\frac{180}{36}\right)=5 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $p=5$.
EXAMPLE 5 The mean of the following frequency distribution is 1.46.

| No. of accidents $(x)$ | 0 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 | Total |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| No. of drivers $(f)$ | 46 | $?$ | $?$ | 25 | 10 | 5 | 200 |

Find the missing frequencies.

SOLUTION Let the missing frequencies be $p$ and $q$ respectively. For calculating the mean, we prepare the table given below:

| $x_{i}$ | $f_{i}$ | $f_{i} \times x_{i}$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0 | 46 | 0 |
| 1 | $p$ | $p$ |
| 2 | $q$ | $2 q$ |
| 3 | 25 | 75 |
| 4 | 10 | 40 |
| 5 | 5 | 25 |
|  | $\Sigma f_{i}=86+p+q$ | $\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)=140+p+2 q$ |

Here, $\Sigma f_{i}=86+p+q$.
But, $\Sigma f_{i}=200$ (given).
$\therefore \quad 86+p+q=200 \Rightarrow p+q=114$.

Also, mean $=\frac{\Sigma\left(f_{i} \times x_{i}\right)}{\Sigma f_{i}}=\frac{140+p+2 q}{86+p+q}=\frac{140+114+q}{86+114}$ [using (i)]

$$
=\frac{254+q}{200} .
$$

But, mean $=1.46$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \frac{254+q}{200}=1.46 & \Rightarrow 254+q=292 \\
& \Rightarrow q=292-254=38
\end{aligned}
$$

Putting $q=38$ in (i), we get $p=76$.
Hence, the missing frequencies are 76 and 38 respectively.
