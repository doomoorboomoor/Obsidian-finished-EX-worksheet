## Mean, Median, and Mode of Ungrouped Data

## Arithmetic Mean

The average of a given set of numbers is called the **arithmetic mean**, or simply the **mean**, of the given numbers.

$$
\text{Mean} = \frac{\text{sum of observations}}{\text{number of observations}}
$$

Thus, the mean of $n$ observations $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ is given by:

$$
\bar{x} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}}{n}
$$

where the symbol $\Sigma$, called **sigma**, stands for summation, and we write:

$$
(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i}
$$

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1

The heights of five players are $153 \text{ cm}, 140 \text{ cm}, 148 \text{ cm}, 150 \text{ cm}$ and $154 \text{ cm}$ respectively. Find the mean height of the five players.

#### Solution:

$$
\text{Mean height} = \frac{\text{sum of the heights of the players}}{\text{number of players}}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{(153 + 140 + 148 + 150 + 154)}{5} \text{ cm} \\
& = \frac{745}{5} \text{ cm} = 149 \text{ cm}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the mean height of the five players is **149 cm**.

### Example: 2

Find the mean of the first six multiples of 3.

#### Solution:

The first six multiples of 3 are $3, 6, 9, 12, 15$ and $18$.

$$
\therefore \quad \text{their mean} = \frac{(3 + 6 + 9 + 12 + 15 + 18)}{6} = \frac{63}{6} = 10 \frac{1}{2}
$$

### Example: 3

Find the mean of the first eight prime numbers.

#### Solution:

The first eight prime numbers are $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \text{their mean} & = \frac{(2 + 3 + 5 + 7 + 11 + 13 + 17 + 19)}{8} \\
& = \frac{77}{8} = 9 \frac{5}{8}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 4

If the mean of $6, 8, 9, x, 13$ is $10$, find the value of $x$.

#### Solution:

Mean of the given numbers $= \frac{(6 + 8 + 9 + x + 13)}{5} = \frac{(36 + x)}{5}$.

But, the mean is given as $10$.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{36+x}{5} = 10 \Rightarrow 36 + x = 50
$$

$$
\Rightarrow x = (50 - 36) = 14
$$

Hence, the value of $x$ is **14**.

---

### Example: 5

If the mean of the observations $x, x+3, x+5, x+7, x+10$ is $9$, find the mean of the last three observations.

#### Solution:

Mean of the given five observations:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{x + (x+3) + (x+5) + (x+7) + (x+10)}{5} \\
& = \frac{(5x + 25)}{5} = \frac{5(x+5)}{5} = (x+5)
\end{aligned}
$$

But, the mean of these observations is given as $9$.

$$
\therefore x+5=9 \Rightarrow x=9-5=4
$$

So, the last three observations are $(4+5), (4+7), (4+10)$, i.e., $9, 11, 14$.

Mean of these three observations $= \frac{(9 + 11 + 14)}{3} = \frac{34}{3} = 11 \frac{1}{3}$.

### Example: 6

The mean of five numbers is $30$. If one number is excluded, their mean becomes $28$. Find the excluded number.

#### Solution:

Mean of the given five numbers $= 30$.
Sum of these five numbers $= (30 \times 5) = 150$.

Mean of the remaining four numbers $= 28$.
Sum of these four numbers $= (28 \times 4) = 112$.

Excluded number $= (150 - 112) = 38$.

### Example: 7

The mean of the heights of six girls is $148 \text{ cm}$. If the individual heights of five of them are $150 \text{ cm}, 154 \text{ cm}, 146 \text{ cm}, 142 \text{ cm}$ and $145 \text{ cm}$, find the height of the sixth girl.

#### Solution:

Mean of the heights of six girls $= 148 \text{ cm}$.
Sum of the heights of six girls $= (148 \times 6) \text{ cm} = 888 \text{ cm}$.

Sum of the heights of the five girls:

$$
= (150 + 154 + 146 + 142 + 145) \text{ cm} = 737 \text{ cm}
$$

Height of the sixth girl $= (888 - 737) \text{ cm} = 151 \text{ cm}$.

Hence, the height of the sixth girl is **151 cm**.

### Example: 8

The mean of $40$ numbers was found to be $38$. Later on, it was detected that a number $56$ was misread as $36$. Find the correct mean of the given numbers.

#### Solution:

Calculated mean of 40 numbers $= 38$.
$\therefore$ calculated sum of these numbers $= (38 \times 40) = 1520$.

Correct sum of these numbers:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = [1520 - (\text{wrong item}) + (\text{correct item})] \\
& = (1520 - 36 + 56) = 1540
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore$ the correct mean $= \frac{1540}{40} = 38.5$.

### Example: 9

The mean of $25$ observations is $36$. Out of these observations, if the mean of the first $13$ observations is $32$ and that of the last $13$ observations is $40$, find the $13^{th}$ observation.

#### Solution:

Mean of 25 observations $= 36$.
Sum of 25 observations $= (36 \times 25) = 900$.

Mean of first 13 observations $= 32$.
Sum of first 13 observations $= (32 \times 13) = 416$.

Mean of last 13 observations $= 40$.
Sum of last 13 observations $= (40 \times 13) = 520$.

The $13^{th}$ observation is:
$= (\text{sum of first 13 observations}) + (\text{sum of last 13 observations}) - (\text{sum of 25 observations})$
$= (416 + 520) - 900 = 936 - 900 = 36$.

Hence, the 13th observation is **36**.

### Example: 10

The mean of $100$ items was found to be $64$. Later on it was discovered that two items were misread as $26$ and $9$ instead of $36$ and $90$ respectively. Find the correct mean.

#### Solution:

Mean of 100 items, as calculated $= 64$.
Sum of 100 items, as calculated $= (64 \times 100) = 6400$.

Correct sum of 100 items:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \{6400 - (\text{sum of wrong items}) + (\text{sum of correct items})\} \\
& = \{6400 - (26 + 9) + (36 + 90)\} \\
& = (6400 - 35 + 126) = (6526 - 35) = 6491
\end{aligned}
$$

Correct mean $= \left(\frac{6491}{100}\right) = 64.91$.

### Example: 11

The mean weight of a class of $35$ students is $45 \text{ kg}$. If the weight of the teacher be included, the mean weight increases by $500 \text{ g}$. Find the weight of the teacher.

#### Solution:

Mean weight of 35 students $= 45 \text{ kg}$.
Total weight of 35 students $= (45 \times 35) \text{ kg} = 1575 \text{ kg}$.

Mean weight of 35 students and the teacher:
$= (45 + 0.5) \text{ kg} = 45.5 \text{ kg}$.

Total weight of 35 students and the teacher:
$= (45.5 \times 36) \text{ kg} = 1638 \text{ kg}$.

Weight of the teacher $= (1638 - 1575) \text{ kg} = 63 \text{ kg}$.

Hence, the weight of the teacher is **63 kg**.

### Example: 12

The average temperature of Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday was $40^{\circ}\text{C}$. The average temperature of Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday was $41^{\circ}\text{C}$. If the temperature on Thursday was $42^{\circ}\text{C}$, what was the temperature on Monday?

#### Solution:

Sum of temperatures on Monday, Tuesday and Wednesday:

$$
= (40 \times 3)^{\circ}\text{C} = 120^{\circ}\text{C} \tag{i}
$$

Sum of temperatures on Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday:

$$
= (41 \times 3)^{\circ}\text{C} = 123^{\circ}\text{C} \tag{ii}
$$

Temperature on Thursday $= 42^{\circ}\text{C}$ (iii).

$\therefore$ the temperature on Monday $= (120 + 42 - 123)^{\circ}\text{C} = 39^{\circ}\text{C}$.
(Obtained by $\{(\text{i}) + (\text{iii}) - (\text{ii})\}$)

Hence, the temperature on Monday was $39^{\circ}\text{C}$.

### Example: 13

A cricketer has a mean score of $58$ runs in nine innings. Find out how many runs are to be scored by him in the tenth innings to raise the mean score to $61$.

#### Solution:

Mean score of 9 innings $= 58$ runs.
Total score of 9 innings $= (58 \times 9)$ runs $= 522$ runs.

Required mean score of 10 innings $= 61$ runs.
Required total score of 10 innings $= (61 \times 10)$ runs $= 610$ runs.

Number of runs to be scored in the 10th innings:
$= (\text{total score of 10 innings}) - (\text{total score of 9 innings})$
$= 610 - 522 = 88$.

Hence, the number of runs to be scored in the 10th innings is **88**.

### Example: 14

A batsman in his $12^{th}$ innings makes a score of $63$ runs and thereby increases his average score by $2$. What is his average after the $12^{th}$ innings?

#### Solution:

Let the average score of 12 innings be $x$.
Then, the average score of 11 innings $= (x-2)$.
Total score of 12 innings $= 12x$.
Total score of 11 innings $= 11(x-2) = (11x - 22)$.

Score of the 12th innings:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{total score of 12 innings}) - (\text{total score of 11 innings}) \\
& = [12x - (11x - 22)] = (x+22)
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\therefore \quad x + 22 = 63 \Rightarrow x = 41
$$

Hence, the average score after the 12th innings is **41**.

### Example: 15

A train travels between two stations A and B. While going from A to B, its average speed is $80 \text{ km/hr}$, and when coming back from B to A, its average speed is $120 \text{ km/hr}$. Find the average speed of the train during the whole journey.

#### Solution:

Let the distance between A and B be $x \text{ km}$.
Average speed while going from A to B $= 80 \text{ km/hr}$.
Time taken to cover $x \text{ km}$ from A to B $= \left(\frac{x}{80}\right)$ hours.

Average speed while coming from B to A $= 120 \text{ km/hr}$.
Time taken to cover $x \text{ km}$ from B to A $= \left(\frac{x}{120}\right)$ hours.

Total time taken to cover $2x \text{ km} = \left(\frac{x}{80} + \frac{x}{120}\right)$ hours $= \left(\frac{3x+2x}{240}\right)$ hours $= \left(\frac{5x}{240}\right)$ hours $= \left(\frac{x}{48}\right)$ hours.

Average speed during the whole journey:

$$
= \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}} = \frac{2x}{(x / 48)} \text{ km/hr} = (2x \times \frac{48}{x}) \text{ km/hr} = 96 \text{ km/hr}
$$

Hence, the average speed of the train during the whole journey is **96 km/hr**.

## Properties of Arithmetic Mean

### Theorem 1
If $\bar{x}$ is the arithmetic mean of $n$ observations $x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}, \ldots, x_{n}$, then the sum of the deviations of the observations from the mean is zero.

$$
(x_{1} - \bar{x}) + (x_{2} - \bar{x}) + (x_{3} - \bar{x}) + \ldots + (x_{n} - \bar{x}) = 0
$$

#### Proof:
We know that:

$$
\bar{x} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{n}) = n\bar{x} \quad \tag{i}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad & (x_{1} - \bar{x}) + (x_{2} - \bar{x}) + (x_{3} - \bar{x}) + \ldots + (x_{n} - \bar{x}) \\
& = (x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{n}) - n\bar{x} \\
& = (n\bar{x} - n\bar{x}) = 0 \quad [\text{using (i)}]
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i} - \bar{x}) = 0$.

---

### Theorem 2
The mean of $n$ observations $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ is $\bar{x}$. If each observation is **increased by** $p$, the mean of the new observations is $(\bar{x} + p)$.

#### Proof:
$$
\bar{x} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n}) = n\bar{x} \quad \tag{i}
$$

Mean of $(x_{1} + p), (x_{2} + p), \ldots, (x_{n} + p)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{(x_{1} + p) + (x_{2} + p) + \ldots + (x_{n} + p)}{n} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n}) + np}{n} \\
& = \frac{n\bar{x} + np}{n} \quad [\text{using (i)}] \\
& = \frac{n(\bar{x} + p)}{n} = (\bar{x} + p)
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the mean of the new observations is $(\bar{x} + p)$.

### Theorem 4
The mean of $n$ observations $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ is $\bar{x}$. If each observation is **multiplied by** a nonzero number $p$, the mean of the new observations is $p\bar{x}$.

#### Proof:
$$
\bar{x} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n}) = n\bar{x} \quad \tag{i}
$$

Mean of $px_{1}, px_{2}, \ldots, px_{n}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{px_{1} + px_{2} + \ldots + px_{n}}{n} = \frac{p(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} = \frac{p(n\bar{x})}{n} \quad [\text{using (i)}] \\
& = p\bar{x}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the mean of the new observations is $p\bar{x}$.

---

### Theorem 5
The mean of $n$ observations $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{n}$ is $\bar{x}$. If each observation is **divided by** a nonzero number $p$, the mean of the new observations is $\left(\frac{\bar{x}}{p}\right)$.

#### Proof:
$$
\bar{x} = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{n} \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n}) = n\bar{x} \quad \tag{i}
$$

Mean of $\left(\frac{x_{1}}{p}\right), \left(\frac{x_{2}}{p}\right), \ldots, \left(\frac{x_{n}}{p}\right)$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{1}{n} \cdot \left(\frac{x_{1}}{p} + \frac{x_{2}}{p} + \ldots + \frac{x_{n}}{p}\right) = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{n})}{np} = \frac{n\bar{x}}{np} \quad [\text{using (i)}] \\
& = \left(\frac{\bar{x}}{p}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

## Some More Examples

### Example: 16

The mean of $8$ numbers is $25$. If $5$ is subtracted from each number, what will be the new mean?

#### Solution:

Let the given numbers be $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots, x_{8}$.
Then, the mean of these numbers $= \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{8})}{8}$.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{8}}{8} = 25 \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{8}) = 200 \quad \tag{i}
$$

The new numbers are $(x_{1}-5), (x_{2}-5), \ldots, (x_{8}-5)$.
Mean of the new numbers $= \frac{(x_{1}-5) + (x_{2}-5) + \ldots + (x_{8}-5)}{8}$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{8}) - 40}{8} = \frac{200 - 40}{8} \quad [\text{using (i)}] \\
& = \frac{160}{8} = 20
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the new mean is **20**.

### Example: 17

The mean of $16$ numbers is $8$. If $2$ is added to every number, what will be the new mean?

#### Solution:

Let the given numbers be $x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}, \ldots, x_{16}$.
Then, the mean of these numbers $= \frac{x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{16}}{16}$.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{16}}{16} = 8 \Rightarrow (x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{16}) = 128 \quad \tag{i}
$$

The new numbers are $(x_{1}+2), (x_{2}+2), (x_{3}+2), \ldots, (x_{16}+2)$.
Mean of the new numbers:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \frac{(x_{1}+2) + (x_{2}+2) + (x_{3}+2) + \ldots + (x_{16}+2)}{16} \\
& = \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + x_{3} + \ldots + x_{16}) + 32}{16} = \frac{(128+32)}{16} \quad [\text{using (i)}] \\
& = \frac{160}{16} = 10
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the new mean is **10**.

### Example: 18

There are $50$ numbers. Each number is subtracted from $53$ and the mean of the numbers so obtained is found to be $-3.5$. Find the mean of the given numbers.

#### Solution:

Let the given numbers be $x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}, \ldots, x_{50}$. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{(53-x_{1}) + (53-x_{2}) + \ldots + (53-x_{50})}{50} = -3.5 \\
\Rightarrow & (53 \times 50) - (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{50}) = (-3.5 \times 50) \\
\Rightarrow & (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{50}) = (53 \times 50) + (3.5 \times 50) \\
\Rightarrow & (x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{50}) = (53 + 3.5) \times 50 = (56.5 \times 50) \\
\Rightarrow & \frac{(x_{1} + x_{2} + \ldots + x_{50})}{50} = 56.5 \\
\Rightarrow & \text{mean of the given numbers} = 56.5
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the mean of the given numbers is **56.5**.
