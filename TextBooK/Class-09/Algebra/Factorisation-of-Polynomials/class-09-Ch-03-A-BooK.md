# Factorisation of Polynomials

**Factor**
Let $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ be two polynomials. We say that $q(x)$ is a factor of $p(x)$, if $q(x)$ divides $p(x)$ exactly.

**Examples**
* $(x-2)$ is a factor of $\left(x^{2}-3 x+2\right)$.
* $(x+1)$ is a factor of $\left(x^{2}-1\right)$.

**Factorisation**
To express a given polynomial as the product of polynomials, each of degree less than that of the given polynomial such that no such a factor has a factor of lower degree, is called factorisation.

**Examples**
It is easy to verify that:
* $\left(x^{2}-16\right)=(x-4)(x+4)$.
* $\left(x^{2}-3 x+2\right)=(x-1)(x-2)$.

---

## Some Expansions

We know that:
1.  $(a+b)^{2}=\left(a^{2}+2 a b+b^{2}\right)$.
2.  $(a-b)^{2}=\left(a^{2}-2 a b+b^{2}\right)$.
3.  $(a+b+c)^{2}=\left(a^{2}+b^{2}+c^{2}\right)+2(a b+b c+c a)$.
4.  $(a+b)^{3}=a^{3}+b^{3}+3 a b(a+b)$.
5.  $(a-b)^{3}=a^{3}-b^{3}-3 a b(a-b)$.

## Some Formulae for Factorisation

1.  $\left(a^{2}-b^{2}\right)=(a-b)(a+b)$.
2.  $\left(a^{3}+b^{3}\right)=(a+b)\left(a^{2}-a b+b^{2}\right)$.
3.  $\left(a^{3}-b^{3}\right)=(a-b)\left(a^{2}+a b+b^{2}\right)$.

# Methods of Factorisation

## Factorisation by Taking Out the Common Factor

**Method**
When each term of an expression has a common factor, we divide each term by this factor and take it out as a multiple, as shown below.

### Solved Examples

**Example 1**: Factorise
(i) $5 a^{2}-20 a b$
(ii) $36 a^{3} b-60 a^{2} b c$
(iii) $5 a(b+c)-7 b(b+c)$
(iv) $6(2 a+3 b)^{2}-8(2 a+3 b)$

**Solution**: We have
(i) $5 a^{2}-20 a b=5 a(a-4 b)$.
(ii) $36 a^{3} b-60 a^{2} b c=12 a^{2} b(3 a-5 c)$.
(iii) $5 a(b+c)-7 b(b+c)=(b+c)(5 a-7 b)$.
(iv) $6(2 a+3 b)^{2}-8(2 a+3 b)=2(2 a+3 b)[3(2 a+3 b)-4] =2(2 a+3 b)(6 a+9 b-4)$.

---

**Example 2**: Factorise $x(x-y)^{3}+3 x^{2} y(x-y)$.

**Solution**:
$x(x-y)^{3}+3 x^{2} y(x-y)=x(x-y)\left[(x-y)^{2}+3 x y\right]$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& =x(x-y)\left(x^{2}+y^{2}-2 x y+3 x y\right) \\
& =x(x-y)\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+x y\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

## Factorisation by Grouping

**Method**
Sometimes in a given expression it is not possible to take out a common factor directly. However, the terms of the given expression are grouped in such a manner that we may have a common factor. This can now be factorised as discussed above.

### Solved Examples

**Example 3**: Factorise
(i) $a b+b c+a x+c x$
(ii) $x^{2}+3 x+x+3$
(iii) $6 a b-b^{2}+12 a c-2 b c$
(iv) $a^{2}+b-a b-a$

**Solution**: We have
(i) $a b+b c+a x+c x=(a b+b c)+(a x+c x)$

$$
=b(a+c)+x(a+c)=(a+c)(b+x)
$$

(ii) $x^{2}+3 x+x+3=\left(x^{2}+3 x\right)+(x+3)$

$$
=x(x+3)+(x+3)=(x+3)(x+1)
$$

(iii) $6 a b-b^{2}+12 a c-2 b c=(6 a b+12 a c)-\left(b^{2}+2 b c\right)$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& =6 a(b+2 c)-b(b+2 c) \\
& =(b+2 c)(6 a-b)
\end{aligned}
$$

(iv) $a^{2}+b-a b-a=a^{2}-a-a b+b=\left(a^{2}-a\right)-(a b-b)$

$$
=a(a-1)-b(a-1)=(a-1)(a-b) .
$$

---

**Example 4**: Factorise $x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}+2-2 x-\frac{2}{x}$.

**Solution**:
$x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}+2-2 x-\frac{2}{x}=\left(x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}+2\right)-2\left(x+\frac{1}{x}\right)$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& =\left(x+\frac{1}{x}\right)^{2}-2\left(x+\frac{1}{x}\right) \\
& =\left(x+\frac{1}{x}\right)\left(x+\frac{1}{x}-2\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$

---

**Example 5**: Factorise $1+x+y-z+x y-y z-z x-x y z$.

**Solution**: We have

$$
\begin{aligned}
& 1+x+y-z+x y-y z-z x-x y z \\
= & (1+x)+(y+x y)-z-z x-y z-x y z \\
= & (1+x)+y(1+x)-z(1+x)-y z(1+x) \\
= & (1+x)(1+y-z-y z) \\
= & (1+x) \times[(1+y)-z(1+y)] \\
= & (1+x)(1+y)(1-z) .
\end{aligned}
$$

---

**Example 6**: Factorise $(2 x-3)^{2}-8 x+12$.

**Solution**: We have

$$
\begin{aligned}
(2 x-3)^{2}-8 x+12 & =(2 x-3)^{2}-4(2 x-3) \\
& =(2 x-3) \times[(2 x-3)-4] \\
& =(2 x-3)(2 x-7)
\end{aligned}
$$
