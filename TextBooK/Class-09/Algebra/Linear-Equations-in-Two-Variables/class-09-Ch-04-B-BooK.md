# Graph of a Linear Equation in Two Variables

## Method of Drawing a Graph of $\boldsymbol{a} \boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{b} \boldsymbol{y}+\boldsymbol{c}=\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{a} \neq \mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{b} \neq \mathbf{0}$

1.  Express $y$ in terms of $x$.
2.  Choose some convenient values of $x$ and find the corresponding values of $y$ satisfying the given equation.
3.  Write down these values of $x$ and $y$ in the form of a table.
4.  Plot the ordered pairs $(x, y)$ from the table on a graph paper.
5.  Join these points by a straight line and extend it in both the directions.

This line is the required graph of the equation $a x+b y+c=0$.

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Draw the graph of the equation $2 x-y+3=0$. Using the graph, find the value of $y$ when (a) $x=2$, (b) $x=-3$.

**Solution:**

We have

$$
\begin{equation*}
2 x-y+3=0 \Rightarrow y=2 x+3 \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

* Putting $x=0$ in (i), we get $y=(2 \times 0+3)=3$.
* Putting $x=1$ in (i), we get $y=(2 \times 1+3)=5$.
* Putting $x=-1$ in (i), we get $y=[2 \times(-1)+3]=1$.
* Putting $x=-2$ in (i), we get $y=[2 \times(-2)+3]=-1$.

Thus, we have the following table:

| $x$ | 0 | 1 | -1 | -2 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $y$ | 3 | 5 | 1 | -1 |

On a graph paper, draw lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ representing the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively.
On this graph paper, plot the points $A(0,3), B(1,5), C(-1,1)$ and $D(-2,-1)$. Join $A B, A C$ and $C D$ to get a straight line $B A C D$. Produce it in both ways to get the required graph.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-001.jpg)

**(a)** On the $x$-axis, we take a point $P$ for which $x=2$, i.e., $O P=2$. From $P$, draw $P E \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line $B A C D$ produced at $E$. Then, $E(2,7)$ shows that

$$
(x=2 \Rightarrow y=7) \text {, i.e., when } x=2 \text {, then } y=7 \text {. }
$$

**(b)** On the $x$-axis, we take a point $Q$ for which $x=-3$, i.e., $O Q=3$ units.
From $Q$, draw $Q F \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line $B A C D$ produced at $F$. Then, $F(-3,-3)$ shows that

$$
(x=-3 \Rightarrow y=-3) \text {, i.e., when } x=-3 \text {, then } y=-3 \text {. }
$$

### Example 2

Draw the graph of the equation $2 x+3 y=11$. From your graph, find the value of $y$ when (a) $x=7$, (b) $x=-8$.

**Solution:**

We have

$$
\begin{equation*}
2 x+3 y=11 \Rightarrow y=\frac{(11-2 x)}{3} \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

* Putting $x=1$, we get $y=\frac{(11-2 \times 1)}{3}=\frac{9}{3}=3$.
* Putting $x=4$, we get $y=\frac{(11-2 \times 4)}{3}=\frac{3}{3}=1$.
* Putting $x=-2$, we get $y=\frac{\{11-2 \times(-2)\}}{3}=\frac{15}{3}=5$.
* Putting $x=-5$, we get $y=\frac{\{11-2 \times(-5)\}}{3}=\frac{21}{3}=7$.

Thus, we have the following table:

| $x$ | 1 | 4 | -2 | -5 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $y$ | 3 | 1 | 5 | 7 |

On a graph paper, draw lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively.
On this graph paper, plot the points $A(1,3), B(4,1), C(-2,5)$ and $D(-5,7)$. Join $A B, A C$ and $C D$ to get the straight line $B A C D$. Produce it in both ways to get the required graph.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-002.jpg)

**(a)** On the $x$-axis, we take a point $P$ for which $x=7$, i.e., $O P=7$.
From $P$, draw $P E \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting $D C A B$ produced at $E(7,-1)$.
$\therefore \quad(x=7 \Rightarrow y=-1)$. Thus, when $x=7$, then $y=-1$.

**(b)** On the $x$-axis, we take a point $Q$ on LHS of the $y$-axis, such that $O Q=x=-8$.
From $Q$, draw $Q F \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting $B A C D$ produced above at $F(-8,9)$.
$\therefore \quad(x=-8 \Rightarrow y=9)$. Thus, when $x=-8$, then $y=9$.

### Example 3

Draw the graph of the equation $2 x+3 y=6$. From the graph, find the value of $y$, when
(i) $x=\frac{3}{2}$
(ii) $x=-3$.

**Solution:**

We have

$$
\begin{equation*}
2 x+3 y=6 \Rightarrow 3 y=6-2 x \Rightarrow y=\frac{6-2 x}{3} \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

* Putting $x=0$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{6-(2 \times 0)}{3}=\frac{6-0}{3}=\frac{6}{3}=2$.
* Putting $y=0$ in (i), we get $(6-2 x)=3 \times 0=0 \Rightarrow 2 x=6 \Rightarrow x=3$.
* Putting $x=6$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{(6-2 \times 6)}{3}=\frac{(6-12)}{3}=\frac{-6}{3}=-2$.

Thus, we have the following table:

| $x$ | 0 | 3 | 6 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $y$ | 2 | 0 | -2 |

On a graph paper, draw lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively.

Plot the points $A(0,2), B(3,0)$ and $C(6,-2)$ on this graph paper. Join $A B$ and $B C$ to get the line $A B C$. Extend it in both the directions to get the required graph.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-003.jpg)

**(a)** On the $x$-axis, take a point $P$ such that $O P=x=\frac{3}{2}$.
Draw $P D \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line at $D\left(\frac{3}{2}, 1\right)$.
Thus, $\left(x=\frac{3}{2} \Rightarrow y=1\right)$. Thus, when $x=\frac{3}{2}$, then $y=1$.

**(b)** On the $x$-axis, take a point $Q$ such that $O Q=x=-3$.
Draw $Q E \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line at $E(-3,4)$.
Thus, $(x=-3 \Rightarrow y=4)$, i.e., when $x=-3$, then $y=4$.

### Example 4

A taxi charges ₹ 20 for the first kilometre and @ ₹ 12 per km for subsequent distance covered. Taking the total distance covered as $x \mathrm{~km}$ and total fare $₹ y$, write a linear equation depicting the relation between $x$ and $y$. Draw the graph between $x$ and $y$.
From your graph, find the taxi charges for covering (a) 12 km and (b) 20 km.

**Solution:**

The required linear equation is given by

$$
\begin{equation*}
y=20+12(x-1) \Rightarrow y=8+12 x \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

* Putting $x=1$ in (i), we get $y=(8+12 \times 1)=20$.
* Putting $x=6$ in (i), we get $y=(8+12 \times 6)=80$.
* Putting $x=10$ in (i), we get $y=(8+12 \times 10)=128$.
* Putting $x=15$ in (i), we get $y=(8+12 \times 15)=188$.

Thus, we have the following table.

| $x$ | 1 | 6 | 10 | 15 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $y$ | 20 | 80 | 128 | 188 |

On a graph paper, draw lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively. Choose the scale.

Along the $x$-axis: 1 small division $=1 \mathrm{~km}$.
Along the $y$-axis: 5 small divisions $=₹ 40$.
Now, the plot the points $A(1,20), B(6,80), C(10,128)$ and $D(15,188)$. Join $A B, B C$ and $C D$ to get the single graph line $A D$, as shown below.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-004.jpg)

**(a)** On the $x$-axis, take a point $P$ such that $O P=12 \mathrm{~km}$.
Draw $P M \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line at $M(12,152)$. Thus, $(x=12 \Rightarrow y=152)$, i.e., when distance covered is 12 km then the taxi-fare is $₹ 152$.

**(b)** On the $x$-axis, take a point $Q$ such that $O Q=20 \mathrm{~km}$. Draw $Q N \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line at $N(20,248)$. Thus, $(x=20 \Rightarrow y=248)$, i.e., when distance covered is 20 km then the taxi-fare is ₹ 248.

### Example 5

There are two scales of measuring the temperature, namely degree Fahrenheit ( ${ }^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ ) and degree Celsius ( ${ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ). The relation between the two scales is given by, $F=\frac{9}{5} C+32$.

(i) If the temperature is $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, what is the temperature in Fahrenheit?
(ii) If the temperature is $50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$, what is the temperature in Fahrenheit?
(iii) If the temperature is $86^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$, what is the temperature in Celsius?
(iv) If the temperature is $0^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$, what is the temperature in Celsius?
(v) Find the numerical value of the temperature which is the same in both the scales.
(vi) Draw the graph of the linear equation $F=\frac{9}{5} C+32$, taking $C$ along the $x$-axis and $F$ along the $y$-axis.
(vii) Using this graph, fill in the blanks given below: $-5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}=(\ldots \ldots)^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ and $14^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=(\ldots \ldots)^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

**Solution:**

The given relation is $F=\frac{9}{5} C+32$. (A)

**(i)** Putting $C=0$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
& F=\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 0\right)+32=(0+32)=32 . \\
\therefore \quad & C=0 \Rightarrow F=32 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $0^{\circ} \mathrm{C}=32^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.

**(ii)** Putting $C=50$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
F & =\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 50\right)+32=(90+32)=122 . \\
\therefore \quad C & =50 \Rightarrow F=122 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}=122^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.

**(iii)** Putting $F=86$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{9}{5} C+32=86 & \Rightarrow \frac{9}{5} C=(86-32)=54 \\
& \Rightarrow C=\left(54 \times \frac{5}{9}\right)=30 . \\
\therefore \quad F=86 \Rightarrow C= & 30 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $86^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=30^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

**(iv)** Putting $F=0$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{9}{5} C+32=0 & \Rightarrow \frac{9}{5} C=-32 \\
& \Rightarrow C=\left(-32 \times \frac{5}{9}\right)=\frac{-160}{9}=-17.8 . \\
\therefore \quad F=0 \Rightarrow C= & -17.8 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $0^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=-17.8^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

**(v)** Let $C=F$. Then, (A) becomes

$$
\begin{aligned}
F=\frac{9}{5} F+32 & \Rightarrow\left(\frac{9}{5}-1\right) F=-32 \\
& \Rightarrow \frac{4}{5} F=-32 \Rightarrow F=\left(-32 \times \frac{5}{4}\right)=-40 . \\
\therefore \quad C=F=-40 . &
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $-40^{\circ} \mathrm{C}=-40^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.

**(vi)** We have, $F=\frac{9}{5} C+32$.

Putting $C=0$ in (A), we get

$$
F=\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 0\right)+32=(0+32)=32
$$

Putting $C=10$ in (A), we get

$$
F=\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 10\right)+32=(18+32)=50 .
$$

Putting C $=20$ in (A), we get

$$
F=\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 20\right)+32=(36+32)=68
$$

Thus, we have the following table.

| $C$ | 0 | 10 | 20 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $F$ | 32 | 50 | 68 |

On a graph paper, we take $C$ along the $x$-axis and the corresponding values of $F$ along the $y$-axis.
We plot the points $A(0,32), B(10,50)$ and $C(20,68)$ on this graph paper.
Now, we join the points $A, B$, and $B, C$ to get a line $A B C$, which we extend in both the directions to get the required graph of the given equation, as shown below.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-005.jpg)

**(vii)** On the $x$-axis, we take a point $P$ at which $C=-5$.

From $P$, draw $P D \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the line $A B C$ at a point $D(-5,23)$.
$\therefore \quad-5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}=23^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.
On the $y$-axis, take a point $Q$ at which $F=14$.
From $Q$, draw $Q E \| x$-axis, meeting the line $A B C D$ at the point $E(-10,14)$.
Hence, $14^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=-10^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.

### Example 6

There are two scales of measuring the temperature of a liquid, namely Kelvin (K) and Fahrenheit ( ${ }^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ ). The relation between the two scales is given by $F=\frac{9}{5}(K-273)+32$.

(i) Find the temperature of the liquid in Fahrenheit, when it is 313 K in Kelvin.
(ii) If the temperature of the liquid is $158^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ then express it in Kelvin.
(iii) Draw the graph of the linear equation, $F=\frac{9}{5}(K-273)+32$.
(iv) Using the graph, fill in the blanks given below:

$$
\begin{equation*}
95^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=(\ldots \ldots) \mathrm{K} \text { and } 300 \mathrm{~K}=(\ldots \ldots)^{\circ} \mathrm{F} . \tag{A}
\end{equation*}
$$

**Solution:**

The given relation is, $F=\frac{9}{5}(K-273)+32$.

**(i)** Putting $K=313$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
\quad F & =\frac{9}{5} \times(313-273)+32 \\
\Rightarrow \quad F & =\left(\frac{9}{5} \times 40\right)+32=(72+32)=104 . \\
\therefore \quad K & =313 \Rightarrow F=104 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $313 \mathrm{~K}=104^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.

**(ii)** Putting $F=158$ in (A), we get

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{9}{5}(K-273)+32=158 \\
\Rightarrow & \frac{9}{5}(K-273)=(158-32)=126 \\
\Rightarrow & (K-273)=\left(126 \times \frac{5}{9}\right)=70 \\
\Rightarrow & K=(70+273)=343 \\
\therefore & F=158 \Rightarrow K=343
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $158^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=343 \mathrm{~K}$.

**(iii)** We have, $F=\frac{9}{5}(K-273)+32$.

* Putting $K=273$ in (A), we get $F=\frac{9}{5}(273-273)+32=(0+32)=32$.
* Putting $K=283$ in (A), we get $F=\frac{9}{5}(283-273)+32=(18+32)=50$.
* Putting $K=293$ in (A), we get $F=\frac{9}{5}(293-273)+32=(36+32)=68$.
* Putting $K=298$ in (A), we get $F=\frac{9}{5}(298-273)+32=(45+32)=77$.

Thus, we have the following table:

| $K$ | 273 | 283 | 293 | 298 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $F$ | 32 | 50 | 68 | 77 |

On a graph paper, we draw $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively. We take the values of $K$ along the $x$-axis and those of $F$ along the $y$-axis.
As we start the values of $K$ from 270 , we make a kink ( $\triangle$ ) at the origin and start with 270.
On the above graph, we plot the points $A(273,32)$, $B(283,50), C(293,68)$ and $D(298,77)$. Join $A B, B C$ and $C D$ to get the graph line $A B C D$, which is extended in both the directions, as shown below.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-006.jpg)

**(iv)** Along the $y$-axis, take a point $P$, showing $95^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$. Draw $P Q \| X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the graph line $A B C D$ at $Q$. Draw
$Q R \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the $x$-axis at $R$. Clearly, $R$ represents 308 K .

Hence, $95^{\circ} \mathrm{F}=308 \mathrm{~K}$.
Further, let $G$ be a point representing 300 K .
Draw $G H \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting $A B C D$ at the point $H(300,80)$.
Hence, $300 \mathrm{~K}=80^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$.

### Example 7

If the work done by a force applied on a body is directly proportional to the distance travelled by the body, express this in the form of an equation in two variables, taking the constant force as 3 units. Also, read from the graph the work done when the distance travelled by the body is (i) 0 unit, (ii) 2 units.

**Solution:**

Let $W$ be the work done by a force on a body and let $x$ be the distance travelled by the body. Then,
$W \propto x$
$\Rightarrow \quad W=F x$, where $F$ is the constant force applied on the body
$\Rightarrow \quad W=3 x \quad[\because F=3$ units (given) $]$.

Thus, the required equation in two variables is $W=3 x \ldots$ (A)

* Putting $x=1$ in (A), we get $W=(3 \times 1)=3$.
* Putting $x=3$ in (A), we get $W=(3 \times 3)=9$.
* Putting $x=4$ in (A), we get $W=(3 \times 4)=12$.
* Putting $x=5$ in (A), we get $W=(3 \times 5)=15$.

Thus, we have the following table.

| $x$ | 1 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $W$ | 3 | 9 | 12 | 15 |

On a graph paper draw mutually perpendicular lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively.
Along the $x$-axis, take 10 small divisions $=1$ unit and along the $y$-axis, take 5 small divisions $=1$ unit.

On this graph paper, plot the points $A(1,3), B(3,9), C(4,12)$ and $D(5,15)$. Join $A B, B C$ and $C D$ to obtain the graph line $A B C D$, shown below.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-007.jpg)

Produce the graph line $A B C D$ in both the directions.

**(i)** Clearly, the graph line passes through the point $O(0,0)$.
$\therefore \quad x=0 \Rightarrow W=0$.
Thus, when the body travels 0 distance, the work done is 0 .

**(ii)** Take a point $G$ on the $x$-axis such that $x=2$.
Draw $G H \perp x$-axis, cutting $A B C D$ at $H(2,6)$.
$\therefore \quad x=2 \Rightarrow W=6$.
Thus, when the body travels 2 units, the work done is 6 units.

### Example 8

The force exerted to pull a cart is directly proportional to the acceleration produced in the body. Express the statement as a linear equation of two variables and draw the graph of the same by taking the constant mass equal to 6 kg . Read from the graph the force required when the acceleration produced is (i) $5 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}$, (ii) $6 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}^{2}$.

**Solution:**

When a force $F$ is applied on a body, let an acceleration $a$ be produced. Then,

$$
\begin{array}{ll} 
& F \propto a \Rightarrow F=m a, \text { where } m \text { is the mass of the body } \\
\therefore \quad F=6 a & \ldots \text { (i) }[\because m=6 \mathrm{~kg} \text { (given) }] .
\end{array}
$$

On a graph paper, let us draw two mutually perpendicular lines $X^{\prime} O X$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ as the $x$-axis and the $y$-axis respectively. On this graph paper, we take the values of $a$ along the $x$-axis and the corresponding values of $F$ along the $y$-axis.
Some of the values of $a$ and $F$ are given below.

| $a$ | 0 | 3 | 4 | 8 |
| :---: | :-: | :-: | :-: | :-: |
| $F$ | 0 | 18 | 24 | 48 |

On the graph paper, we plot the points $O(0,0), A(3,18), B(4,24)$ and $C(8,48)$. Join $O A, A B$ and $B C$ to get the graph line $O A B C$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Algebra/Linear-Equations-in-Two-Variables/class-09-Ch-04-B-BooK-008.jpg)

**(i)** On the graph paper, take a point $P$ along $O X$ such that $O P=a=5$. Draw $P D \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting the line $O A B C$ at D. Clearly, $D(5,30)$ cuts $O A B C$ at $D$.
So, when $a=5$, we have $F=30$.

**(ii)** On the graph paper, take a point $Q$ along $O X$ such that $O Q=a=6$. Draw $Q E \perp X^{\prime} O X$, meeting $O A B C$ at $E$.
Clearly, $E(6,36)$ cuts $O A B C$ at $E$. Hence, $a=6 \Rightarrow F=36$.
