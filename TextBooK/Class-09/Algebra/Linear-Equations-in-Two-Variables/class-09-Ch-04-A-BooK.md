## Linear Equations in Two Variables

## Introduction

In earlier classes, we have learnt that a **linear equation in one variable** $x$ is of the form $ax+b=0$, where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers and $a \neq 0$.

The value of $x$ which satisfies a given linear equation is called its **solution** or **root**.

$$
ax+b=0 \Rightarrow ax=-b \Rightarrow x=\frac{-b}{a}
$$

Thus, a given linear equation in one variable can be solved.
In this chapter, we shall introduce the notion of a linear equation in two variables.

---

## Linear Equations in Two Variables

An equation of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a, b, c$ are real numbers such that $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$, is called a **linear equation in two variables** $x$ and $y$.

*Examples:* Each of the equations
- (i) $2x+3y-12=0$
- (ii) $4x-y+5=0$
- (iii) $\sqrt{2}x-\sqrt{3}y-9=0$
is a linear equation in two variables $x$ and $y$.

---

## Illustrative Examples

### Example 1:

Write each of the following equations in the form $ax+by+c=0$ and indicate the values of $a, b, c$ in each case.
1. $3=2x+y$
2. $3x-8=5y$
3. $x=4y$
4. $\frac{x}{3}-\frac{y}{2}=5$
5. $4y-3=\sqrt{2}x$
6. $\pi x+y=6$

#### Solution:

We have:

1.  $3=2x+y \Rightarrow 2x+y-3=0$.
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=2, b=1$ and $c=-3$.

2.  $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3x-8=5y & \Rightarrow 3x=5y+8 \\
    & \Rightarrow 3x-5y-8=0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=3, b=-5$ and $c=-8$.

3.  $x=4y \Rightarrow x-4y=0$
    $$
    \Rightarrow x-4y+0=0
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=1, b=-4$ and $c=0$.

4.  $\frac{x}{3}-\frac{y}{2}=5 \Rightarrow 2x-3y=30 \Rightarrow 2x-3y-30=0$.
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=2, b=-3$ and $c=-30$.

5.  $4y-3=\sqrt{2}x \Rightarrow \sqrt{2}x-4y+3=0$.
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=\sqrt{2}, b=-4$ and $c=3$.

6.  $\pi x+y=6 \Rightarrow \pi x+y-6=0$.
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=\pi, b=1$ and $c=-6$.

### Example 2:

Write each of the following as an equation of the form $ax+by+c=0$ and write the values of $a, b, c$ in each case.
1. $x=-3$
2. $y=5$
3. $3x=2$
4. $5y=4$

#### Solution:

We have:

1.  $x=-3 \Rightarrow x+3=0$
    $$
    \Rightarrow 1 \cdot x+0 \cdot y+3=0
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=1, b=0$ and $c=3$.

2.  $y=5 \Rightarrow y-5=0$
    $$
    \Rightarrow 0 \cdot x+1 \cdot y-5=0
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=0, b=1$ and $c=-5$.

3.  $$
    \begin{aligned}
    3x=2 & \Rightarrow 3x-2=0 \\
    & \Rightarrow 3x+0 \cdot y-2=0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=3, b=0$ and $c=-2$.

4.  $$
    \begin{aligned}
    5y=4 & \Rightarrow 5y-4=0 \\
    & \Rightarrow 0 \cdot x+5y-4=0
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    This is of the form $ax+by+c=0$, where $a=0, b=5$ and $c=-4$.

## Solution of a Linear Equation

Let $ax+by+c=0$ be a given linear equation in $x$ and $y$.

Then, a pair of values, one for $x$ and one for $y$, which satisfy the given equation, is called its **solution**.

If $x=\alpha$ and $y=\beta$ satisfy the equation $ax+by+c=0$ then we say that the ordered pair $(\alpha, \beta)$ is its solution.

---

### Example 3:

Show that $x=3$ and $y=2$ is a solution of the linear equation $2x+3y=12$.

#### Solution:

The given linear equation is $2x+3y-12=0$.

Substituting $x=3$ and $y=2$ in (i), we get:
$$
\text{LHS}=(2 \times 3+3 \times 2-12)=(6+6-12)=0=\text{RHS}
$$
$\therefore x=3$ and $y=2$ is a solution of the equation $2x+3y=12$.

**Note:** We may say that $(3,2)$ is a solution of the equation $2x+3y=12$.

### Example 4:

Check which of the following are solutions of the equation $x-2y=4$ and which are not.
1. $(2,0)$
2. $(0,-2)$
3. $(4\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{2})$
4. $(4,0)$

#### Solution:

The given equation may be written as $x-2y-4=0$.

1.  Putting $x=2$ and $y=0$ in (A), we get:
    $$
    \text{LHS}=(2-2 \times 0-4)=(2-0-4)=-2 \neq 0=\text{RHS}
    $$
    $\therefore (2,0)$ is not a solution of $x-2y=4$.

2.  Putting $x=0$ and $y=-2$ in (A), we get:
    $$
    \text{LHS}=\{0-2 \times (-2)-4\}=\{0+4-4\}=0=\text{RHS}
    $$
    $\therefore (0,-2)$ is a solution of $x-2y=4$.

3.  Putting $x=4\sqrt{2}$ and $y=\sqrt{2}$ in (A), we get:
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{LHS} & =(4\sqrt{2}-2 \times \sqrt{2}-4)=(4\sqrt{2}-2\sqrt{2}-4) \\
    & =(2\sqrt{2}-4) \neq 0=\text{RHS}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    $\therefore (4\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{2})$ is not a solution of $x-2y=4$.

4.  Putting $x=4$ and $y=0$ in (A), we get:
    $$
    \text{LHS}=(4-2 \times 0-4)=(4-0-4)=0=\text{RHS}
    $$
    $\therefore (4,0)$ is a solution of $x-2y=4$.

**An important fact:** A linear equation in two variables has infinitely many solutions.

### Example 5:

Find six different solutions of the equation $x+2y=6$.

#### Solution:

We have
$$
\begin{align*}
x+2y=6 & \Rightarrow 2y=(6-x) \\
& \Rightarrow y=\frac{1}{2}(6-x) \tag{i}
\end{align*}
$$
- Putting $x=2$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}(6-2)=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 4\right)=2$.
- Putting $x=4$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}(6-4)=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 2\right)=1$.
- Putting $x=6$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}(6-6)=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 0\right)=0$.
- Putting $x=-2$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}\{6-(-2)\}=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 8\right)=4$.
- Putting $x=-4$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}\{6-(-4)\}=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 10\right)=5$.
- Putting $x=-6$ in (i), we get $y=\frac{1}{2}\{6-(-6)\}=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 12\right)=6$.

So, the required solutions are given below.

| $x$ | 2 | 4 | 6 | -2 | -4 | -6 |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| $y$ | 2 | 1 | 0 | 4 | 5 | 6 |

### Example 6:

If $x=2, y=1$ is a solution of the equation $2x+3y=k$, find the value of $k$.

#### Solution:

The given equation is $2x+3y-k=0$.

Since $x=2, y=1$ is a solution of (i), we have:
$$
2 \times 2+3 \times 1-k=0 \Rightarrow 4+3-k=0 \Rightarrow k=7
$$
Hence, $k=7$.

---

### Example 7:

If $x=2k-1$ and $y=k$ is a solution of the equation $3x-5y-7=0$, find the value of $k$.

#### Solution:

The given equation is $3x-5y-7=0$.

Since $x=2k-1$ and $y=k$ is solution of (i), we have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 3 \times (2k-1)-5k-7=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 6k-3-5k-7=0 \Rightarrow k-10=0 \Rightarrow k=10
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $k=10$.
