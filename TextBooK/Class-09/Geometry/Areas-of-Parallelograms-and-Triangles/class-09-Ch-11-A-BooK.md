## Areas of Parallelograms and Triangles

**Planar Region:** The planar region corresponding to a simple closed figure is the part of the plane enclosed within the boundary of the figure.

**Area:** The magnitude or measure of the planar region enclosed by a closed figure is called the area of that figure.

**Congruent Figures:** Two figures are said to be congruent, if they have the same shape and the same size.

If two congruent figures are cut and one of them is superposed on the other then each figure covers the other completely.

---

## Euclidean Area Axioms

1.  **Existence Postulate:** Corresponding to each polygon $P$ lying in a plane, there exists a real number $\operatorname{ar}(P) \geq 0$, called its area.
2.  **Dominance Postulate:** For regions $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ in a plane, if $R_{1} \subseteq R_{2}$, i.e., if $R_{1}$ is a part of $R_{2}$ then $\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1}\right) \leq \operatorname{ar}\left(R_{2}\right)$.
3.  **Postulate of Additivity:** For any two plane regions $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ such that $\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1} \cap R_{2}\right)=0$ i.e., if $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ are nonoverlapping regions then $\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1} \cup R_{2}\right)=\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1}\right)+\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{2}\right)$.

In the adjoining figure, if $R$ represents the total region of a polygon $ABCD$ then $\operatorname{ar}(R)$ is equal to the sum of the areas of regions $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$, i.e.,

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-01.jpg?height=274&width=318&top_left_y=1041&top_left_x=877)

$$
\operatorname{ar}(R)=\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1}\right)+\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{2}\right).
$$

4.  **Congruence Postulate:** If $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ are two congruent figures then they have equal areas.

Thus, $R_{1} \cong R_{2} \Rightarrow \operatorname{ar}\left(R_{1}\right)=\operatorname{ar}\left(R_{2}\right)$. However, the converse is not true. Two figures having equal areas need not be congruent.

### Example:

A square of side 4 cm has the same area as a rectangle $8 \mathrm{~cm} \times 2 \mathrm{~cm}$. But they are not congruent.

## Parallelograms on the Same Base and Between the Same Parallels

Parallelograms on the same base and between the same parallels are equal in area. We shall verify this result through a simple activity.

**Activity 1.** On a drawing sheet, construct a parallelogram $ABCD$. Draw a line segment $AE$ as shown in Fig. (i). On a separate sheet, construct $\triangle A^{\prime} D^{\prime} E^{\prime}$ congruent to $\triangle ADE$ using a tracing paper. Cut $\triangle A^{\prime} D^{\prime} E^{\prime}$ and place it in such a way that $A^{\prime} D^{\prime}$ coincides with side $BC$ of the parallelogram $ABCD$ as shown in Fig. (ii). We thus have two parallelograms $ABCD$ and $ABE^{\prime} E$ which are on the same base $AB$ and between the same parallels $AB$ and $DE^{\prime}$.

(i)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-03.jpg?height=176&width=289&top_left_y=539&top_left_x=877)

(ii)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-03.jpg?height=184&width=342&top_left_y=777&top_left_x=853)

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Now, } \triangle ADE \cong \triangle A^{\prime} D^{\prime} E^{\prime} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ADE)=\operatorname{ar}\left(\triangle A^{\prime} D^{\prime} E^{\prime}\right) \\
& \text { Also, } \operatorname{ar}(ABCD)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ADE)+\operatorname{ar}(\text { quad. } ABCE) \\
& =\operatorname{ar}\left(\triangle A^{\prime} D^{\prime} E^{\prime}\right)+\operatorname{ar}(\text { quad. } ABCE) \\
& =\operatorname{ar}\left(ABE^{\prime} E\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, the two parallelograms $ABCD$ and $ABE^{\prime} E$ are equal in area.

### Theorem 1

**Parallelograms on the same base and between the same parallels are equal in area.**

#### Given
Two $\| \mathrm{gms} ABCD$ and $ABEF$ on the same base $AB$ and between the same parallels $AB$ and $FC$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABEF)$.

#### Proof
In $\triangle ADF$ and $\triangle BCE$, we have:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-03.jpg?height=176&width=269&top_left_y=1478&top_left_x=928)

$AD=BC$ (opp. sides of a $\| \mathrm{gm}$)

$\angle ADF=\angle BCE$ (corresponding $\angle \mathrm{s}$)

$DF=CE\left[\begin{array}{ll}\because & DC=FE(\text { each equal to } AB) \\ \Rightarrow & DC-FC=FE-FC \Rightarrow DF=CE\end{array}\right]$

$\therefore \quad \triangle ADF \cong \triangle BCE$ (SAS-criterion).
And so, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ADF)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BCE)$ (congruent figures have equal areas).

$$
\text { Now, } \begin{aligned}
\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD) & =\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ADF)+\operatorname{ar}(\text { quad. } ABCF) \\
& =\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BCE)+\operatorname{ar}(\text { quad. } ABCF) \\
& =\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABEF)
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABEF)$.

#### Corollary
**A parallelogram and a rectangle on the same base and between the same parallels are equal in area.**

#### Hint
A rectangle is also a parallelogram.

## Base and Altitude of a Parallelogram

**Base:** Any side of a parallelogram can be called its base.

**Altitude:** The length of the line segment which is perpendicular to the base from the opposite side is called the **altitude** or **height** of the parallelogram corresponding to the given base.

In the adjoining figure
(i) $DL$ is the altitude of $\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD$, corresponding to the base $AB$.
(ii) $DM$ is the altitude of $\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD$, corresponding to the base $BC$.

**The area of a parallelogram is equal to the product of its any side and the corresponding altitude.**

### Theorem 3

**Parallelograms on equal bases and between the same parallels are equal in area.**

#### Given
Two $\|$ gms $ABCD$ and $PQRS$ with equal bases $AB$ and $PQ$ and between the same parallels, $AQ$ and $DR$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} PQRS)$.

#### Construction
Draw $AL \perp DR$ and $PM \perp DR$.

#### Proof
$AB \| DR, AL \perp DR$ and $PM \perp DR$.
$\therefore \quad AL=P M$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-04.jpg?height=187&width=396&top_left_y=1741&top_left_x=801)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Now, } \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} A B C D) & =A B \times A L \\
& =P Q \times P M \quad[\because A B=P Q \text { and } A L=P M] \\
& =\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} P Q R S) .
\end{aligned}
$$

### Theorem 4 (Converse)

**Parallelograms on the same base and having equal areas lie between the same parallels.**

#### Given
Two $\| \mathrm{gms} ABCD$ and $ABMN$ on the same base $AB$ such that

$$
\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} A B C D)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} A B M N)
$$

#### To Prove
$DCNM$ is a straight line parallel to $AB$, i.e., $CN \| AB$.

#### Construction
Draw $CP \perp AB$ and $NQ \perp AB$.

#### Proof
Since $CP \perp AB$ and $NQ \perp AB$, we have $CP \| NQ$ (lines perpendicular to the same line are parallel).
Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABMN)$
$\Rightarrow \quad A B \times C P=A B \times N Q \quad$ (area of a $\| \mathrm{gm}=$ base $\times$ height)
$\Rightarrow \quad C P=N Q$.
Now, $CP \| NQ$ and $CP=NQ \Rightarrow CPNQ$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$
$\Rightarrow \quad CN\|PQ \Rightarrow CN\| AB$.
Thus, parallelograms $ABCD$ and $ABMN$ lie between the same parallels.

### Theorem 5

**A diagonal of a parallelogram divides it into two triangles of equal area.**

#### Given
A $\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD$ in which $BD$ is a diagonal.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle CDB)$.

#### Proof
In $\triangle ABD$ and $CDB$, we have:

$AB=CD$ (opposite sides of a $\| \mathrm{gm}$)
$AD=CB$ (opposite sides of a $\| \mathrm{gm}$)
$BD=DB$ (common)
$\triangle ABD \cong \triangle CDB$ (SSS-criterion)

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-05.jpg?height=205&width=366&top_left_y=1457&top_left_x=831)

Hence, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle CDB)$.

### Theorem 6

**Triangles on the same base and between the same parallels are equal in area.**

#### Given
Two $\triangle ABC$ and $DBC$ on the same base $BC$ and between the same parallel lines $BC$ and $AD$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)$.

#### Construction
Through $B$, draw $BE \| CA$, meeting $DA$ produced in $E$. And, through $C$, draw $CF \| BD$, meeting $AD$ produced in $F$.

#### Proof
$BE \| CA$ and $EA \| BC \Rightarrow BCAE$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$.
$CF \| BD$ and $DF \| BC \Rightarrow BCFD$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$.
Clearly, the $\| \mathrm{gms} BCAE$ and $BCFD$ are on the same base $BC$ and between the same parallel lines $BC$ and $EF$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-06.jpg?height=180&width=519&top_left_y=169&top_left_x=676)

$$
\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} BCAE)=\operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} BCFD) . \tag{i}
$$

But, a diagonal of a $\| \mathrm{gm}$ divides it into two triangles of equal areas.

$$
\begin{align*}
& \therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} BCAE)  \tag{ii}\\
& \text { and } \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} BCFD) . \tag{iii}
\end{align*}
$$

Thus, from (i), (ii) and (iii), we get

$$
\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC).
$$

### Theorem 7

**Area of a triangle $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ base $\times$ height.**

#### Given
A $\triangle ABC$ in which $BC$ is the base and $AL$ is the corresponding height.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times AL$.

#### Construction
Through $A$ and $C$, draw $AD \| BC$ and $CD \| BA$, intersecting each other at $D$.

#### Proof
$AD \| BC$ and $CD \| BA \Rightarrow BCDA$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$.
Thus, $BCDA$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$ whose diagonal $AC$ divides it into two triangles of equal areas.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-06.jpg?height=198&width=382&top_left_y=1055&top_left_x=813)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle A B C) & =\frac{1}{2} \times \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} B C D A) \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times B C \times A L \quad[\because \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} B C D A)=B C \times A L]
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore \quad$ area of a triangle $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ base $\times$ height.

### Theorem 8

**If a triangle and a parallelogram are on the same base, and between the same parallels then the area of the triangle is equal to half the area of the parallelogram.**

#### Given
A $\triangle ABC$ and a $\| \mathrm{gm} BCDE$ on the same base $BC$ and between the same parallel lines $BC$ and $AD$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} BCDE)$.

#### Construction
Draw $AL \perp BC$, meeting $BC$ in $L$, and $DM \perp BC$, meeting $BC$ produced in $M$.

#### Proof
Since $BC \| AD$, we have $AL=DM$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-07.jpg?height=194&width=293&top_left_y=173&top_left_x=902)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle A B C) & =\frac{1}{2} \times B C \times A L=\frac{1}{2} \times B C \times D M \quad[\because A L=D M] \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times \operatorname{ar}(\| \operatorname{gm} B C D E) .
\end{aligned}
$$

### Theorem 9

**Two triangles having the same base (or equal bases) and equal areas lie between the same parallels.**

#### Given
Two triangles $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle ABD$ have the same base $AB$ and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)$.

#### To Prove
$CD \| AB$.

#### Construction
Draw $CP \perp AB$ and $DQ \perp AB$.

#### Proof
Since $CP \perp AB$ and $DQ \perp AB$, we have $CP \| DQ$ [$\because$ lines perpendicular to same line are parallel].
Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)$
$\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} \times A B \times C P=\frac{1}{2} \times A B \times D Q$
$\Rightarrow \quad C P=D Q$

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-07.jpg?height=198&width=326&top_left_y=666&top_left_x=869)

Now, $CP \| DQ$ and $CP=DQ$
$\Rightarrow \quad CPQD$ is a $\| \mathrm{gm}$
$\Rightarrow \quad CD\|PQ \Rightarrow CD\| AB$.
Thus, the two triangles lie between the same parallels.

### Theorem 10

**If two triangles have equal areas and one side of the one triangle is equal to one side of the other then their corresponding altitudes are equal.**

#### Given
Two $\triangle ABC$ and $DEF$ such that $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)$ and $BC=EF$.

#### To Prove
Altitude $AL =$ altitude $DM$.

#### Proof
$\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times AL$ [$\because \operatorname{ar}(\triangle)=\frac{1}{2} \times$ base $\times$ height]
and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)=\frac{1}{2} \times EF \times DM$.
Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)$
$\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} BC \times AL=\frac{1}{2} \times EF \times DM \Rightarrow AL=DM$ [$\because BC=EF$].
Hence, altitude $AL =$ altitude $DM$.

### Theorem 11

**Area of a trapezium $=\frac{1}{2} \times(\text { sum of the parallel sides }) \times(\text { distance between them })$.**

#### Given
A trapezium $ABCD$ in which $AB \| DC$ and $CM \perp AB$. Let $CM=h$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}($ trap. $A B C D)=\frac{1}{2} \times(A B+D C) \times h$.

#### Construction
Draw $AL \perp DC$, meeting $CD$ produced at $L$. Join $AC$.

#### Proof
$\operatorname{ar}($ trap. $A B C D)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle A B C)+\operatorname{ar}(\triangle A C D)$

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-08.jpg?height=199&width=398&top_left_y=573&top_left_x=797)

$$
\begin{aligned}
& =\left(\frac{1}{2} \times A B \times C M\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2} \times D C \times A L\right) \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times(A B+D C) \times h \quad[\because C M=A L=h]
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore \quad$ area of a trapezium $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ (sum of the parallel sides) $\times$ (distance between them).

### Theorem 12

**Area of a rhombus $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ product of the diagonals.**

#### Given
A rhombus $ABCD$ whose diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ intersect at a point $O$.

#### To Prove
$\operatorname{ar}($ rhombus $A B C D)=\frac{1}{2} \times A C \times B D$.

#### Proof
Since the diagonals of a rhombus intersect each other at right angles, we have

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-08.jpg?height=196&width=237&top_left_y=1244&top_left_x=958)

$B O \perp A C \text { and } D O \perp A C .$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\text { rhombus } A B C D) & =\operatorname{ar}(\triangle A B C)+\operatorname{ar}(\triangle A C D) \\
& =\left(\frac{1}{2} \times A C \times B O\right)+\left(\frac{1}{2} \times A C \times D O\right) \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times A C \times(B O+D O) \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times A C \times B D \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \times(\text { product of diagonals }) .
\end{aligned}
$$

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Show that the line segment joining the midpoints of a pair of opposite sides of a parallelogram divides it into two equal parallelograms.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-09.jpg?height=178&width=277&top_left_y=388&top_left_x=922)

**GIVEN:** A parallelogram $ABCD$ in which $E$ and $F$ are the midpoints of $AB$ and $DC$ respectively. $E$ and $F$ are joined.

**TO PROVE:** $\operatorname{ar}(\square AEFD) = \operatorname{ar}(\square EBCF)$.

**PROOF:** Since $ABCD$ is a parallelogram, we have $AB \| DC$ and $AB = DC$.

$\therefore \quad AE \| DF$ and $AE = DF$
$$
\left[\because AB = DC \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} AB = \frac{1}{2} DC \Rightarrow AE = DF\right].
$$
$\therefore \quad AEFD$ is a parallelogram.

Again, $EB \| FC$ and $EB = FC$
$$
\left[\because AB = DC \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} AB = \frac{1}{2} DC \Rightarrow EB = FC\right].
$$
$\therefore \quad EBCF$ is a parallelogram.

Now, $AEFD$ and $EBCF$ being two parallelograms with equal bases and between the same parallels, they must have equal areas.

$\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} AEFD) = \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} EBCF)$.

### Example 2

In the adjoining figure, $ABCD$ is a parallelogram whose diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ intersect at $O$. A line segment through $O$ meets $AB$ at $P$ and $DC$ at $Q$. Prove that
$$
\operatorname{ar}(\square APQD) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD).
$$
#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-09.jpg?height=192&width=307&top_left_y=1087&top_left_x=892)

Diagonal $AC$ of parallelogram $ABCD$ divides it into two triangles of equal area.
$$
\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ACD) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \mathrm{gm} ABCD). \tag{i}
$$
In $\triangle OAP$ and $\triangle OCQ$, we have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& OA = OC \quad (\text{diagonals of a } \| \mathrm{gm} \text{ bisect each other}) \\
& \angle AOP = \angle COQ \quad (\text{vert. opp. } \angle \mathrm{s}) \\
& \angle PAO = \angle QCO \quad (\text{alt. interior } \angle \mathrm{s}) \\
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore \quad \triangle OAP \cong \triangle OCQ$.

$\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle OAP) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle OCQ)$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Rightarrow \quad & \operatorname{ar}(\triangle OAP) + \operatorname{ar}(\text{quad. } AOQD) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle OCQ) + \operatorname{ar}(\text{quad. } AOQD) \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \operatorname{ar}(\text{quad. } APQD) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ACD) \\
& = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\| \operatorname{gm} ABCD) \quad [\text{using (i)}]

& = \frac{1}{2} \times BP \times AL + \frac{1}{2} \times PC \times AL = \frac{1}{2} \times AL \times (BP+PC) \\
& = \frac{1}{2} \times AL \times BC = \frac{1}{2} \times PM \times AD \quad [\because AL = PM \text{ and } BC = AD] \\
& = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle PDA).
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 30

In the adjoining figure, $ABC$ and $BDE$ are two equilateral triangles such that $D$ is the midpoint of $BC$. If $AE$ intersects $BC$ at $F$, show that:
(i) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{1}{4} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)$
(ii) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BAE)$
(iii) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC) = 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BEC)$
(iv) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BFE) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFD)$
(v) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BFE) = 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED)$
(vi) $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) = \frac{1}{8} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFC)$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-23.jpg?height=322&width=245&top_left_y=1175&top_left_x=950)

Join $AD$ and $EC$. We have $\angle ABC = \angle DBE = 60^{\circ} \Rightarrow DE \| AB$ (alt. interior $\angle$s) and $\angle ACB = \angle DBE = 60^{\circ} \Rightarrow BE \| AC$ [$\because \triangle ABC$ and $\triangle BDE$ are equilateral triangles].

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_c695d6ccf21fb709bd20g-23.jpg?height=289&width=229&top_left_y=1604&top_left_x=968)

Now, let each side of $\triangle BDE = a$. Then, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} a^{2}$. (i)

Each side of $\triangle ABC = 2a$ [$\because BC = 2BD$].
$\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(2a)^{2} = 4\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} a^{2}\right)$. (ii)

From (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{1}{4} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC) \tag{iii}
$$
Now, $AD$ is a median of $\triangle ABC$
$\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)$. (iv)

But, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BAE) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)$ [$\because$ same base $AB$ and same parallels $AB$ and $DE$]. (v)

From (iv) and (v), we get
$$
\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BAE) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC). \tag{vi}
$$
From (iii) and (vi), we get
$$
\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BAE) \tag{vii}
$$
Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) = \frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BEC)$ [$\because ED$ is a median of $\triangle BEC$]. (viii)

From (iii) and (viii), we get
$$
\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC) = 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BEC) \tag{ix}
$$
Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle AED) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE)$ [$\because$ same base $DE$ and same parallels $AB$ and $DE$]
$$
\Rightarrow \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AED) - \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE) - \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) \tag{x}
$$
$\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFD) = \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BFE)$.

Now, height of $\triangle AFD$ = height of $\triangle ABC = H = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}(2a) = \sqrt{3}a$
and height of $\triangle FED$ = height of $\triangle BDE = h = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}(a) = \frac{\sqrt{3}a}{2} = \frac{H}{2}$.
$\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFD) = \frac{1}{2} \times FD \times H$ and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) = \frac{1}{2} \times FD \times \frac{H}{2}$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFD) = 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) \tag{xi}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BFE) = 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) \tag{xii}
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Now, } \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFC) &= \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFD) + \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ADC) \\
&= 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) + \frac{1}{2}\{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)\} \quad [\text{using (xi)}] \\
&= 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) + \frac{1}{2}\{4 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle BDE)\} \quad [\text{using (iii)}] \\
&= 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) + 2\{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BFE) + \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED)\} \\
&= 2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) + 2\{2 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) + \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED)\} \quad [\text{using (xii)}] \\
&= 8 \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED)
\end{aligned}
$$
$\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle FED) = \frac{1}{8} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AFC)$.

---

$\therefore \quad$ area of a rhombus $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ (product of the diagonals).
