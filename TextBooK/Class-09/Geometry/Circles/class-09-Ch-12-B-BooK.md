## Results on Angles Subtended by Arcs

### Theorem 1: The angle subtended by an arc of a circle at the centre is double the angle subtended by it at any point on the remaining part of the circle.
[CBSE 2002]

**GIVEN** A circle $C(O, r)$ in which arc $AB$ subtends $\angle AOB$ at the centre and $\angle ACB$ at any point $C$ on the remaining part of the circle.

**TO PROVE** $\angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB$, when $\widehat{AB}$ is a minor arc or a semicircle.
Reflex $\angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB$, when $\widehat{AB}$ is a major arc.

**CONSTRUCTION** Join $AB$ and $CO$. Produce $CO$ to a point $D$ outside the circle.

(i)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-001.jpg)

(ii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-002.jpg)

(iii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-003.jpg)

**PROOF** Clearly, there are three cases.

- **Case I** $\widehat{AB}$ is a minor arc [Fig. (i)].
- **Case II** $\widehat{AB}$ is a semicircle [Fig. (ii)].
- **Case III** $\widehat{AB}$ is a major arc [Fig. (iii)].

We know that when one side of a triangle is produced then the exterior angle so formed is equal to the sum of the interior opposite angles.
$\therefore \angle AOD = \angle OAC + \angle OCA$ and $\angle BOD = \angle OBC + \angle OCB$.

But, $\angle OAC = \angle OCA$ $[\because OC = OA = r]$ and $\angle OBC = \angle OCB$ $[\because OC = OB = r]$.
$\therefore \angle AOD = 2 \angle OCA$ and $\angle BOD = 2 \angle OCB$.

In Fig. (i) and Fig. (ii),
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle AOD + \angle BOD = 2 \angle OCA + 2 \angle OCB \\
\Rightarrow & \angle AOB = 2(\angle OCA + \angle OCB) \\
\Rightarrow & \angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB .
\end{aligned}
$$
In Fig. (iii),
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
& \angle AOD + \angle BOD = 2 \angle OCA + 2 \angle OCB \\
\Rightarrow & \text{reflex } \angle AOB = 2(\angle OCA + \angle OCB) \\
\Rightarrow & \text{reflex } \angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB .
\end{array}
$$

### Theorem 2: The angle in a semicircle is a right angle.

**GIVEN** $AB$ is a diameter of a circle $C(O, r)$ and $\angle ACB$ is an angle in a semicircle.

**TO PROVE** $\angle ACB = 90^{\circ}$.

**PROOF** We know that the angle subtended by an arc at the centre of a circle is twice the angle formed by it at any point on the remaining part of the circle.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-004.jpg)

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & \angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB \\
& \text{[angle subtended by } \widehat{AB} \text{ at } O = 2 \times \text{ angle formed by it at } C \text{]} \\
\Rightarrow & 2 \angle ACB = \angle AOB = 180^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle AOB \text{ is a straight angle}] \\
\Rightarrow & \angle ACB = 90^{\circ} .
\end{array}
$$

### Theorem 3: (Converse of Theorem 2) The arc of a circle subtending a right angle at any point of the circle in its alternate segment is a semicircle.

**GIVEN** An arc $AB$ of a circle with centre $O$, which subtends $\angle ACB$ at a point $C$ of $\widehat{BA}$, other than $A$ and $B$ such that $\angle ACB = 90^{\circ}$.

**TO PROVE** $\widehat{AB}$ is a semicircle.

**CONSTRUCTION** Join $OA$ and $OB$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-005.jpg)

**PROOF** Since $\widehat{AB}$ subtends $\angle AOB$ at the centre and $\angle ACB$ at the remaining part of the circle, we have
$$
\angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB = (2 \times 90^{\circ}) = 180^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle ACB = 90^{\circ} \text{ (given)}].
$$
This shows that $AO$ and $OB$ are in the same straight line.
Hence, $\widehat{AB}$ is a semicircle.

### Theorem 4: Angles in the same segment of a circle are equal.

**GIVEN** A circle $C(O, r)$ in which $\angle ACB$ and $\angle ADB$ are two angles made by $\widehat{AB}$ in the same segment $ACDB$ of the circle.

**TO PROVE** $\angle ACB = \angle ADB$.

**CONSTRUCTION** Join $OA$ and $OB$.

(i)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-006.jpg)

(ii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-007.jpg)

(iii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-008.jpg)

**PROOF** We know that angle made by an arc at the centre of a circle $= 2 \times$ (angle made by the arc at any point on its remaining part). ... (i)

- **Case I** When $\widehat{AB}$ is a minor arc [Fig. (i)]
Using (i), we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB \quad \text{and} \quad \angle AOB = 2 \angle ADB \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2 \angle ACB = 2 \angle ADB \quad \text{[each equal to } \angle AOB] \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle ACB = \angle ADB .
\end{aligned}
$$
- **Case II** When $\widehat{AB}$ is a semicircle [Fig. (ii)]
We know that the angle in a semicircle is 1 right angle.
$\therefore \angle ACB = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle ADB = 90^{\circ}$.
$\therefore \angle ACB = \angle ADB$.

- **Case III** When $\widehat{AB}$ is a major arc [Fig. (iii)]
Using (i), we have
reflex $\angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB$ and reflex $\angle AOB = 2 \angle ADB$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Rightarrow \quad & 2 \angle ACB = 2 \angle ADB \quad \text{[each equal to reflex } \angle AOB] \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle ACB = \angle ADB .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, in all the cases we have $\angle ACB = \angle ADB$.

### Theorem 5: If a line segment joining two points subtends equal angles at two other points lying on the same side of the line segment then the four points are concyclic, i.e., lie on the same circle.

**GIVEN** $AB$ is a line segment and $C, D$ are two points lying on the same side of $AB$ such that $\angle ACB = \angle ADB$.

**TO PROVE** $A, B, C, D$ lie on the same circle.

**CONSTRUCTION** Draw the circle through three noncollinear points $A, B$ and $C$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-009.jpg)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-010.jpg)

**PROOF** If $D$ lies on the circle passing through $A, B$ and $C$ then clearly the result follows.

If possible, suppose $D$ does not lie on this circle.
Then, this circle will intersect $AD$ or $AD$ produced in $D'$.
Join $D'B$.
Now, $\angle ACB = \angle ADB$ [given]
and $\angle ACB = \angle AD'B$ [angles in the same segment].
$\therefore \angle ADB = \angle AD'B$.
But, an exterior angle of a triangle can never be equal to its interior opposite angle.
So, $\angle ADB = \angle AD'B$ is true only when $D'$ coincides with $D$.
Thus, $D$ lies on the circle passing through $A, B$ and $C$.
Hence, the points $A, B, C, D$ are concyclic.

If $O$ is the centre of a circle, find the value of $x$ in each of the following figures:

(i)

(ii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-012.jpg)

(iii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-013.jpg)

(iv)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-014.jpg)

(v)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-015.jpg)

(vi)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-016.jpg)

#### Solution:
(i) $\angle BDC = \angle BAC = 30^{\circ}$ [$\angle s$ in the same segment]. In $\triangle BCD$, the sum of the angles is $180^{\circ}$.
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & \angle CBD + \angle BCD + \angle BDC = 180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & 70^{\circ} + x^{\circ} + 30^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & x = 80 .
\end{array}
$$
(ii) $\angle APB = 90^{\circ}$ [angle in a semicircle].
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \quad \angle BAP + \angle APB + \angle ABP = 180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle BAP + 90^{\circ} + 35^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle BAP = (180^{\circ} - 125^{\circ}) = 55^{\circ}
\end{aligned}
$$
Now, $x^{\circ} = \angle BQP = \angle BAP = 55^{\circ}$ [$\angle s$ in the same segment].
Hence, $x = 55$.

(iii) $OA = OB \Rightarrow \angle OAB = \angle OBA = 25^{\circ}$.
$OC = OA \Rightarrow \angle OAC = \angle OCA = 35^{\circ}$.
$\therefore \angle BAC = \angle OAB + \angle OAC = (25^{\circ} + 35^{\circ}) = 60^{\circ}$.
Now, $x^{\circ} = \angle BOC = 2 \angle BAC = (2 \times 60^{\circ}) = 120^{\circ}$.
$\therefore x = 120$.

(iv) We know that the sum of all angles at a point is $360^{\circ}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore & 90^{\circ} + 120^{\circ} + \angle BOC = 360^{\circ} \Rightarrow \angle BOC = 150^{\circ} . \\
& \angle BOC = 2 \angle BAC \Rightarrow 2x = 150 \Rightarrow x = 75 .
\end{aligned}
$$
(v) $\angle ODB = \angle OAC = 50^{\circ}$ [angles in the same segment]
$$
\begin{aligned}
OB = OD & \Rightarrow \angle OBD = \angle ODB = 50^{\circ} = x^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow x = 50 .
\end{aligned}
$$
(vi) Reflex $\angle AOC = (360^{\circ} - 130^{\circ}) = 230^{\circ}$.
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & \angle ABC = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{reflex } \angle AOC = (\frac{1}{2} \times 230^{\circ}) = 115^{\circ} = x^{\circ} . \\
\therefore & x = 115 .
\end{array}
$$

### Example 2
If $O$ is the centre of the circle, find the value of $x$ in each of the following figures:

(i)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-017.jpg)

(ii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-018.jpg)

(iii)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-019.jpg)

(iv)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-020.jpg)

(v)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-021.jpg)

(vi)
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-022.jpg)

#### Solution:
(i) In $\triangle ADC$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle CAD + \angle ADC + \angle ACD = 180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & 50^{\circ} + 90^{\circ} + \angle ACD = 180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & \angle ACD = 40^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$
But, $\angle ACB = 90^{\circ}$ [angle in a semicircle].
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad & \angle ACD + x^{\circ} = 90^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 40^{\circ} + x^{\circ} = 90^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x = 50 .
\end{aligned}
$$
(ii) Since $AOB$ is a straight line, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle AOC + \angle BOC = 180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad 130^{\circ} + \angle BOC = 180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle BOC = 180^{\circ} - 130^{\circ} = 50^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$
Now, $\angle BDC = \frac{1}{2} \angle BOC = 25^{\circ} \Rightarrow x = 25$.

(iii) $\angle BDC = \angle BAC = 40^{\circ}$ [$\angle s$ in the same segment].
$\angle BDC + \angle DBC + \angle BCD = 180^{\circ}$
$\therefore 40^{\circ} + 80^{\circ} + x^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \Rightarrow x = 60$.

(iv) $OA = OC \Rightarrow \angle OCA = \angle OAC = 50^{\circ}$.
Side $CO$ of $\triangle OCA$ is produced to $B$.
$\therefore \text{ext. } \angle AOB = \angle OAC + \angle OCA = (50^{\circ} + 50^{\circ}) = 100^{\circ}$.
Now, $\angle BDA = \frac{1}{2} \angle AOB = (\frac{1}{2} \times 100^{\circ}) = 50^{\circ}$.
Hence, $x = 50$.

(v) In $\triangle OBA$, we have $OA = OB$.
$\therefore \angle OBA = \angle OAB = 50^{\circ} \Rightarrow \angle CBA = 50^{\circ}$.
Now, $\angle CDA = x^{\circ} = \angle CBA = 50^{\circ}$ [$\angle s$ in the same segment].
$\therefore x = 50$.

(vi) $OA = OB$ [each equal to the radius of the circle]
$\Rightarrow \angle OBA = \angle OAB = x^{\circ}$
$\therefore x^{\circ} + x^{\circ} + 40^{\circ} = 180^{\circ} \Rightarrow 2x^{\circ} = 140^{\circ} \Rightarrow x = 70$.

### Example 4
In the given figure, $O$ is the centre of a circle. Prove that $\angle x + \angle y = \angle z$.

#### Solution:
In $\triangle ACF$, side $CF$ is produced to $B$.
$$
\angle y = \angle 1 + \angle 3 \quad \text{[ext. } \angle = \text{ sum of int. opp. } \angle s]. \tag{i}
$$
In $\triangle AED$, side $ED$ is produced to $B$.
$$
\angle 1 + \angle x = \angle 4 . \tag{ii}
$$
From (i) and (ii), we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
\angle 1 + \angle x + \angle y &= \angle 1 + \angle 3 + \angle 4 \\
\Rightarrow \quad \angle x + \angle y &= \angle 3 + \angle 4 \\
& = 2 \angle 3 \quad [\because \angle 4 = \angle 3, \text{ angles in the same segment}] \\
& = \angle z \quad [\because \angle AOB = 2 \angle ACB] .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\angle x + \angle y = \angle z$.

### Example 5
In the given figure, $O$ is the centre of the circle. Prove that $\angle XO_Z = 2(\angle XZY + \angle YXZ)$.

#### Solution:
Join $OY$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-025.jpg)

Now, arc $XY$ subtends $\angle XOY$ at the centre and $\angle XZY$ at a point $Z$ on the remaining part of the circle.
$$
\therefore \angle XOY = 2 \angle XZY . \tag{i}
$$
Similarly, arc $YZ$ subtends $\angle YOZ$ at the centre and $\angle YXZ$ at a point $X$ on the remaining part of the circle.
$$
\therefore \angle YOZ = 2 \angle YXZ . \tag{ii}
$$
Adding (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle XOY + \angle YOZ = 2(\angle XZY + \angle YXZ) \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle XOZ = 2(\angle XZY + \angle YXZ) .
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 6
If $O$ is the circumcentre of a $\triangle ABC$ and $OD \perp BC$, prove that $\angle BOD = \angle A$.

#### Solution:
**GIVEN** A $\triangle ABC$ whose circumcentre is $O$ and $OD \perp BC$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Circles/class-09-Ch-12-B-BooK-026.jpg)

**TO PROVE** $\angle BOD = \angle A$.

**CONSTRUCTION** Join $OB$ and $OC$.

**PROOF** In the right $\triangle OBD$ and $OCD$, we have
$$
\begin{array}{lll}
& OB = OC \quad \text{[radii of the same circle]} \\
& OD = OD \quad \text{[common]} \\
\therefore \quad & \triangle OBD \cong \triangle OCD \quad \text{[by RHS-congruence]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle BOD = \angle COD \quad \text{[c.p.c.t.]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle BOD = \frac{1}{2} \angle BOC \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle BOD = \angle A \quad [\because \angle A = \frac{1}{2} \angle BOC] .
\end{array}
$$
