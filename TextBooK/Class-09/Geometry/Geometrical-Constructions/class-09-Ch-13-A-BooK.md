## Perpendicular Bisector of a Line Segment

Let $AB$ be a given line segment and let $O$ be the midpoint of $AB$. Then, a line segment $CD$ passing through the point $O$ and perpendicular to $AB$, is called the **perpendicular bisector** of $AB$.

Clearly, $OA=OB$ and $\angle AOC=\angle BOC=90^{\circ}$.

## To Draw the Perpendicular Bisector of a Given Line Segment

### Example: 1

Draw a line segment 6.4 cm long and draw its perpendicular bisector. Measure the length of each part.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a ling segment $AB=6.4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. With $A$ as centre and a radius equal to more than half of $AB$, draw two arcs, one above $AB$ and the other below $AB$.
3. With $B$ as centre and the same radius, draw two arcs, cutting the previously drawn arcs at points $C$ and $D$ respectively.
4. Join $CD$, intersecting $AB$ at a point $O$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-01.jpg?height=325&width=471&top_left_y=1137&top_left_x=724)

Then, $CD$ is the required perpendicular bisector of $AB$ at the point $O$.

On measuring, we find that
$OA=3.2 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $OB=3.2 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Also, $\angle AOC=\angle BOC=90^{\circ}$.

#### Justification

Join $AC, AD, BC$ and $BD$.

In $\triangle CAD$ and $\triangle CBD$, we have
$$
\begin{array}{lll} 
& AC=BC & \text{(arcs of equal radii)} \\
& AD=BD & \text{(arcs of equal radii)} \\
& CD=CD & \text{(common)} \\
\therefore & \triangle CAD \cong \triangle CBD & \text{(SSS-criteria)} \\
\therefore & \angle ACO=\angle BCO & \text{(c.p.c.t.).}
\end{array}
$$
Now, in $\triangle AOC$ and $\triangle BOC$, we have
$$
\begin{array}{lll} 
& AC=BC & \text{(arcs of equal radii)} \\
& \angle ACO=\angle BCO & \text{(proved above)} \\
& CO=CO & \text{(common)} \\
\therefore & \triangle AOC \cong \triangle BOC & \text{(SAS-criteria).}
\end{array}
$$
Hence, $AO=BO$ and $\angle AOC=\angle BOC$.
But, $\angle AOC+\angle BOC=180^{\circ}$ (linear pair axiom)
$\therefore \quad \angle AOC=\angle BOC=90^{\circ}$.
Hence, $COD$ is the perpendicular bisector of $\angle AOB$.

## Bisector of a Given Angle

Let $\angle AOB$ be a given angle. Then, a ray $OC$ which divides $\angle AOB$ into two angles of equal measures, is called a **bisector** of $\angle AOB$.

In the given figure, $\angle AOC=\angle BOC$.
$\therefore OC$ is the bisector of $\angle AOB$.

### Example: 2

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-02.jpg?height=299&width=326&top_left_y=938&top_left_x=869)

Draw an angle of $110^{\circ}$ with the help of a protractor and bisect it. Measure each of these angles. Justify your answer.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw $\angle AOB=110^{\circ}$ with the help of a protractor.
2. With $O$ as centre and a convenient radius, draw an arc cutting $OA$ at $P$ and $OB$ at $Q$.
3. With $P$ as centre and a convenient radius, draw an arc.
4. With $Q$ as centre and with the same radius, draw another arc, cutting the previous arc at a point $C$.
5. Join $OC$ and produce it beyond $C$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-02.jpg?height=321&width=426&top_left_y=1417&top_left_x=769)

Then, $OC$ is the required bisector of $\angle AOB$.

#### Verification

On measuring $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$, you will find that
$\angle AOC=55^{\circ}$ and $\angle BOC=55^{\circ}$.
Thus, $\angle AOC=\angle BOC=55^{\circ}$ and therefore, $OC$ is the bisector of $\angle AOB$.

#### Justification

Join $CP$ and $CQ$.
In $\triangle OPC$ and $\triangle OQC$, we have
$OP=OQ$ (radii of the same arc)
$PC=QC$ (arcs of equal radii)
$OC=OC$ (common)
$\therefore \quad \triangle OPC \cong \triangle OQC$.
Hence, $\angle POC=\angle QOC$ (c.p.c.t.).
Consequently, $OC$ is the bisector of $\angle AOB$.

## Construction of Angles of $60^{\circ}, 30^{\circ}, 15^{\circ}, 120^{\circ}, 90^{\circ}, 45^{\circ}$ Using Ruler and Compasses

### Example: 3

Construct an angle of $60^{\circ}$ using ruler and compasses only. Justify your construction.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a ray $OA$.
2. With $O$ as centre and a convenient radius, draw an arc, cutting $OA$ at $P$.
3. With $P$ as centre and the same radius as above, draw an arc, cutting the previous arc at $Q$.
4. Join $OQ$ and produce it to a point $B$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-03.jpg?height=310&width=330&top_left_y=1167&top_left_x=865)

Then, $\angle AOB$ is the required angle of measure $60^{\circ}$.

#### Verification

If you measure $\angle AOB$ by using a protractor, you will find that $\angle AOB=60^{\circ}$.

#### Justification

Join $PQ$, as shown in the figure.
Then, $OQ=OP=PQ$ (by construction)
$\therefore \triangle OPQ$ is an equilateral triangle.
Hence, $\angle POQ=60^{\circ}$ and therefore, $\angle AOB=60^{\circ}$.

## Construction of Triangle, Rhombus, etc.

### Some Facts About a Triangle

1. In any triangle, the angle opposite to the larger side is always greater than the angle opposite to the smaller side.
2. In any triangle, the side opposite to the larger angle is always greater than the side opposite to the smaller angle.
3. The sum of any two sides of a triangle is always greater than the third side.
4. The difference of any two sides of a triangle is always less than the third side.
5. The sum of all the three angles of a triangle is always $180^{\circ}$.
6. In a right-angled triangle, the sum of the two acute angles is always $90^{\circ}$.
7. In a right-angled triangle, the hypotenuse is the largest side.

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1

In each of the following cases, give reason to show that the construction of $\triangle ABC$ is not possible.

- **(i)** $AB=6 \mathrm{~cm}, \angle A=45^{\circ}$ and $(BC+AC)=5.8 \mathrm{~cm}$.
- **(ii)** $AB=5 \mathrm{~cm}, BC=4 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $AC=9 \mathrm{~cm}$.
- **(iii)** $AB=5.4 \mathrm{~cm}, \angle B=60^{\circ}$ and $(BC-AC)=6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
- **(iv)** $BC=4 \mathrm{~cm}, \angle A=80^{\circ}, \angle B=50^{\circ}$ and $\angle C=60^{\circ}$.

#### Solution:

1. Here $(BC+AC)=5.8 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $AB=6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore (BC+AC)<AB$.
Thus, the sum of two sides is not greater than the third side. Hence, the construction of $\triangle ABC$ is not possible.

2. Here, $(AB+BC)=(5+4) \mathrm{cm}=9 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $AC=9 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore (AB+BC)=AC$.
Thus, the sum of two sides is not greater than the third side. Hence, the construction of $\triangle ABC$ is not possible.

3. Here, $(BC-AC)=6 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $AB=5.4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore (BC-AC)>AB$.
Thus, the difference of two sides is not less than the third side. Hence, the construction of $\triangle ABC$ is not possible.

4. Here, $\angle A+\angle B+\angle C=(80^{\circ}+50^{\circ}+60^{\circ})=190^{\circ}$.
But, we know that the sum of the angles of a triangle is always $180^{\circ}$.
Hence, the construction of $\triangle ABC$ is not possible in this case.

## Construction of Triangles

### Example: 2

Construct a triangle whose sides are $3.6 \mathrm{~cm}, 3 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $4.8 \mathrm{~cm}$. Bisect the smallest angle and measure each part.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw $AB=3.6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. With $A$ as centre and $3 \mathrm{~cm}$ as radius, draw an arc.
3. With $B$ as centre and $4.8 \mathrm{~cm}$ as radius, draw another arc, cutting the previous arc at $A$.
4. Join $AC$ and $BC$.

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

5. Clearly, the angle opposite to the smallest side is smallest. So, $\angle B$ is the smallest angle.
6. So, we draw $BD$, the bisector of $\angle B$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-07.jpg?height=365&width=438&top_left_y=171&top_left_x=761)

On measuring, we find that $\angle ABD=\angle CBD=20^{\circ}$.

### Example: 3

Construct an equilateral triangle whose altitude is $4 \mathrm{~cm}$. Give justification for your construction.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a line $XY$.
2. Mark any point $D$ on $XY$.
3. From $D$, draw $DE \perp XY$.
4. From $D$, set off $DA=4 \mathrm{~cm}$, cutting $DE$ at $A$.
5. Construct $\angle DAB=30^{\circ}$ and $\angle DAC=30^{\circ}$, meeting $XY$ at $B$ and $C$ respectively.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-07.jpg?height=393&width=549&top_left_y=745&top_left_x=646)

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required equilateral triangle.

#### Verification

On measuring, we find that
$$
\begin{gathered}
\angle A=\angle B=\angle C=60^{\circ} \\
\text{and } AB=BC=CA=4.5 \mathrm{~cm} .
\end{gathered}
$$

#### Justification

In $\triangle DAB$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
\angle ABD+\angle BDA+\angle DAB=180^{\circ} & \Rightarrow \angle ABD+90^{\circ}+30^{\circ}=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \angle ABD=180^{\circ}-120^{\circ}=60^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$
In $\triangle DAC$, we have
$$
\begin{aligned}
\angle ACD+\angle CDA+\angle DAC=180^{\circ} & \Rightarrow \angle ACD+90^{\circ}+30^{\circ}=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \angle ACD=180^{\circ}-120^{\circ}=60^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$
In $\triangle ABC$, we have $\angle A=\angle B=\angle C=60^{\circ}$.
Hence, $\triangle ABC$ is an equilateral triangle.

### To Construct a Triangle Given its Base, a Base Angle, and the Sum of Other Two Sides

### Example: 4

Construct a $\triangle ABC$ in which $BC=6 \mathrm{~cm}, \angle B=60^{\circ}$ and $(AB+AC)=9 \mathrm{~cm}$. Measure $AB$ and $AC$. Justify your answer.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw $BC=6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. Construct $\angle CBX=60^{\circ}$.
3. Along $BX$, set off $BP=9 \mathrm{~cm}$.
4. Join $CP$.
5. Draw the perpendicular bisector of $CP$ to intersect $BP$ at $A$.
6. Join $AC$.

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

#### Verification

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-08.jpg?height=429&width=390&top_left_y=445&top_left_x=809)

On measuring, we find that $AB+AC=(4+5) \mathrm{cm}=9 \mathrm{~cm}$.

#### Justification

Clearly, $A$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $CP$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore \quad AP=AC \\
& \text{Now, } BP=9 \mathrm{~cm} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad AB+AP=9 \mathrm{~cm} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad AB+AC=9 \mathrm{~cm} \quad[\because AP=AC]
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

### To Construct a Triangle Given its Base, a Base Angle, and the Difference of the Other Two Sides

### Example: 6

Construct a $\triangle ABC$ in which $BC=5.6 \mathrm{~cm}, \angle B=30^{\circ}$ and $(AB-AC)=3 \mathrm{~cm}$. Measure $AB$ and $AC$. Justify your construction.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw $BC=5.6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. Construct $\angle CBX=30^{\circ}$.
3. Along $BX$, set off $BD=3 \mathrm{~cm}$.
4. Join $CD$.
5. Draw the right bisector of $CD$, meeting $BD$ produced at $A$.
6. Join $AC$.

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

#### Verification

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-09.jpg?height=289&width=402&top_left_y=845&top_left_x=789)

On measuring, we find that $AB=6.1 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $AC=3.1 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore (AB-AC)=(6.1-3.1) \mathrm{cm}=3 \mathrm{~cm}$.

#### Justification

Since $A$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $CD$, we have $AD=AC$.
Now, $BD=3 \mathrm{~cm} \Rightarrow AB-AD=3 \mathrm{~cm}$
$\Rightarrow AB-AC=3 \mathrm{~cm} \quad[\because AD=AC]$.
Hence, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

### Example: 10

Construct a $\triangle ABC$ whose perimeter is $12 \mathrm{~cm}$ and the base angles are $60^{\circ}$ and $70^{\circ}$. Justify the construction.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a line segment $PQ=12 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. Make $\angle QPR=60^{\circ}$ and $\angle PQS=70^{\circ}$.
3. Draw the bisectors of $\angle QPR$ and $\angle PQS$ to meet at $A$.
4. Draw the perpendicular bisectors of $PA$ and $QA$ to meet $PQ$ at $B$ and $C$ respectively.
5. Join $AB$ and $AC$.

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

#### Justification

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-11.jpg?height=326&width=817&top_left_y=1602&top_left_x=249)

Since $B$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $AP$, we have $BA=BP$.
Since $C$ lies on the perpendicular bisector of $AQ$, we have $CA=CQ$.
Thus, $AB=PB$ and $AC=CQ$.
$\therefore AB+BC+AC=PB+BC+CQ=PQ=12 \mathrm{~cm}$.

Now, $BA=BP \Rightarrow \angle BPA=\angle BAP$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Rightarrow \angle ABC & =\angle BPA+\angle BAP=2 \angle BPA \\
& =2 \times \frac{1}{2} \times 60^{\circ}=60^{\circ}
\end{aligned}
$$
And, $CA=CQ \Rightarrow \angle CQA=\angle CAQ$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\Rightarrow \angle ACB & =\angle CQA+\angle CAQ=2 \angle CQA \\
& =2 \times \frac{1}{2} \times 70^{\circ}=70^{\circ}
\end{aligned}
$$

#### Verification

On measurement, we find that
$AB+BC+CA=(4.4+3.5+4.1) \mathrm{cm}=12 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\angle B=60^{\circ}$ and $\angle C=70^{\circ}$.

### Example: 11

Construct a $\triangle ABC$ whose perimeter is $13.5 \mathrm{~cm}$ and its sides are in the ratio $2:3:4$.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a line segment $XY=13.5 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. Draw a ray $XZ$, making an acute angle with $XY$ and drawn in the downward direction.
3. From $X$, set off $(2+3+4)=9$ equal distances along $XZ$.
4. Mark points $L, M, N$ on $XZ$ such that $XL=2$ units, $LM=3$ units and $MN=4$ units.
5. Join $NY$.
6. Draw $LB \| NY$ and $MC \| NY$, cutting $XY$ at $B$ and $C$ respectively.
7. With $B$ as centre and radius $BX$, draw an arc.
8. With $C$ as centre and radius $CY$ draw another arc, cutting the previous arc at $A$.
9. Join $AB$ and $AC$.

Then, $\triangle ABC$ is the required triangle.

#### Verification

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_bc1add08c778f160f60eg-12.jpg?height=505&width=730&top_left_y=1441&top_left_x=281)

On measuring, we find that
$AB=3 \mathrm{~cm}, BC=4.5 \mathrm{~cm}$ and $CA=6 \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore AB:BC:CA=3:\frac{9}{2}:6=6:9:12=2:3:4$.

### Rhombus

We know that a rhombus is a quadrilateral in which:

1. all sides are equal.
2. adjacent angles are supplementary.
3. opposite angles are equal.
4. the diagonals bisect each other at right angles.

### Example: 12

Construct a rhombus each of whose sides is of length $3.4 \mathrm{~cm}$ and one of its angles is $45^{\circ}$.

#### Steps of Construction

1. Draw a line segment $AB=3.4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
2. Construct $\angle BAX=45^{\circ}$.
3. Along $AX$ set off $AD=3.4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
4. Construct $\angle ABY=135^{\circ}$.
5. Along $BY$ set off $BC=3.4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
6. Join $CD$.

Then, $ABCD$ is the required rhombus.
