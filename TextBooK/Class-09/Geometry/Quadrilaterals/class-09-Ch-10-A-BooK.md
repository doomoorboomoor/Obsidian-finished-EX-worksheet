## Quadrilaterals

**QUADRILATERAL** A plane figure bounded by four line segments $AB, BC, CD$ and $DA$ is called a **quadrilateral**, written as quad. $ABCD$ or $\square ABCD$.

In a quad. $ABCD$, we have:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-001.jpg)

- **Vertices**: The points $A, B, C, D$ are called the vertices of quad. $ABCD$.
- **Sides**: The line segments $AB, BC, CD$ and $DA$ are called the sides of quad. $ABCD$.
- **Diagonals**: The line segments $AC$ and $BD$ are called the diagonals of quad. $ABCD$.
- **Adjacent Sides**: Two sides of a quadrilateral having a common end point are called its **consecutive** or **adjacent sides**. $(AB, BC), (BC, CD), (CD, DA)$ and $(DA, AB)$ are four pairs of adjacent sides of quad. $ABCD$.
- **Opposite Sides**: Two sides of a quadrilateral having no common end point are called its **opposite sides**. $(AB, CD)$ and $(AD, BC)$ are two pairs of opposite sides of quad. $ABCD$.
- **Consecutive Angles**: Two angles of a quadrilateral having a common arm are called its **consecutive angles**. $(\angle A, \angle B), (\angle B, \angle C), (\angle C, \angle D)$ and $(\angle D, \angle A)$ are four pairs of consecutive angles of a quad. $ABCD$.
- **Opposite Angles**: Two angles of a quadrilateral having no common arm are called its **opposite angles**. $(\angle A, \angle C)$ and $(\angle B, \angle D)$ are two pairs of opposite angles of quad. $ABCD$.

## Various Types of Quadrilaterals

### 1. Parallelogram
A quadrilateral in which both pairs of opposite sides are parallel is called a **parallelogram**, written as $\| \mathrm{gm}$. In $\| \mathrm{gm} PQRS$, we have

$$
PQ\|SR, PS\|QR.
$$

### 2. Rectangle
A parallelogram one of whose angles is $90^{\circ}$, is called a **rectangle**, written as rect. $ABCD$, etc. In rect. $ABCD$, we have

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-002.jpg)

$$
AB\|DC, AD\|BC \text{ and } \angle A=90^{\circ}.
$$

### 3. Square
A parallelogram whose all sides are equal and one of whose angles is $90^{\circ}$ is called a **square**. A square is thus a rectangle having all sides equal. In square $MNPQ$, we have

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-003.jpg)

$$
MN\|QP, MQ\|NP \text{ and } MN=NP=PQ=QM
$$

and $\angle M=90^{\circ}$.

### 4. Rhombus
A parallelogram having all sides equal is called a **rhombus**. In rhombus $DEFG$, we have

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-004.jpg)

$$
DE\|GF, DG\|EF \text{ and } DE=EF=FG=GD.
$$

### 5. Trapezium
A quadrilateral having one pair of opposite sides parallel is called a **trapezium**. In trapezium $KLMN$, we have

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-005.jpg)

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-006.jpg)

$$
KL \| NM.
$$

The line segment joining the midpoints of nonparallel sides of a trapezium is called its **median**.

### 6. Isosceles Trapezium
If the two nonparallel sides of a trapezium are equal then it is called an **isosceles trapezium**. In isosceles trapezium $PQST$, we have

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-007.jpg)

$$
PQ \| TS \text{ and } PT=QS
$$

### 7. Kite
A quadrilateral in which two pairs of adjacent sides are equal is known as a **kite**. Quad. $ABEF$ is a kite, in which

$$
AB=AF \text{ and } EB=EF.
$$

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-008.jpg)

From the above definitions it is clear that:
- Rectangle, square and rhombus are all parallelograms.
- A parallelogram is a trapezium while a trapezium is not a parallelogram.
- A square is both a rectangle and a rhombus.
- A kite is not a parallelogram.
- A rectangle or a rhombus is not necessarily a square.

### Theorem 1
**The sum of all the four angles of a quadrilateral is $360^{\circ}$.**

**Given**: A quad. $ABCD$.

**To Prove**: $\angle A+\angle B+\angle C+\angle D=360^{\circ}$.

**Construction**: Join BD.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-009.jpg)

**Proof**: Since the sum of the angles of a triangle is $180^{\circ}$, we have

$$
\angle A+\angle 1+\angle 2=180^{\circ} \quad \ldots \text{ (i) } \quad (\text{sum of } \angle s \text{ of } \triangle ABD)
$$

and

$$
\angle 3+\angle C+\angle 4=180^{\circ} \quad \ldots \text{ (ii) } \quad (\text{sum of } \angle s \text{ of } \triangle BCD).
$$

On adding (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\angle A+\angle C+(\angle 1+\angle 3)+(\angle 2+\angle 4)=360^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle A+\angle C+\angle B+\angle D=360^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle 1+\angle 3=\angle B \text{ and } \angle 2+\angle 4=\angle D]
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle A+\angle B+\angle C+\angle D=360^{\circ}.
$$

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Three angles of a quadrilateral measure $110^{\circ}, 82^{\circ}$ and $68^{\circ}$. Find the measure of the fourth angle.

#### Solution

Let the measure of the fourth angle be $x^{\circ}$.

Then, the sum of the angles of a quadrilateral being $360^{\circ}$, we have:
$110+82+68+x=360 \Rightarrow 260+x=360 \Rightarrow x=100$.

Hence, the measure of the fourth angle is $100^{\circ}$.

### Example 2

The angles of a quadrilateral are in the ratio $3:5:9:13$. Find all the angles of the quadrilateral.

#### Solution

Let $ABCD$ be the given quadrilateral and let

$$
\angle A=(3x)^{\circ}, \angle B=(5x)^{\circ}, \angle C=(9x)^{\circ} \text{ and } \angle D=(13x)^{\circ}.
$$

Since the sum of the angles of a quadrilateral is $360^{\circ}$, we have

$$
3x+5x+9x+13x=360 \Rightarrow 30x=360 \Rightarrow x=12.
$$

$$
\therefore \quad \angle A=36^{\circ}, \angle B=60^{\circ}, \angle C=108^{\circ} \text{ and } \angle D=156^{\circ}.
$$

### Example 3

The sides $BA$ and $DC$ of a quadrilateral are produced as shown in the given figure. Prove that $x+y=a+b$.

#### Solution

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-010.jpg)

We have $\angle A+b^{\circ}=180^{\circ}$ (linear pair)
$\Rightarrow \angle A=\left(180^{\circ}-b^{\circ}\right)$.

Also, $\angle C+a^{\circ}=180^{\circ}$ (linear pair)
$\Rightarrow \angle C=\left(180^{\circ}-a^{\circ}\right)$.

Now, $\angle A+\angle B+\angle C+\angle D=360^{\circ}$ (sum of the $\angle s$ of a quad. is $360^{\circ}$)

$$
\Rightarrow\left(180^{\circ}-b^{\circ}\right)+x^{\circ}+\left(180^{\circ}-a^{\circ}\right)+y^{\circ}=360^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad x+y=a+b.
$$

### Example 4

In a quadrilateral $ABCD$, the line segments bisecting $\angle C$ and $\angle D$ meet at $E$. Prove that $\angle A+\angle B=2 \angle CED$.

#### Solution

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-011.jpg)

Let $CE$ and $DE$ be the bisectors of $\angle C$ and $\angle D$ respectively. Then, $\angle 1=\frac{1}{2} \angle C$ and $\angle 2=\frac{1}{2} \angle D$.

In $\triangle DEC$, we have $\angle 1+\angle 2+\angle CED=180^{\circ}$ (sum of the $\angle s$ of a $\triangle$ is $180^{\circ}$)

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle CED=180^{\circ}-(\angle 1+\angle 2). \quad \ldots(i)
$$

Again, the sum of the angles of a quadrilateral is $360^{\circ}$.

$$
\therefore \quad \angle A+\angle B+\angle C+\angle D=360^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}(\angle A+\angle B)+\frac{1}{2} \angle C+\frac{1}{2} \angle D=180^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}(\angle A+\angle B)+\angle 1+\angle 2=180^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2}(\angle A+\angle B)=180^{\circ}-(\angle 1+\angle 2). \quad \ldots(ii)
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get $\frac{1}{2}(\angle A+\angle B)=\angle CED$.

Hence, $\angle A+\angle B=2 \angle CED$.

### Example 5

In the adjoining figure, a point $O$ is taken inside an equilateral quad. $ABCD$ such that $OB=OD$. Show that $A, O$ and $C$ are in the same straight line.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-012.jpg)

**Given**: A quad. $ABCD$ in which $AB=BC=CD=DA$ and $O$ is a point within it such that $OB=OD$.

**To Prove**: $\angle AOB+\angle COB=180^{\circ}$.

#### Solution

In $\triangle OAB$ and $OAD$, we have

$$
AB=AD \text{ (given), } OA=OA \text{ (common) and } OB=OD \text{ (given)}
$$

$$
\therefore \triangle OAB \cong \triangle OAD.
$$

$$
\therefore \angle AOB=\angle AOD. \quad \ldots(i)
$$

Similarly, $\triangle OBC \cong \triangle ODC$.

$$
\therefore \angle COB=\angle COD. \quad \ldots(ii)
$$

Now, $\angle AOB+\angle COB+\angle COD+\angle AOD=360^{\circ} \quad [\angle s \text{ at a point}]$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad 2(\angle AOB+\angle COB)=360^{\circ} \Rightarrow \angle AOB+\angle COB=180^{\circ}.
$$

### Example 6

In the adjoining figure, $ABCD$ is a quadrilateral in which $AB$ is the longest side and $CD$ is the shortest side. Prove that (i) $\angle C>\angle A$, (ii) $\angle D>\angle B$.

#### Solution

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-013.jpg)

Join $AC$ and $BD$.

In $\triangle ABC, AB>BC \Rightarrow \angle ACB>\angle BAC$.
In $\triangle ADC, AD>DC \Rightarrow \angle ACD>\angle CAD$.
$[\because \text{ in a triangle, the longer side has the greater angle opposite it}]$.

$$
\therefore \quad \angle ACB+\angle ACD>\angle BAC+\angle CAD.
$$

$$
\therefore \quad \angle C>\angle A.
$$

Similarly, $\angle D>\angle B$.

### Example 7

In the adjoining figure, the bisectors of $\angle B$ and $\angle D$ of a quadrilateral $ABCD$ meet $CD$ and $AB$ produced at $P$ and $Q$ respectively. Prove that

$$
\angle P+\angle Q=\frac{1}{2}(\angle B+\angle D).
$$

#### Solution

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-014.jpg)

In $\triangle ADQ$, we have
$\angle A+\angle ADQ+\angle Q=180^{\circ}$ (sum of the $\angle s$ of a $\triangle$)

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle A+\frac{1}{2} \angle D+\angle Q=180^{\circ}. \quad \ldots(i)
$$

In $\triangle CBP$, we have
$\angle C+\angle CBP+\angle P=180^{\circ}$ (sum of the $\angle s$ of a $\triangle$)

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle C+\frac{1}{2} \angle B+\angle P=180^{\circ}. \quad \ldots(ii)
$$

Adding (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\angle A+\angle C+\frac{1}{2} \angle B+\frac{1}{2} \angle D+\angle P+\angle Q=360^{\circ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \angle A+\angle C+\angle B+\angle D+\angle P+\angle Q=360^{\circ}+\frac{1}{2} \angle B+\frac{1}{2} \angle D
$$
$[\text{ adding } (\frac{1}{2} \angle B+\frac{1}{2} \angle D) \text{ on both sides}]$

$$
\Rightarrow 360^{\circ}+\angle P+\angle Q=360^{\circ}+\frac{1}{2}(\angle B+\angle D)
$$
$[\because \text{ sum of all the angles of a quadrilateral is } 360^{\circ}]$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle P+\angle Q=\frac{1}{2}(\angle B+\angle D).
$$

### Example 8

If $ABCD$ is a quadrilateral whose diagonals $AC$ and $BD$ intersect at $O$, prove that
(i) $(AB+BC+CD+DA)>(AC+BD)$,
(ii) $(AB+BC+CD+DA)<2(AC+BD)$.

#### Solution

(i) We know that the sum of any two sides of a triangle is greater than the third.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/Quadrilaterals/class-09-Ch-10-A-BooK-015.jpg)

In $\triangle ABC$, we have $AB+BC>AC$.
In $\triangle ACD$, we have $CD+DA>AC$.
In $\triangle BCD$, we have $BC+CD>BD$.
In $\triangle ABD$, we have $DA+AB>BD$.

Adding these inequalities, we get

$$
2(AB+BC+CD+DA)>2(AC+BD).
$$

$$
\therefore \quad AB+BC+CD+DA>AC+BD.
$$

(ii) In $\triangle AOB$, we have $OA+OB>AB$.
In $\triangle BOC$, we have $OB+OC>BC$.
In $\triangle COD$, we have $OC+OD>CD$.
In $\triangle DOA$, we have $OD+OA>DA$.

Adding these inequalities, we get

$$
2(OA+OC)+2(OB+OD)>AB+BC+CD+DA
$$

$$
\Rightarrow 2(AC+BD)>AB+BC+CD+DA
$$

$$
\Rightarrow AB+BC+CD+DA<2(AC+BD)
$$
