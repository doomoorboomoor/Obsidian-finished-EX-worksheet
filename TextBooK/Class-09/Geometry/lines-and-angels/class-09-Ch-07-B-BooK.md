## Some Angle Relations

**Adjacent angles** Two angles are called adjacent angles, if
1.  they have the same vertex,
2.  they have a common arm and
3.  their non-common arms are on either side of the common arm.

In the given figure, $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$ are adjacent angles having the same vertex $O$, a common arm $OC$ and their non-common arms $OA$ and $OB$ on either side of $OC$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-001.jpg)

**LINEAR PAIR OF ANGLES** Two adjacent angles are said to form a linear pair of angles, if their non-common arms are two opposite rays.

In the adjoining figure, $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$ are two adjacent angles whose non-common arms $OA$ and $OB$ are two opposite rays, i.e., $BOA$ is a line.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-002.jpg)

$\therefore \quad \angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$ form a linear pair of angles.

## Some Results on Angle Relations

**THEOREM 1** If a ray stands on a line then the sum of the adjacent angles so formed is $180^{\circ}$.

**GIVEN** A ray $CD$ stands on a line $AB$ such that $\angle ACD$ and $\angle BCD$ are formed.

**TO PROVE** $\angle ACD+\angle BCD=180^{\circ}$.

**CONSTRUCTION** Draw $CE \perp AB$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-003.jpg)

**PROOF**
$$
\begin{align*}
\angle ACD & =\angle ACE+\angle ECD  \tag{i}\\
\text { and } \angle BCD & =\angle BCE-\angle ECD . \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$
Adding (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
\angle ACD+\angle BCD & =(\angle ACE+\angle ECD)+(\angle BCE-\angle ECD) \\
& =\angle ACE+\angle BCE \\
& =\left(90^{\circ}+90^{\circ}\right)=180^{\circ}\left[\because \angle ACE=\angle BCE=90^{\circ}\right] .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\angle ACD+\angle BCD=180^{\circ}$.

**REMARK** We may state the above theorem as 'the sum of the angles of a linear pair is $180^{\circ}$'.

**COROLLARY 1** Prove that the sum of all the angles formed on the same side of a line at a given point on the line is $180^{\circ}$.

**GIVEN** $AOB$ is a straight line and rays $OC, OD$ and $OE$ stand on it, forming $\angle AOC, \angle COD, \angle DOE$ and $\angle EOB$.

**TO PROVE** $\angle AOC+\angle COD+\angle DOE+\angle EOB=180^{\circ}$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-004.jpg)

**PROOF** Ray $OC$ stands on line $AB$.
$$
\begin{array}{cc}
\therefore & \angle AOC+\angle COB=180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & \angle AOC+(\angle COD+\angle DOE+\angle EOB)=180^{\circ} \\
& {[\because \angle COB=\angle COD+\angle DOE+\angle EOB]} \\
\Rightarrow & \angle AOC+\angle COD+\angle DOE+\angle EOB=180^{\circ} .
\end{array}
$$
Hence, the sum of all the angles formed on the same side of line $AB$ at a point $O$ on it is $180^{\circ}$.

**COROLLARY 2** Prove that the sum of all angles around a point is $360^{\circ}$.

**GIVEN** A point $O$ and the rays $OA, OB, OC$ and $OD$ make angles around $O$.

**TO PROVE** $\angle AOB+\angle BOC+\angle COD+\angle AOD=360^{\circ}$.

**CONSTRUCTION** Produce ray $OB$ backwards to a point $E$ such that $EOB$ is a line.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-005.jpg)

**PROOF** Clearly, $EOB$ is a straight line and the ray $OA$ stands on it.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle AOE+\angle AOB=180^{\circ} . \tag{i}
$$
We know that the sum of all angles at a point on a given line on the same side of it, is $180^{\circ}$.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle BOC+\angle COD+\angle DOE=180^{\circ} . \tag{ii}
$$
Adding the corresponding sides of (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle AOE+\angle AOB+\angle BOC+\angle COD+\angle DOE=360^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle AOB+\angle BOC+\angle COD+(\angle AOE+\angle DOE)=360^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle AOB+\angle BOC+\angle COD+\angle AOD=360^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the sum of all the angles around a given point is $360^{\circ}$.

**THEOREM 2 (Converse of Theorem 1)** If the sum of two adjacent angles is $180^{\circ}$ then the non-common arms of the angles are in a straight line.

**GIVEN** Two adjacent angles $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$ with common arm $OC$ such that $\angle AOC+\angle BOC=180^{\circ}$.

**TO PROVE** $OA$ and $OB$ are in the same straight line, i.e., $AOB$ is a straight line.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-006.jpg)

**CONSTRUCTION** If possible, let $AOB$ be not a straight line. Then, produce $AO$ to $D$ so that $AOD$ is a straight line.

**PROOF** By our assumption, $AOD$ is a straight line and ray $OC$ stands on it.
$\therefore \quad \angle AOC+\angle COD=180^{\circ} \quad$ [linear pair].
But, $\angle AOC+\angle BOC=180^{\circ} \quad$ [given].
$\therefore \quad \angle AOC+\angle COD=\angle AOC+\angle BOC \quad$ [each equal to $180^{\circ}$].
So, $\angle COD=\angle BOC$.
But, this is not true, since a part cannot be equal to whole.
$\therefore \quad$ our supposition is wrong.
Hence, $AOB$ is a straight line.

**Vertically opposite angles** Two angles are called a pair of vertically opposite angles, if their arms form two pairs of opposite rays.

Let two lines $AB$ and $CD$ intersect at a point $O$. Then, two pairs of vertically opposite angles are formed:

1.  $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOD$

2.  $\angle AOD$ and $\angle BOC$.

**THEOREM 3** If two lines intersect then the vertically opposite angles are equal.

**GIVEN** Two lines $AB$ and $CD$ intersect at a point $O$.

**TO PROVE** (i) $\angle AOC=\angle BOD, \quad$ (ii) $\angle AOD=\angle BOC$.

**PROOF** Since ray $OA$ stands on line $CD$, we have
$$
\angle AOC+\angle AOD=180^{\circ} \text{ [linear pair]. }
$$
Again, ray $OD$ stands on line $AB$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-008.jpg)

$\therefore \quad \angle AOD+\angle BOD=180^{\circ} \quad$ [linear pair].
$\therefore \quad \angle AOC+\angle AOD=\angle AOD+\angle BOD \quad$ [each equal to $180^{\circ}$]
$\therefore \quad \angle AOC=\angle BOD$.
Similarly, $\angle AOD=\angle BOC$.

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1

In the adjoining figure, $AOB$ is a straight line. Find $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOD$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-009.jpg)

Since $AOB$ is a straight line, the sum of all the angles on the same side of $AOB$ at a point $O$ on it, is $180^{\circ}$.
$\therefore \quad x+65+(2 x-20)=180$
$\Rightarrow 3 x=135 \Rightarrow x=45$.
$\therefore \angle AOC=x^{\circ}=45^{\circ}$ and $\angle BOD=(2 \times 45-20)^{\circ}=70^{\circ}$.

In the adjoining figure, what value of $x$ will make $AOB$ a straight line?

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-010.jpg)

### Example: 3

Calculate $\angle AOC, \angle BOD$ and $\angle AOE$ in the adjoining figure, it is being given that $\angle COD=90^{\circ}, \angle BOE=72^{\circ}$ and $AOB$ is a straight line.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-011.jpg)

Since $AOB$ is a straight line, the sum of all the angles on the lower side of $AOB$ at a point $O$ on it, is $180^{\circ}$.
$\therefore \quad \angle AOE+\angle BOE=180^{\circ}$
$\Rightarrow 3 x+72=180$
$\Rightarrow 3 x=(180-72)=108 \Rightarrow x=\frac{108}{3}=36$.

Again, $AOB$ is a straight line and $O$ is a point on it. So, the sum of all angles on the upper side of $AOB$ at a point $O$ on it, is $180^{\circ}$.
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & \angle AOC+\angle COD+\angle DOB=180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & x+90+y=180 \\
& \quad\left[\because \angle AOC=x^{\circ}, \angle COD=90^{\circ} \text { and } \angle DOB=y^{\circ}\right] \\
\Rightarrow & 36+90+y=180 \quad[\because x=36] \\
\Rightarrow & 126+y=180 \Rightarrow y=(180-126)=54 . \\
\therefore & \angle AOC=x^{\circ}=36^{\circ}, \angle BOD=y^{\circ}=54^{\circ} \\
& \angle AOE=(3 x)^{\circ}=(3 \times 36)^{\circ}=108^{\circ}
\end{array}
$$

### Example: 4

In the given figure, ray $OC$ stands on a straight line $AOB$. Ray $OD$ and ray $OE$ are the bisectors of $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOC$ respectively. Find the measure of $\angle DOE$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-012.jpg)

Since $AOB$ is a straight line and the ray $OC$ stands on it.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore \angle AOC+\angle BOC=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} \angle AOC+\frac{1}{2} \angle BOC=90^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \angle COD+\angle COE=90^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \angle DOE=90^{\circ} \\
& \quad[\because \angle COD+\angle COE=\angle DOE] .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the bisectors of a linear pair are at right angles.

### Example: 5

In the given figure, lines $XY$ and $LM$ intersect at $O$. If $\angle XOL=a, \angle LOP=b, \angle POY=90^{\circ}, \angle XOM=c$ and $a: b=2: 3$, find the value of $c$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-013.jpg)

It is given that $a: b=2: 3$.
So, let $a=(2 x)^{\circ}$ and $b=(3 x)^{\circ}$.

In the given figure, ray $OP$ stands on line $XY$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore \quad \angle XOP+\angle POY=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle XOL+\angle LOP+90^{\circ}=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad a+b+90=180 \\
& \Rightarrow \quad a+b=90 \\
& \Rightarrow \quad 2 x+3 x=90 \Rightarrow 5 x=90 \Rightarrow x=18 . \\
& \therefore \quad a=(2 x)^{\circ}=(2 \times 18)^{\circ}=36^{\circ} \text { and } b=(3 x)^{\circ}=(3 \times 18)^{\circ}=54^{\circ}
\end{aligned}
$$
Again, ray $OX$ stands on line $LM$.
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & \angle LOX+\angle XOM=180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & a+c=180^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & 36^{\circ}+c=180^{\circ} \Rightarrow c=\left(180^{\circ}-36^{\circ}\right)=144^{\circ} .
\end{array}
$$
Hence, $c=144^{\circ}$.

### Example: 6

In the given figure, if $x+y=z+w$ then prove that $AOB$ is a line.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-014.jpg)

We know that the sum of all the angles around a point is $360^{\circ}$.
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\therefore & x+y+z+w=360^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & (x+y)+(z+w)=360^{\circ} \\
\Rightarrow & (x+y)+(x+y)=360^{\circ} \quad[\because z+w=x+y] \\
\Rightarrow & 2(x+y)=360^{\circ} \Rightarrow x+y=180^{\circ}
\end{array}
$$
Hence, $AOB$ is a straight line.

### Example: 7

In the given figure, if $\angle PQR=\angle PRQ$ then prove that $\angle PQS=\angle PRT$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-015.jpg)

Since ray $QP$ stands on line $ST$, we have
$$
\angle PQR+\angle PQS=180^{\circ} . \tag{i}
$$
Again, ray $RP$ stands on line $ST$.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle PRQ+\angle PRT=180^{\circ} . \tag{ii}
$$
From (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle PQR+\angle PQS=\angle PRQ+\angle PRT \\
\Rightarrow & \angle PRQ+\angle PQS=\angle PRQ+\angle PRT \quad[\because \angle PQR=\angle PRQ] \\
\Rightarrow & \angle PQS=\angle PRT .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\angle PQS=\angle PRT$.

### Example: 8

In the given figure, $\angle XYZ=56^{\circ}$ and $XY$ is produced to a point $P$. If ray $YQ$ bisects $\angle ZYP$, find $\angle XYQ$ and reflex $\angle QYP$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-016.jpg)

Since $XY$ is produced to point $P$, it follows that $XP$ is a straight line and ray $YZ$ stands on it.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore \quad \angle XYZ+\angle ZYP=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad 56^{\circ}+\angle ZYP=180^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle ZYP=\left(180^{\circ}-56^{\circ}\right)=124^{\circ} \\
& \Rightarrow \quad \angle ZYQ=\frac{1}{2} \angle ZYP=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 124^{\circ}\right)=62^{\circ} . \\
& \therefore \quad \angle XYQ=\angle XYZ+\angle ZYQ=\left(56^{\circ}+62^{\circ}\right)=118^{\circ} . \\
& \text { Also, } \angle QYP=\frac{1}{2} \angle ZYP=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 124^{\circ}\right)=62^{\circ} . \\
& \therefore \quad \text { reflex } \angle QYP=360^{\circ}-\angle QYP=(360-62)^{\circ}=298^{\circ} .
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 9

In the given figure, $AB, CD$ and $EF$ are three lines concurrent at $O$. Find the value of $y$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-017.jpg)

Lines $EOF$ and $AOB$ intersect each other at the point $O$.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle BOE=\angle AOF=5 y . \tag{i}
$$
[vertically opposite angles]

Also, the sum of all the angles formed on the upper side of $COD$ at the point $O$ is $180^{\circ}$.
$\therefore \quad \angle DOB+\angle BOE+\angle EOC=180^{\circ}$
$\Rightarrow 2 y+5 y+2 y=180^{\circ}$
[using (i)]
$\Rightarrow 9 y=180^{\circ} \Rightarrow y=20^{\circ}$.
Hence, $y=20^{\circ}$.

### Example: 10

In the given figure, $AB$ is a mirror, $PQ$ is the incident ray and $QR$, the reflected ray. If $\angle PQR=112^{\circ}$, find $\angle PQA$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-018.jpg)

When a ray falls on a mirror, it is reflected and angle of incidence $=$ angle of reflection $=x^{\circ}$ (say). $QM$ is drawn normal to $AB$ and therefore,
angle of incidence $=\angle PQM$,
angle of reflection $=\angle MQR$
and $\angle AQM=90^{\circ}$.
Now, $\angle PQM+\angle MQR=\angle PQR=112^{\circ}$ (given)
$\therefore \quad 2 \angle PQM=112^{\circ}$
$\therefore \quad \angle PQM=56^{\circ}$.
So, $\angle PQA=\angle AQM-\angle PQM=90^{\circ}-56^{\circ}=34^{\circ}$.

### Example: 11

Prove that the bisectors of a pair of vertically opposite angles are in the same straight line.

**GIVEN** Two lines $AB$ and $CD$ intersect at a point $O$. Also, $OE$ and $OF$ are the bisectors of $\angle AOC$ and $\angle BOD$ respectively.

**TO PROVE** $EOF$ is a straight line.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-019.jpg)

**PROOF** Since the sum of all the angles around a point is $360^{\circ}$, we have:
$$
\angle AOC+\angle BOC+\angle BOD+\angle AOD=360^{\circ}
$$
$$
\begin{gathered}
\Rightarrow \quad \angle AOC+2 \angle BOC+\angle BOD=360^{\circ} \\
\quad[\because \angle AOD=\angle BOC(\text { vert. opp. } \angle \text { s })] \\
\Rightarrow \quad 2 \angle EOC+2 \angle BOC+2 \angle BOF=360^{\circ}
\end{gathered}
$$
$[\because OE$ is the bisector of $\angle AOC$ and $OF$ is the bisector of $\angle BOD]$
$\Rightarrow \quad \angle EOC+\angle BOC+\angle BOF=180^{\circ}$
$\Rightarrow \angle EOF=180^{\circ}$, i.e., $\angle EOF$ is a straight angle.
Hence, $EOF$ is a straight line.

### Example: 12

In the given figure, ray $OC$ is the bisector of $\angle AOB$ and $OD$ is the ray opposite to $OC$. Show that $\angle AOD=\angle BOD$.

#### Solution:

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Geometry/lines-and-angels/class-09-Ch-07-B-BooK-020.jpg)

Since $OC$ is the bisector of $\angle AOB$, we have
$$
\angle BOC=\angle AOC . \tag{i}
$$
Since ray $OB$ stands on line $DOC$ at a point $O$, we have
$$
\angle BOC+\angle BOD=180^{\circ} . \tag{ii}
$$
Again, ray $OA$ stands on line $DOC$ at a point $O$, so we have
$$
\angle AOC+\angle AOD=180^{\circ} . \tag{iii}
$$
From (ii) and (iii), we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle AOC+\angle AOD=\angle BOC+\angle BOD \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \angle AOD=\angle BOD \quad[\because \angle AOC=\angle BOC \text { from (i) }] .
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\angle AOD=\angle BOD$.
