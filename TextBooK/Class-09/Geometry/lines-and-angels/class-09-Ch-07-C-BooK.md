## Results on Parallel Lines

**Parallel lines**: If two lines lie in the same plane and do not intersect when produced on either side, then such lines are said to be parallel to each other.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-15.jpg?height=80&width=301&top_left_y=1695&top_left_x=890)

**Transversal**: A straight line which cuts two or more straight lines at distinct points is called a transversal.

---

## The Angles Formed When a Transversal Cuts Two Lines

Let $A B$ and $C D$ be two lines, cut by a transversal $t$. Then, the following angles are formed.
1.  **Pairs of corresponding angles**:
    $$
    (\angle 1, \angle 5); (\angle 4, \angle 8); (\angle 2, \angle 6) \text{ and } (\angle 3, \angle 7).
    $$

2.  **Pairs of alternate interior angles**: $(\angle 3, \angle 5)$ and $(\angle 4, \angle 6)$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-16.jpg?height=333&width=386&top_left_y=217&top_left_x=809)

3.  **Pairs of consecutive interior angles** (also called **allied angles** or **conjoined angles**): $(\angle 4, \angle 5)$ and $(\angle 3, \angle 6)$.

**REMARKS**: We shall abbreviate as follows:
-   Corresponding angles as *corres. $\angle$s*.
-   Alternate interior angles as *alt. int. $\angle$s*.
-   Consecutive interior angles as *co. int. $\angle$s*.

---

## Corresponding Angles Axiom

If a transversal cuts two parallel lines then each pair of corresponding angles are equal.

Conversely, if a transversal cuts two lines, making a pair of corresponding angles equal, then the lines are parallel.

Thus, whenever $A B \| C D$ are cut by a transversal $t$, then $\angle 1=\angle 5; \angle 4=\angle 8; \angle 2=\angle 6$ and $\angle 3=\angle 7$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-16.jpg?height=345&width=378&top_left_y=1550&top_left_x=229)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-16.jpg?height=345&width=376&top_left_y=1550&top_left_x=692)

On the other hand, if a transversal $t$ cuts two lines $A B$ and $C D$ such that $(\angle 1=\angle 5)$ or $(\angle 4=\angle 8)$ or $(\angle 2=\angle 6)$ or $(\angle 3=\angle 7)$ then $A B \| C D$.

---

### Theorem 1
*If a transversal intersects two parallel lines then alternate angles of each pair of interior angles are equal.*

**GIVEN**: $A B \| C D$ and a transversal $t$ cuts $A B$ at $E$ and $C D$ at $F$, forming two pairs of alternate interior angles, namely $(\angle 3, \angle 5)$ and $(\angle 4, \angle 6)$.

**TO PROVE**: $\angle 3=\angle 5$ and $\angle 4=\angle 6$.

**PROOF**: We have:
$$
\angle 3=\angle 1 \quad (\text{vert. opp. } \angle s)
$$
$$
\text{and } \angle 1=\angle 5 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 3=\angle 5.
$$
Again,
$$
\angle 4=\angle 2 \quad (\text{vert. opp. } \angle s)
$$
$$
\text{and } \angle 2=\angle 6 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 4=\angle 6.
$$
Hence, $\angle 3=\angle 5$ and $\angle 4=\angle 6$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-17.jpg?height=289&width=376&top_left_y=519&top_left_x=821)

---

### Theorem 2
*If a transversal intersects two parallel lines then each pair of consecutive interior angles are supplementary.*

**GIVEN**: $A B \| C D$ and a transversal $t$ cuts $A B$ at $E$ and $C D$ at $F$, forming two pairs of consecutive interior angles, namely $(\angle 3, \angle 6)$ and $(\angle 4, \angle 5)$.

**TO PROVE**: $\angle 3+\angle 6=180^{\circ}$ and $\angle 4+\angle 5=180^{\circ}$.

**PROOF**: Since ray $E F$ stands on line $A B$, we have:
$$
\angle 3+\angle 4=180^{\circ} \quad (\text{linear pair}).
$$
But, $\angle 4=\angle 6$ (alt. int. $\angle$s)
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 3+\angle 6=180^{\circ}.
$$
Again, since ray $F E$ stands on line $C D$, we have:
$$
\angle 6+\angle 5=180^{\circ}.
$$
But, $\angle 6=\angle 4$ (alt. int. $\angle$s)
$$
\therefore \angle 4+\angle 5=180^{\circ}.
$$
Hence, $\angle 3+\angle 6=180^{\circ}$ and $\angle 4+\angle 5=180^{\circ}$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-17.jpg?height=281&width=376&top_left_y=1165&top_left_x=821)

---

### Theorem 3 (Converse of Theorem 1)
*If a transversal intersects two lines, making a pair of alternate interior angles equal, then the two lines are parallel.*

**GIVEN**: A transversal $t$ cuts two lines $A B$ and $C D$ at $E$ and $F$ respectively such that $\angle 3=\angle 5$.

**TO PROVE**: $A B \| C D$.

**PROOF**: We have:
$$
\angle 3=\angle 5 \quad (\text{given}).
$$
But,
$$
\angle 3=\angle 1 \quad (\text{vert. opp. } \angle s)
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 1=\angle 5.
$$
But, these are corresponding angles.
$$
\therefore \quad A B \| C D \quad (\text{by corres. } \angle s \text{ axiom}).
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-18.jpg?height=285&width=378&top_left_y=173&top_left_x=821)

---

### Theorem 4 (Converse of Theorem 2)
*If a transversal intersects two lines in such a way that a pair of consecutive interior angles are supplementary then the two lines are parallel.*

**GIVEN**: A transversal cuts two lines $A B$ and $C D$ at $E$ and $F$ respectively such that $\angle 4+\angle 5=180^{\circ}$.

**TO PROVE**: $A B \| C D$.

**PROOF**: Since ray $E B$ stands on line $t$, we have:
$$
\angle 1+\angle 4 = 180^{\circ} \quad (\text{linear pair})
$$
and
$$
\angle 4+\angle 5 = 180^{\circ} \quad (\text{given}).
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 1+\angle 4 = \angle 4+\angle 5.
$$
This gives, $\angle 1=\angle 5$.
But, these are corresponding angles.
$$
\therefore \quad A B \| C D \quad (\text{by corres. } \angle s \text{ axiom}).
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-18.jpg?height=287&width=380&top_left_y=767&top_left_x=815)

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1
*Prove that the two lines which are both parallel to the same line are parallel to one another.*

**GIVEN**: Three lines $l, m, n$ such that $l \| n$ and $m \| n$.

**TO PROVE**: $l \| m$.

**CONSTRUCTION**: Draw a transversal $t$, cutting $l, m, n$ at $E, F, G$ respectively.

**PROOF**: Since $l \| n$ and transversal $t$ cuts them at $E$ and $G$ respectively, so
$$
\angle 1=\angle 3 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
Again $m \| n$ and transversal $t$ cuts them at $F$ and $G$ respectively, so
$$
\angle 2=\angle 3 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-18.jpg?height=256&width=402&top_left_y=1498&top_left_x=793)
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 1=\angle 2 \quad (\text{each equal to } \angle 3).
$$
But these are corresponding angles formed when the transversal $t$ cuts $l$ and $m$ at $E$ and $F$ respectively.
$$
\therefore \quad l \| m \quad (\text{by corres. } \angle s \text{ axiom}).
$$

### Example: 2
*If a line is perpendicular to one of the two given parallel lines then prove that it is also perpendicular to the other line.*

**GIVEN**: $l, m, n$ are three lines such that $m \| n$ and $l \perp m$.

**TO PROVE**: $l \perp n$.

**PROOF**: Since $l \perp m$, we have: $\angle 1=90^{\circ}$.
Now, $m \| n$ and the transversal $l$ cuts them.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 2=\angle 1 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s)
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 2=90^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle 1=90^{\circ}].
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-19.jpg?height=255&width=320&top_left_y=269&top_left_x=875)
Hence, $l \perp n$.

### Example: 3
*If two parallel lines are intersected by a transversal then prove that the bisectors of any pair of alternate interior angles are parallel.*

**GIVEN**: $A B \| C D$ are cut by a transversal $t$ at $E$ and $F$ respectively; $E G$ and $F H$ are the bisectors of a pair of alt. int. $\angle s, \angle A E F$ and $\angle E F D$ respectively.

**TO PROVE**: $E G \| F H$.

**PROOF**: Since $A B \| C D$ and $t$ is a transversal, we have:
$$
\angle A E F=\angle E F D \quad [\text{alt. int. } \angle s]
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} \angle A E F=\frac{1}{2} \angle E F D
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle G E F=\angle E F H.
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-19.jpg?height=285&width=374&top_left_y=841&top_left_x=821)
But, these are alternate interior angles formed when the transversal $E F$ cuts $E G$ and $F H$.
$$
\therefore \quad E G \| F H.
$$

### Example: 4
*If the bisectors of a pair of corresponding angles formed by a transversal with two given lines are parallel, prove that the given lines are parallel.*

**GIVEN**: A transversal $P Q$ cuts two lines $A B$ and $C D$ at $E$ and $F$ respectively. $E G$ and $F H$ are the bisectors of a pair of corresponding angles $\angle P E B$ and $\angle E F D$ respectively such that $E G \| F H$.

**TO PROVE**: $A B \| C D$.

**PROOF**: $E G \| F H$ are cut by the transversal $E F$.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle P E G=\angle E F H
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle G E B=\angle H F D
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 2 \angle G E B=2 \angle H F D
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle P E B=\angle E F D
$$
$$
\left[\because \angle G E B=\frac{1}{2} \angle P E B \text{ and } \angle H F D=\frac{1}{2} \angle E F D\right].
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-19.jpg?height=343&width=378&top_left_y=1522&top_left_x=817)
But, these are corresponding angles when $A B$ and $C D$ are cut by the transversal $P Q$.
$$
\therefore \quad A B \| C D \quad (\text{by corres. } \angle s \text{ axiom}).
$$

### Example: 5
*If two parallel lines are intersected by a transversal, prove that the bisectors of the two pairs of interior angles enclose a rectangle.*

**GIVEN**: $A B \| C D$ and a transversal $t$ cuts them at $E$ and $F$ respectively. $E G, F G, E H$ and $F H$ are the bisectors of the interior angles $\angle A E F$, $\angle C F E, \angle B E F$ and $\angle E F D$ respectively.

**TO PROVE**: $E G F H$ is a rectangle.

**PROOF**: $A B \| C D$ and the transversal $t$ cuts them at $E$ and $F$ respectively.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle A E F=\angle E F D \quad [\text{alt. int. } \angle s]
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} \angle A E F=\frac{1}{2} \angle E F D \Rightarrow \angle G E F=\angle E F H.
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-20.jpg?height=269&width=380&top_left_y=428&top_left_x=817)
But, these are alternate interior angles formed when the transversal $E F$ cuts $E G$ and $F H$.
$$
\therefore \quad E G \| F H.
$$
Similarly, $E H \| F G$.
$$
\therefore \quad E G F H \text{ is a parallelogram.}
$$
Now, ray $E F$ stands on line $A B$.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle A E F+\angle B E F=180^{\circ} \quad (\text{linear pair})
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} \angle A E F+\frac{1}{2} \angle B E F=90^{\circ} \Rightarrow \angle G E F+\angle H E F=90^{\circ}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \angle G E H=90^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle G E F+\angle H E F=\angle G E H].
$$
Thus, $E G F H$ is a parallelogram, one of whose angles is $90^{\circ}$. Hence, $E G F H$ is a rectangle.

### Example: 6
*In the given figure, $l \| m$ and a transversal $t$ cuts them. If $\angle 1=70^{\circ}$, find the measure of each of the remaining marked angles.*

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-20.jpg?height=285&width=400&top_left_y=1401&top_left_x=795)

$$
\Rightarrow \angle 2=\left(180^{\circ}-70^{\circ}\right)=110^{\circ}.

$$
\therefore \quad \angle 4=\angle 2=110^{\circ} \quad [\text{vertically opposite } \angle s]

\text{and } \angle 3=\angle 1=70^{\circ} \quad [\text{vertically opposite } \angle s].

### Example: 8
*In the given figure, $A B \| C D$ and $\angle A O C=x^{\circ}$. If $\angle O A B=104^{\circ}$ and $\angle O C D=116^{\circ}$, find the value of $x$.*

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-21.jpg?height=249&width=311&top_left_y=1433&top_left_x=886)

Through $O$ draw $O E\|A B\| C D$.
Then, $\angle A O E+\angle C O E=x^{\circ}$.

Now, $A B \| O E$ and $A O$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle OAB+\angle A O E=180^{\circ}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 104^{\circ}+\angle A O E=180^{\circ}
$$
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-21.jpg?height=247&width=324&top_left_y=1701&top_left_x=873)
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle A O E=(180-104)^{\circ}=76^{\circ}.
$$
Again, $C D \| O E$ and $O C$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle C O E+\angle O C D=180^{\circ}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \angle C O E+116^{\circ}=180^{\circ}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \angle C O E=\left(180^{\circ}-116^{\circ}\right)=64^{\circ}.
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \angle A O C=\angle A O E+\angle C O E=\left(76^{\circ}+64^{\circ}\right)=140^{\circ}.
$$
Hence, $x=140$.

\Rightarrow \angle B O E=\left(180^{\circ}-40^{\circ}\right)=140^{\circ}.

### Example: 12
*In the given figure, $A B \| C D$, $\angle B A E=50^{\circ}, \quad \angle A E C=x^{\circ} \quad$ and $\angle E C D=100^{\circ}$. Find the value of $x$.*

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-23.jpg?height=209&width=362&top_left_y=1296&top_left_x=833)

Through $E$, draw a line $G E H\|A B\| C D$.

Now, $G E \| A B$ and $E A$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle G E A=\angle E A B=50^{\circ} \quad [\text{alt. int. } \angle s].
$$

$$
\therefore \quad \angle H E C+\angle E C D=180^{\circ} \quad [\text{co. int. } \angle s]
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \angle H E C+100^{\circ}=180^{\circ} \Rightarrow \angle H E C=80^{\circ}.
$$

\therefore \quad \angle G E A+\angle A E C+\angle H E C=180^{\circ} \quad [\text{straight angle}]

$$

### Example: 14
*Two plane mirrors, $m$ and $n$, are placed parallel to each other as shown in the figure. A ray $A B$ is incident on the first mirror. It is reflected twice and emerges in the direction $C D$. Prove that $A B \| C D$.*

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-24.jpg?height=227&width=400&top_left_y=1314&top_left_x=797)

**GIVEN**: Two mirrors $m$ and $n$ such that $m \| n$. The paths taken by the incident ray $A B$ after reflections at $m$ and $n$ are $B C$ and $C D$ respectively. Also, $B M$ and $C N$ are normals to $m$ and $n$ respectively.

**TO PROVE**: $A B \| C D$.

**PROOF**: Since $m \| n$ and $C N \perp n$, therefore, $C N \perp m$.
Now, $B M \perp m$ and $C N \perp m \Rightarrow B M \| C N$.
Now, $B M \| C N$ and $B C$ is a transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 2=\angle 3 \quad [\text{alt. int. } \angle s].
$$
But, $\angle 1=\angle 2$ and $\angle 3=\angle 4$ [by laws of reflection]
$$
\Rightarrow \angle 1+\angle 2=2(\angle 2) \text{ and } \angle 3+\angle 4=2(\angle 3)
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle 1+\angle 2=\angle 3+\angle 4 \quad [\because \angle 2=\angle 3 \Rightarrow 2(\angle 2)=2(\angle 3)]
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle A B C=\angle B C D.
$$
But, these are alternate interior angles formed when lines $A B$ and $C D$ are cut by the transversal $B C$.
$$
\therefore \quad A B \| C D.
$$

### Example: 15
*If the arms of one angle are respectively parallel to the arms of another angle, show that the two angles are either equal or supplementary.*

**GIVEN**: Two angles $\angle A B C$ and $\angle D E F$ such that $B A \| E D$ and $B C \| E F$.

**TO PROVE**: $\angle A B C=\angle D E F$ or $\angle A B C+\angle D E F=180^{\circ}$.

**PROOF**: The arms of the angles may be parallel in the same sense or in the opposite sense. So, three cases arise.
(i)

**Case I**: When both pairs of arms are parallel in same sense [Fig. (i)]:
In this case, $B A \| E D$ and $B C$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle A B C=\angle 1 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
Again, $B C \| E F$ and $D E$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle 1=\angle D E F \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
Hence, $\angle A B C=\angle D E F$.

**Case II**: When both pairs of arms are parallel in opposite sense [Fig. (ii)]:
In this case, $B A \| E D$ and $B C$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle A B C=\angle 1 \quad (\text{corres. } \angle s).
$$
Again, $F E \| B C$ and $E D$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle D E F=\angle 1 \quad (\text{alt. int. } \angle s).
$$
Hence, $\angle A B C=\angle D E F$.

**Case III**: When one pair of arms are parallel in same sense and other pair parallel in opposite sense: [Fig. (iii)]
In this case, $B A \| E D$ and $B C$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle E G B=\angle A B C \quad [\text{Alt. Int. } \angle s].

Now, $B C \| E F$ and $D E$ is the transversal.
$$
\therefore \quad \angle D E F+\angle E G B=180^{\circ} \quad [\text{Co. Int. } \angle s]
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle D E F+\angle A B C=180^{\circ} \quad [\because \angle E G B=\angle A B C].
$$
Hence, $\angle A B C$ and $\angle D E F$ are supplementary.

*Prove that the opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal.*

**GIVEN**: A parallelogram $A B C D$ in which $A B \| D C$ and $A D \| B C$.

**TO PROVE**: $\angle A=\angle C$ and $\angle B=\angle D$.

$$
\therefore \quad \angle B A D+\angle A D C=180^{\circ}. \quad \text{(i)}

$$
\therefore \quad \angle A D C+\angle D C B=180^{\circ}. \quad \text{(ii)}

$$
\angle B A D+\angle A D C=\angle A D C+\angle D C B
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \angle B A D=\angle D C B \Rightarrow \angle A=\angle C.
$$
Similarly, $\angle B=\angle D$.
Hence, $\angle A=\angle C$ and $\angle B=\angle D$.

## IMPORTANT FACTS AND FORMULAE

1. ANGLE Two rays $A B$ and $A C$ having a common end point $A$ form angle $B A C$, written as $\angle B A C$ or $\angle A$.

We measure angles in degrees, denoted by ' ${ }^{{ }^{{ }^{\prime} \text { ' }}}$.
2. (i) RIGHT ANGLE An angle whose measure is $90^{\circ}$ is called a right angle.
(ii) ACUTE ANGLE An angle whose measure is more than $0^{\circ}$ but less than $90^{\circ}$ is called an acute angle.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-42.jpg?height=223&width=318&top_left_y=1564&top_left_x=877)
(iii) OBTUSE ANGLE An angle whose measure is more than $90^{\circ}$ but less than $180^{\circ}$ is called an obtuse angle.
(iv) STRAIGHT ANGLE An angle whose measure is $180^{\circ}$ is called a straight angle.
(v) REFLEX ANGLE An angle whose measure is more than $180^{\circ}$ but less than $360^{\circ}$ is called a reflex angle.
(vi) COMPLETE ANGLE An angle whose measure is $360^{\circ}$ is called a complete angle.
3. (i) COMPLEMENTARY ANGLES Two angles are said to be complementary, if their sum is $90^{\circ}$.

Example Complement of $30^{\circ}=\left(90^{\circ}-30^{\circ}\right)=60^{\circ}$.
(ii) SUPPLEMENTARY ANGLES Two angles are said to be supplementary, if their sum is $180^{\circ}$.

Example Supplement of $30^{\circ}=\left(180^{\circ}-30^{\circ}\right)=150^{\circ}$.
4. (i) ADJACENT ANGLES Two angles are called adjacent angles, if
(a) they have the same vertex,
(b) they have a common arm, and
(c) their non-common arms are on either side of the common arm.
(ii) LINEAR PAIR OF ANGLES Two adjacent angles are said to form a linear pair, if their sum is $180^{\circ}$.
5. (i) If a ray stands on a line, then the sum of the adjacent angles so formed is $180^{\circ}$.
In the given figure, a ray $C D$ stands on a line $A B$.

$$
\angle A C D+\angle B C D=180^{\circ} .
$$

(ii) The sum of all the angles formed on the same side of a line at a given point on the line is $180^{\circ}$.
In the given figure, $A O B$ is a line.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_e97ef562f1d61fa42038g-43.jpg?height=211&width=342&top_left_y=1133&top_left_x=853)

$$
\therefore \quad \angle A O C+\angle C O D+\angle D O E+\angle E O B=180^{\circ} .
$$

(iii) The sum of all the angles around a point is $360^{\circ}$.
6. vertically opposite angles If two lines $A B$ and $C D$ intersect at a point $O$ then the vertically opposite angles are equal.
