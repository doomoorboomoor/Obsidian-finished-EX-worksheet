
# Irrational Numbers

**Irrational Number**: A number which can neither be expressed as a terminating decimal nor as a repeating decimal is called an **irrational number**.

Thus, **nonterminating, nonrepeating decimals** are irrational numbers.

---

## Examples of Irrational Numbers

### **TYPE 1**

- (i) Clearly, $0.01001000100001 \ldots$ is a nonterminating and nonrepeating decimal and therefore, it is irrational.
- (ii) Similarly, $0.02002000200002 \ldots$ is irrational, $0.03003000300003 \ldots$ is irrational, and so on.
- (iii) $0.12112111211112 \ldots$ is irrational, $0.13113111311113 \ldots$ is irrational, and so on.
- (iv) $0.54554555455554 \ldots$ is irrational, $0.64664666466664 \ldots$ is irrational, and so on.

### **TYPE 2**

If $m$ is a positive integer which is not a perfect square then $\sqrt{m}$ is irrational.
Thus $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{5}, \sqrt{6}, \sqrt{7}, \sqrt{8}, \sqrt{10}, \sqrt{11}$, etc., are all irrational numbers.

### **TYPE 3**

If $m$ is a positive integer which is not a perfect cube then $\sqrt[3]{m}$ is irrational.
Thus, $\sqrt[3]{2}, \sqrt[3]{3}, \sqrt[3]{4}, \sqrt[3]{5}, \sqrt[3]{6}, \sqrt[3]{7}, \sqrt[3]{9}$, etc., are all irrational numbers.

### **The Number $\pi$**

$\pi$ is a number whose exact value is not $\frac{22}{7}$. In fact, $\pi$ has a value which is nonterminating and nonrepeating. So, **$\pi$ is irrational**, while **$\frac{22}{7}$ is rational**.

---

## Properties of Irrational Numbers

1.  Irrational numbers satisfy the **commutative**, **associative** and **distributive laws** for addition and multiplication.
2.
    - (i) **Sum** of two irrationals need not be an irrational.
      *Example*: Each one of $(2+\sqrt{3})$ and $(4-\sqrt{3})$ is irrational. But, their sum $(2+\sqrt{3})+(4-\sqrt{3})=6$, which is rational.
    - (ii) **Difference** of two irrationals need not be an irrational.
      *Example*: Each one of $(5+\sqrt{2})$ and $(3+\sqrt{2})$ is irrational. But, their difference $(5+\sqrt{2})-(3+\sqrt{2})=2$, which is rational.
    - (iii) **Product** of two irrationals need not be an irrational.
      *Example*: $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational. But, the product $\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3}=3$, which is rational.
    - (iv) **Quotient** of two irrationals need not be an irrational.
      *Example*: Each one of $2 \sqrt{3}$ and $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational. But, the quotient $\frac{2 \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}}=2$, which is rational.
3.
    - (i) Sum of a rational and an irrational is **irrational**.
    - (ii) Difference of a rational and an irrational is **irrational**.
    - (iii) Product of a rational and an irrational is **irrational**.
    - (iv) Quotient of a rational and an irrational is **irrational**.

**Remark**: If $a$ is rational and $\sqrt{b}$ is irrational then each one of $(a+\sqrt{b}), (a-\sqrt{b}), a \sqrt{b}$ and $\frac{a}{\sqrt{b}}$ is irrational.

*Examples*: Each one of $(4+\sqrt{3}), (8-\sqrt{5}), 5 \sqrt{3}$ and $\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational.

---

## An Important Result

If $a$ and $b$ are two distinct positive rational numbers then:

1.  $\frac{a+b}{2}$ is a **rational number** lying between $a$ and $b$.
2.  $\sqrt{ab}$ is an **irrational number** lying between $a$ and $b$.

## Solved Examples

### Example 1:

Are the square roots of all positive integers irrational? If no, give two examples.

#### Solution:

The square root of every positive integer is not always irrational.

*Examples*:
- (i) $\sqrt{4}=2$, which is rational.
- (ii) $\sqrt{9}=3$, which is rational.

---

### Example 2:

Are the cube roots of all positive integers irrational? If no, give two examples.

#### Solution:

The cube root of a positive integer is not always irrational.

*Examples*:
- (i) $\sqrt[3]{8}=2$, which is rational.
- (ii) $\sqrt[3]{27}=3$, which is rational.

---

### Example 3:

Write three numbers whose decimal expansions are nonterminating and nonrepeating.

#### Solution:

We know that if $m$ is a positive integer which is not a perfect square, then $\sqrt{m}$ is irrational. We also know that the decimal expansion of an irrational number is nonterminating and nonrepeating.

So, the required numbers are $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}$ and $\sqrt{5}$.

---

### Example 4:

Insert a rational and an irrational number between 3 and 4.

#### Solution:

A rational number between 3 and 4 is $\frac{3+4}{2}=3.5$.
An irrational number between 3 and 4 is $\sqrt{3 \times 4}$, i.e., $\sqrt{12}$.

---

### Example 5:

Find two irrational numbers between $\frac{1}{7}$ and $\frac{2}{7}$.

#### Solution:

By actual division, we know that $\frac{1}{7}=0.\overline{142857}$ and $\frac{2}{7}=0.\overline{285714}$.

Now, consider the numbers:
$$
a=0.16016001600016 \ldots \quad \text{and} \quad b=0.21211211121111 \ldots
$$
Clearly, $a$ and $b$ are irrational numbers such that $\frac{1}{7}<a<b<\frac{2}{7}$.

Hence, $a$ and $b$ are the required numbers.

---

### Example 6:

Find two rational and two irrational numbers between $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$.

#### Solution:

We have:
$$
\sqrt{2}=1.41421356 \ldots \quad \text{and} \quad \sqrt{3}=1.732050807 \ldots
$$
If we consider the numbers 1.5 and 1.6, we find that both of them are rational numbers such that $\sqrt{2}<1.5<1.6<\sqrt{3}$. Hence, two rational numbers between $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$ are **1.5** and **1.6**.

Now, consider the numbers:
$$
a=1.5050050005 \ldots \quad \text{and} \quad b=1.6161161116 \ldots
$$
Clearly, $a$ and $b$ are irrational numbers such that $\sqrt{2}<a<b<\sqrt{3}$. Hence, $a$ and $b$ are two irrational numbers between $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$.

---

### Example 7:

Find two rational numbers in the form $\frac{p}{q}$ between $0.343443444344443 \ldots$ and $0.363663666366663 \ldots$.

#### Solution:

Let $a=0.343443444344443 \ldots$ and $b=0.363663666366663 \ldots$.

Consider the numbers:
$$
c=0.35=\frac{35}{100}=\frac{7}{20} \quad \text{and} \quad d=0.36=\frac{36}{100}=\frac{9}{25}
$$
Clearly, $c$ and $d$ are rational numbers such that $a<c<d<b$.

Hence, $\frac{7}{20}$ and $\frac{9}{25}$ are the required rational numbers.
