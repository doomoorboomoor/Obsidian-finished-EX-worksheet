# Decimal Representation of Rational Numbers

Every rational number $\frac{p}{q}$ can be expressed as a decimal. On dividing $p$ by $q$, two possibilities arise:

(i) The remainder becomes zero and the division concludes after a finite number of steps. In this case, the decimal expansion obtained also terminates or ends.
(ii) The remainder never becomes zero and a repeating string of remainders is obtained. In this case, we get a digit or a block of digits repeating in the decimal expansion.

Thus, we have two types of decimal expressions:

## 1. Terminating Decimal

A decimal that ends after a finite number of digits is called a terminating decimal.

**Examples:** We have (i) $\frac{1}{4}=0.25, \quad$ (ii) $\frac{5}{8}=0.625, \quad$ (iii) $2 \frac{3}{5}=\frac{13}{5}=2.6$.

**Division Demonstrations:**

$$
\begin{array}{c}
\text{(i) } \frac{1}{4} \\[1em]
\begin{array}{r}
0.25 \\
4 \overline{) 1.00} \\
\underline{-8}\phantom{0} \\
20 \\
\underline{-20} \\
0
\end{array}
\end{array}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
\text{(ii) } \frac{5}{8} \\[1em]
\begin{array}{r}
0.625 \\
8 \overline{) 5.000} \\
\underline{-48}\phantom{00} \\
20\phantom{0} \\
\underline{-16}\phantom{0} \\
40 \\
\underline{-40} \\
0
\end{array}
\end{array}
\qquad
\begin{array}{c}
\text{(iii) } \frac{13}{5} \\[1em]
\begin{array}{r}
2.6 \\
5 \overline{) 13.0} \\
\underline{-10}\phantom{.0} \\
30 \\
\underline{-30} \\
0
\end{array}
\end{array}
$$

Thus, each of the numbers $\frac{1}{4}, \frac{5}{8}$ and $2 \frac{3}{5}$ can be expressed as a terminating decimal.

**Important Rule:** A rational number $\frac{p}{q}$ is expressible as a terminating decimal only when prime factors of $q$ are 2 and 5 only.

**Examples:** Each one of the numbers $\frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{4}, \frac{7}{20}, \frac{13}{25}$ is a terminating decimal since the denominator of each has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.

---

**Example:** Without actual division, find which of the following rational numbers are terminating decimals:
(i) $\frac{5}{32}$
(ii) $\frac{11}{24}$
(iii) $\frac{27}{80}$.

**Solution:**
(i) Denominator of $\frac{5}{32}$ is 32. And, $32=2^{5}$.
$\therefore \quad 32$ has no prime factors other than 2.
So, $\frac{5}{32}$ is a terminating decimal.

(ii) Denominator of $\frac{11}{24}$ is 24.
And, $24=2^{3} \times 3$.
Thus, 24 has a prime factor 3, which is other than 2 and 5.
$\therefore \quad \frac{11}{24}$ is not a terminating decimal.

(iii) Denominator of $\frac{27}{80}$ is 80.
And, $80=2^{4} \times 5$.
Thus, 80 has no prime factors other than 2 and 5.
$\therefore \quad \frac{27}{80}$ is a terminating decimal.

## 2. Repeating (or Recurring) Decimals

A decimal in which a digit or a set of digits is repeated periodically, is called a repeating, or a recurring, decimal.

In a recurring decimal, we place a bar over the first block of the repeating digits and omit the other repeating blocks.

**Examples:** We have
(i) $\frac{2}{3}=0.666 \ldots=0 . \overline{6}$.
(ii) $\frac{3}{11}=0.2727 \ldots=0 . \overline{27}$.

$$
\begin{array}{c}
\text{(i)} \\
\begin{array}{r}
0.666\dots \\
3 \overline{) 2.000} \\
\underline{-18}\phantom{00} \\
20\phantom{0} \\
\underline{-18}\phantom{0} \\
20 \\
\underline{-18} \\
2
\end{array}
\end{array}
\qquad \qquad
\begin{array}{c}
\text{(ii)} \\
\begin{array}{r}
0.2727\dots \\
11 \overline{) 3.0000} \\
\underline{-22}\phantom{00} \\
80\phantom{0} \\
\underline{-77}\phantom{0} \\
30 \\
\underline{-22} \\
80 \\
\underline{-77} \\
3
\end{array}
\end{array}
$$

(iii) $\frac{15}{7}=2.142857142857 \ldots =2 . \overline{142857}$.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
2.142857\dots \\
7 \overline{) 15.000000} \\
\underline{-14}\phantom{000000} \\
10\phantom{00000} \\
\underline{-7}\phantom{00000} \\
30\phantom{0000} \\
\underline{-28}\phantom{0000} \\
20\phantom{000} \\
\underline{-14}\phantom{000} \\
60\phantom{00} \\
\underline{-56}\phantom{00} \\
40\phantom{0} \\
\underline{-35}\phantom{0} \\
50 \\
\underline{-49} \\
1
\end{array}
$$


(iv) $\frac{11}{6}=1.8333 \ldots =1.8 \overline{3}$.

$$
\begin{array}{r}
1.833\dots \\
6 \overline{) 11.000} \\
\underline{-6}\phantom{000} \\
50\phantom{00} \\
\underline{-48}\phantom{00} \\
20\phantom{0} \\
\underline{-18}\phantom{0} \\
20 \\
\underline{-18} \\
2
\end{array}
$$

You must have noticed a repeating string of remainders in each of the above cases.

* In (i), it is $2,2, \ldots$; in (ii), it is $8,3,8,3, \ldots$.
* In (iii), it is $1,3,2,6,4,5,1, \ldots$; in (iv), it is $5,2,2,2, \ldots$.

Kindly note that the number of entries in the repeating string of remainders is less than the divisor.

* In $\frac{2}{3}$, only one number 2 repeats itself and the divisor is 3.
* In $\frac{15}{7}$, a set of 6 digits, namely 132645, repeats itself and the divisor is 7.

Thus, if the divisor is $n$ then the maximum number of entries in the repeating block of digits in the decimal expansion of $\frac{1}{n}$ is $(n-1)$.

### Length of Period

Repeated number of decimal places in a rational number is called the length of its period.

**Example:** $\frac{15}{7}=2 . \overline{142857}$.
So, the length of its period is 6.

# Special Characteristics of Rational Numbers

(i) Every rational number is expressible either as a terminating decimal or as a nonterminating recurring decimal.
(ii) A number whose decimal expansion is terminating or nonterminating recurring is rational.

**Example 1:** Express $3 \frac{1}{8}$ in decimal form.

**Solution:** We have $3 \frac{1}{8}=\frac{25}{8}$.
By actual division, we have

$$
\begin{array}{r}

\underline{-40} \\

\end{array}
$$

$\therefore \quad \frac{25}{8}=3.125.$

**Example 4:** Find the decimal expansion of $\frac{1}{7} \cdot$ Can you predict what the decimal expansions of $\frac{2}{7}, \frac{3}{7}, \frac{4}{7}, \frac{5}{7}, \frac{6}{7}$ are, without actually doing the long division? If so, how?

**Solution:** By long division, we have

$$
\begin{array}{r}
0.142857\dots \\
7 \overline{) 1.000000} \\
\underline{-7}\phantom{00000} \\
30\phantom{0000} \\
\underline{-28}\phantom{0000} \\

\underline{-14}\phantom{000} \\

\underline{-56}\phantom{00} \\

\underline{-35}\phantom{0} \\
50 \\
\underline{-49} \\

\end{array}
$$

$\therefore \quad \frac{1}{7}=0.1428571428 \ldots=0 . \overline{142857}$.

Clearly,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{2}{7} &= 2 \times \frac{1}{7}=2 \times 0 . \overline{142857}=0 . \overline{285714}; \\
\frac{3}{7} &= 3 \times \frac{1}{7}=3 \times 0 . \overline{142857}=0 . \overline{428571}; \\
\frac{4}{7} &= 4 \times \frac{1}{7}=4 \times 0 . \overline{142857}=0 . \overline{571428}; \\
\frac{5}{7} &= 5 \times \frac{1}{7}=5 \times 0 . \overline{142857}=0 . \overline{714285}; \\
\frac{6}{7} &= 6 \times \frac{1}{7}=6 \times 0 . \overline{142857}=0 . \overline{857142}.
\end{aligned}
$$

**Example 5:** What can the maximum number of digits be in the repeating block of digits in the decimal expansion of $\frac{1}{17}$ ? Perform the division to check your answer.
**Solution:** By long division, we have

$$
17 \overline{) 1.0000000000000000\dots}
$$

And observing the remainders:

$$
\begin{array}{r}
80 \\
\underline{-68} \\
120 \\
\underline{-119} \\
100 \\
\underline{-85} \\
150 \\
\underline{-136} \\
140 \\
\underline{-136} \\

\end{array}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Clearly, } \frac{1}{17} & =0.05882352941176470588 \ldots \\
& =0 . \overline{0588235294117647}
\end{aligned}
$$

Length of period $=16$.
Hence, the maximum number of digits in the repeating block of digits in the decimal expansion of $\frac{1}{17}$ is less than 17, i.e., 16.

**Example 6:** Express $0 . \overline{3}$ in the form $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

**Solution:** Let $x=0 . \overline{3}$.
Then, $x=0.333 \ldots \tag{i}$.

Since the repeating block has only one digit 3, we multiply $x$ by 10 to get

$$
10 x=3.333 \ldots  \tag{ii}
$$

Subtracting (i) from (ii), we get

$$
9 x=3 \Leftrightarrow x=\frac{3}{9}=\frac{1}{3} .
$$

Hence, $0 . \overline{3}=\frac{1}{3}$.

---

**Example 7:** Express $1 . \overline{4}$ as a fraction in simplest form.

**Solution:** Let $x=1 . \overline{4}$.
Then, $x=1.444 \ldots \tag{i}$.

Since the repeating block has only one digit 4, we multiply $x$ by 10 to get

$$
10 x=14.444 \ldots  \tag{ii}
$$

Subtracting (i) from (ii), we get

$$
9 x=13 \Leftrightarrow x=\frac{13}{9}=1 \frac{4}{9} .
$$

Hence, $1 . \overline{4}=1 \frac{4}{9}$.

---

**Example 8:** Express 0.9999 ... as a fraction in simplest form.

**Solution:** Let $x=0.9999 \ldots \tag{i}$.

Since the repeating block has only digit 9, we multiply $x$ by 10 to get

$$
10 x=9.9999 \ldots \tag{ii}
$$

Subtracting (i) from (ii), we get

$$
9 x=9 \Leftrightarrow x=1 .
$$

Hence, $0.9999 \ldots=1$.

**Example 9:** Express $0 . \overline{57}$ in the form $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$.

**Solution:** Let $x=0 . \overline{57}$.
Then, $x=0.575757 \ldots \tag{i}$.

Since the repeating block 57 has two digits, we multiply $x$ by 100 to get

$$
100 x=57.5757 \ldots \tag{ii}
$$

Subtracting (i) from (ii), we get

$$
99 x=57 \Leftrightarrow x=\frac{57}{99}=\frac{19}{33} .
$$

Hence, $0 . \overline{57}=\frac{19}{33}$.

---

**Example 10:** Express $0 . \overline{001}$ as a fraction in simplest form.

**Solution:** Let $x=0 . \overline{001}$.
Then, $x=0.001001001 \ldots \tag{i}$.

Since the repeating block 001 has 3 digits, we multiply $x$ by 1000 to get

$$
1000 x=1.001001001 \ldots \tag{ii}
$$

Subtracting (i) from (ii), we get

$$
999 x=1 \Leftrightarrow x=\frac{1}{999}
$$

Hence, $0 . \overline{001}=\frac{1}{999}$.
