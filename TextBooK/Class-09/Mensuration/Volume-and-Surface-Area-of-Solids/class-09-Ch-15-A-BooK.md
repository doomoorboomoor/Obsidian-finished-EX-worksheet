# Volume and Surface Area of Solids

**Solids**: The bodies occupying space are called solids. The solid bodies occur in various shapes such as a cuboid, a cube, a cylinder, a cone, a sphere, etc.

**Volume of a solid**: The space occupied by a solid body is called its volume. The units of volume are cubic centimetres (written as $cm^{3}$) or cubic metres (written as $m^{3}$).

**Cuboid**: A solid bounded by six rectangular faces is called a cuboid. A matchbox, a chalkbox, a brick, a tile, a book, etc., are all examples of a cuboid.

A cuboid has **6 rectangular faces**, **12 edges** and **8 vertices**.

In the given figure, $ABCDEFGH$ is a cuboid whose:
- **6 faces are**: $ABCD$, $EFGH$, $ADHE$, $BCGF$, $ABFE$ and $DCGH$. Out of these, the four faces, namely $ABFE$, $DCGH$, $BCGF$ and $ADHE$ are called **lateral faces** of the cuboid.
- **12 edges are**: $AB, BC, CD, DA, EF, FG, GH, HE, CG, BF, AE$ and $DH$.
- **8 vertices are**: $A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H$.

*REMARK*: A rectangular room is in the form of a cuboid and its 4 walls are its lateral faces.

**Cube**: A cuboid whose length, breadth and height are all equal is called a cube. Dice, ice cubes, sugar cubes, etc., are all examples of a cube. Each edge of a cube is called its **side**.

## Volume and Surface Area of a Cuboid

#### Formulae:

Let us consider a cuboid of **length** $= l$ units, **breadth** $= b$ units and **height** $= h$ units. Then, we have:

- **Volume of the cuboid**
$$
V = l \times b \times h \text{ cubic units}
$$
- **Diagonal of the cuboid**
$$
d = \sqrt{l^{2}+b^{2}+h^{2}} \text{ units}
$$
- **Total surface area of the cuboid (TSA)**
$$
TSA = 2(lb+bh+lh) \text{ sq units}
$$
- **Lateral surface area of the cuboid (LSA)**
$$
LSA = 2(l+b) \times h \text{ sq units}
$$
- **Area of four walls of a room**
$$
\text{Area} = 2(l+b) \times h \text{ sq units}
$$

## Volume and Surface Area of a Cube

#### Formulae:

Let us consider a cube of **edge** $= a$ units. Then, we have:

- **Volume of the cube**
$$
V = a^{3} \text{ cu units}
$$
- **Diagonal of the cube**
$$
d = \sqrt{3}a \text{ units}
$$
- **Total surface area of the cube (TSA)**
$$
TSA = 6a^{2} \text{ sq units}
$$
- **Lateral surface area of the cube (LSA)**
$$
LSA = 4a^{2} \text{ sq units}
$$

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Find the volume, the total surface area and the lateral surface area of a cuboid which is 15 m long, 12 m wide and 4.5 m high.

#### Solution:

Here, $l=15 \text{ m}$, $b=12 \text{ m}$ and $h=4.5 \text{ m}$.

**Volume of the cuboid** $=(l \times b \times h)$ cubic units
$$
(15 \times 12 \times 4.5) \text{ m}^{3} = 810 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
**Total surface area of the cuboid** $=2(lb+bh+lh)$ sq units
$$
2(15 \times 12+12 \times 4.5+15 \times 4.5) \text{ m}^{2} = 603 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Lateral surface area of the cuboid** $=[2(l+b) \times h]$ sq units
$$
[2(15+12) \times 4.5] \text{ m}^{2} = 243 \text{ m}^{2}
$$

### Example 2

How many bricks will be required to construct a wall 13.5 m long, 6 m high and 22.5 cm thick? It is being given that each brick measures ($27 \text{ cm} \times 12.5 \text{ cm} \times 9 \text{ cm}$)?

#### Solution:

Length of the wall $=(13.5 \times 100) \text{ cm} = 1350 \text{ cm}$.
Breadth of the wall $= 22.5 \text{ cm}$.
Height of the wall $=(6 \times 100) \text{ cm} = 600 \text{ cm}$.

**Volume of the wall**
$$
(1350 \times 22.5 \times 600) \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
**Volume of each brick**
$$
(27 \times 12.5 \times 9) \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
**Number of bricks required**
$$
\frac{\text{Volume of the wall}}{\text{Volume of 1 brick}} = \frac{1350 \times 22.5 \times 600}{27 \times 12.5 \times 9} = 6000
$$

### Example 3

A room is 16 m long, 9 m wide and 3 m high. It has two doors, each of dimensions ($2 \text{ m} \times 1.5 \text{ m}$) and three windows, each of dimensions ($1.6 \text{ m} \times 75 \text{ cm}$). Find the cost of distempering the walls of the room from inside at the rate of ₹ 50 per square metre.

#### Solution:

**Area of 4 walls of the room** $=[2(l+b) \times h]$ sq units
$$
[2(16+9) \times 3] \text{ m}^{2} = 150 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area of 2 doors**
$$
\left[2 \times\left(2 \times \frac{3}{2}\right)\right] \text{ m}^{2} = 6 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area of 3 windows**
$$
\left[3 \times\left(1.6 \times \frac{75}{100}\right)\right] \text{ m}^{2} = \frac{18}{5} \text{ m}^{2} = 3.6 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area not to be distempered**
$$
(6+3.6) \text{ m}^{2} = 9.6 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area to be distempered**
$$
(150-9.6) \text{ m}^{2} = 140.4 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Cost of distempering the walls**
$$
₹(140.4 \times 50) = ₹ 7020
$$

### Example 4

A field is 70 m long and 40 m broad. In one corner of the field, a pit which is 10 m long, 8 m broad and 5 m deep, has been dug out. The earth taken out of it is evenly spread over the remaining part of the field. Find the rise in the level of the field.

#### Solution:

**Area of the field**
$$
(70 \times 40) \text{ m}^{2} = 2800 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area of the pit**
$$
(10 \times 8) \text{ m}^{2} = 80 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Area over which the earth is spread**
$$
(2800-80) \text{ m}^{2} = 2720 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
**Volume of the earth dug out**
$$
(10 \times 8 \times 5) \text{ m}^{3} = 400 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
**Rise in level of the field**
$$
\frac{\text{Volume of the earth dug out}}{\text{Area on which the earth is spread}} = \left(\frac{400}{2720}\right) \text{ m} = \left(\frac{400 \times 100}{2720}\right) \text{ cm} = \frac{250}{17} \text{ cm} \approx 14.70 \text{ cm}
$$

### Example 5

An open wooden box 80 cm long, 65 cm wide and 45 cm high, is made of 2.5 cm thick wood. Find (i) the capacity of the box, (ii) volume of wood used and (iii) weight of the box, it being given that $100 \text{ cm}^{3}$ of wood weighs 8 g.

#### Solution:

External length of the box $= 80 \text{ cm}$.
External breadth of the box $= 65 \text{ cm}$.
External height of the box $= 45 \text{ cm}$.

**External volume of the box**
$$
(80 \times 65 \times 45) \text{ cu cm} = 234000 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
**Internal length of the box**
$$
[80-(2.5 \times 2)] \text{ cm} = (80-5) \text{ cm} = 75 \text{ cm}
$$
**Internal breadth of the box**
$$
[65-(2.5 \times 2)] \text{ cm} = (65-5) \text{ cm} = 60 \text{ cm}
$$
**Internal height of the box**
$$
(45-2.5) \text{ cm} = 42.5 \text{ cm}
$$
(i) **Capacity of the box** = internal volume of the box
$$
(75 \times 60 \times 42.5) \text{ cm}^{3} = 191250 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
(ii) **Volume of wood used** = (external volume) - (internal volume)
$$
(234000-191250) \text{ cm}^{3} = 42750 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
(iii) **Weight of the box**
Weight of $100 \text{ cm}^{3}$ of wood $= 8 \text{ g}$.
$$
\text{Weight of } 42750 \text{ cm}^{3} \text{ of wood} = \left(\frac{8}{100} \times 42750\right) \text{ g} = 3420 \text{ g} = 3 \text{ kg } 420 \text{ g}
$$

### Example 7

The total surface area of a cube is $216 \text{ cm}^{2}$. Find its volume.

#### Solution:

Let each side of the cube be $a$ cm. Then, the total surface area of the cube is $(6a^{2}) \text{ cm}^{2}$.
$$
6a^{2} = 216 \implies a^{2} = 36 \implies a = \sqrt{36} = 6
$$
**Volume of the cube** $= a^{3} \text{ cm}^{3}$
$$
(6 \times 6 \times 6) \text{ cm}^{3} = 216 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$

$$
4a^{2} = 324 \implies a^{2} = 81 \implies a = \sqrt{81} = 9

**Volume of the cube** $= a^{3} \text{ cm}^{3}$
$$
(9 \times 9 \times 9) \text{ cm}^{3} = 729 \text{ cm}^{3}

**Total surface area of the cube** $= (6a^{2})$ sq units

### Example 9

How many square sheets of coloured paper of side 30 cm would be required to cover a wooden box having length, breadth and height as 90 cm, 60 cm and 30 cm respectively?

#### Solution:

The quantity of paper required is equal to the total surface area of the wooden box (a cuboid).
The dimensions of the box are:
**length** $= 90 \text{ cm}$, **breadth** $= 60 \text{ cm}$ and **height** $= 30 \text{ cm}$.

**The total surface area of the box** $= 2(lb+bh+lh)$
$$
2 \times [(90 \times 60)+(60 \times 30)+(90 \times 30)] \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
= 2 \times (5400+1800+2700) \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
= (2 \times 9900) \text{ cm}^{2} = 19800 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
**The area of each sheet of paper**
$$
(30 \times 30) \text{ cm}^{2} = 900 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
**Number of sheets required**
$$
\frac{\text{Total surface area of the box}}{\text{Area of one sheet of paper}} = \frac{19800}{900} = 22
$$

A small indoor greenhouse (herbarium) is made entirely of glass panes (including base) held together with tape. It is 35 cm long, 30 cm wide and 25 cm high.
(i) What is the area of the glass?
(ii) What length of tape is needed for all the 12 edges?

#### Solution:

The indoor greenhouse is a cuboid with **length** $= 35 \text{ cm}$, **breadth** $= 30 \text{ cm}$ and **height** $= 25 \text{ cm}$.

(i) **Area of the glass** = The total surface area of the cuboid
$$
= 2(lb+bh+lh)
$$
$$
= 2 \times [(35 \times 30)+(30 \times 25)+(35 \times 25)] \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
= 2(1050+750+875) \text{ cm}^{2} = (2 \times 2675) \text{ cm}^{2} = 5350 \text{ cm}^{2}

$$
= (\text{perimeter of top}) + (\text{perimeter of bottom}) + (4 \text{ vertical edges})

= \{2(l+b)+2(l+b)+4h\} = 4(l+b+h)

$$
= 4(35+30+25) \text{ cm} = 360 \text{ cm} = 3 \text{ m } 60 \text{ cm}

### Example 13

A cubical box has each edge 10 cm and another cuboidal box is 12.5 cm long, 10 cm wide and 8 cm high.
(i) Which box has the greater lateral surface area and by how much?
(ii) Which box has the smaller total surface area and by how much?

#### Solution:

(i) **Lateral Surface Area Comparison**
Lateral surface area of the **cubical box**
$$
= 4(\text{edge})^{2} = (4 \times 10 \times 10) \text{ cm}^{2} = 400 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Lateral surface area of the **cuboidal box**
$$
= 2(\text{length} + \text{breadth}) \times \text{height}
$$
$$
= \{2(12.5+10) \times 8\} \text{ cm}^{2} = (2 \times 22.5 \times 8) \text{ cm}^{2} = 360 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Difference in lateral surface areas
$$
(400-360) \text{ cm}^{2} = 40 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Hence, the lateral surface area of the **cubical box is larger** than that of the cuboidal box by $40 \text{ cm}^{2}$.

(ii) **Total Surface Area Comparison**
Total surface area of the **cubical box**
$$
= 6(\text{edge})^{2} = (6 \times 10 \times 10) \text{ cm}^{2} = 600 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Total surface area of the **cuboidal box**
$$
= 2(lb+bh+lh)
$$
$$
= 2(12.5 \times 10+10 \times 8+12.5 \times 8) \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
= 2(125+80+100) \text{ cm}^{2} = (2 \times 305) \text{ cm}^{2} = 610 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Difference in total surface areas
$$
(610-600) \text{ cm}^{2} = 10 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Hence, the total surface area of the **cubical box is smaller** than that of the cuboidal box by $10 \text{ cm}^{2}$.

### Example 14

Kunal built a cubical water tank with lid for his house, with each outer edge 1.5 m long. He gets the outer surface of the tank excluding the base covered with square tiles of side 25 cm. Find how much he would spend for the tiles, if the cost of the tiles is ₹ 540 per dozen.

Leaving aside the base, the remaining five faces of the tank are to be covered with tiles.
Length of each edge of the tank $= 1.5 \text{ m} = (1.5 \times 100) \text{ cm} = 150 \text{ cm}$.

$$
(5 \times 150 \times 150) \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Area of each square tile
$$
(25 \times 25) \text{ cm}^{2}

### Example 15

A solid cube of side 12 cm is cut into 8 cubes of equal volume. What is the side of the new cube? Also, find the ratio between their surface areas.

#### Solution:

Volume of the big cube
$$
(12 \times 12 \times 12) \text{ cm}^{3} = 1728 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
Volume of a small (new) cube
$$
\frac{1728}{8} \text{ cm}^{3} = 216 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
Let each side of the new cube be $a$ cm. Then, its volume is $a^{3} \text{ cm}^{3}$.
$$
a^{3} = 216 = (6)^{3} \implies a = 6
$$
Thus, the side of the new cube is **6 cm**.

Surface area of the big cube
$$
\{6 \times (12)^{2}\} \text{ cm}^{2} = (6 \times 144) \text{ cm}^{2} = 864 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Surface area of the smaller cube
$$
\{6 \times (6)^{2}\} \text{ cm}^{2} = (6 \times 36) \text{ cm}^{2} = 216 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
Ratio between the surface areas
$$
864 : 216 = \frac{864}{216} = \frac{4}{1} = 4:1
$$

### Example 17

The length of a cold storage is double its breadth and its height is 3 metres. If the area of its four walls is $108 \text{ m}^{2}$, find its volume.

#### Solution:

Let the breadth of the cold storage be $x$ metres.
Then, its length $= 2x$ metres and height $= 3$ metres.

Area of the four walls of the cold storage
$$
= \{2(l+b) \times h\} \text{ sq units}
$$
$$
= \{2(2x+x) \times 3\} \text{ m}^{2} = (18x) \text{ m}^{2}
$$
But, the area of 4 walls is given as $108 \text{ m}^{2}$.
$$
18x = 108 \implies x = \frac{108}{18} = 6
$$
So, **breadth** $= 6 \text{ m}$ and **length** $= 12 \text{ m}$.

Volume of the cold storage $= (l \times b \times h)$ cubic units
$$
= (12 \times 6 \times 3) \text{ m}^{3} = 216 \text{ m}^{3}
$$

### Example 18

A village having a population of 4000, requires 150 litres of water per head per day. It has a tank measuring $20 \text{ m} \times 15 \text{ m} \times 6 \text{ m}$. For how many days will the water of this tank last?

#### Solution:

Volume of the water tank
$$
(20 \times 15 \times 6) \text{ m}^{3} = 1800 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
Consumption of water per head per day
$$
= 150 \text{ litres} = \left(150 \times \frac{1}{1000}\right) \text{ m}^{3} = \frac{3}{20} \text{ m}^{3} \quad [\because 1 \text{ litre} = \frac{1}{1000} \text{ m}^{3}]
$$
Total consumption of water per day for 4000 persons
$$
= \left(\frac{3}{20} \times 4000\right) \text{ m}^{3} = 600 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
Required no. of days for which water will last
$$
= \frac{\text{Volume of water tank}}{\text{Consumption per day}} = \frac{1800}{600} \text{ days} = 3 \text{ days}
$$

### Example 19

A room is half as long again as it is broad. The cost of carpeting the room at ₹ 13 per $\text{m}^{2}$ is ₹ 702 and the cost of papering the walls at ₹ 7 per $\text{m}^{2}$ is ₹ 1204. If 1 door and 2 windows occupy $8 \text{ m}^{2}$, find the dimensions of the room.

#### Solution:

Let the breadth of the room be $x$ metres.
Then, its length $= \left(x+\frac{x}{2}\right) \text{ metres} = \frac{3x}{2} \text{ metres}$.

Area of the floor $= (l \times b) = \left(\frac{3x}{2} \times x\right) \text{ m}^{2} = \frac{3x^{2}}{2} \text{ m}^{2}$.
Also, area of the floor
$$
= \frac{\text{Total cost of carpeting}}{\text{Rate per m}^{2}} = \left(\frac{702}{13}\right) \text{ m}^{2} = 54 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
Therefore,
$$
\frac{3x^{2}}{2} = 54 \implies x^{2} = \left(54 \times \frac{2}{3}\right) = 36 = (6)^{2} \implies x = 6
$$
So, **breadth** $= 6 \text{ m}$ and **length** $= \left(\frac{3}{2} \times 6\right) \text{ m} = 9 \text{ m}$.

Area of papered walls
$$
= \frac{\text{Total cost of papering}}{\text{Rate per m}^{2}} = \frac{1204}{7} \text{ m}^{2} = 172 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
Area of 1 door and 2 windows $= 8 \text{ m}^{2}$.
Total area of 4 walls $= (172+8) \text{ m}^{2} = 180 \text{ m}^{2}$.

Let the height of the room be $h$ metres.
Area of 4 walls $= \{2(l+b) \times h\} = \{2(9+6) \times h\} \text{ m}^{2} = (30h) \text{ m}^{2}$.
$$
30h = 180 \implies h = \frac{180}{30} = 6 \text{ m}
$$
Hence, **length** $= 9 \text{ m}$, **breadth** $= 6 \text{ m}$ and **height** $= 6 \text{ m}$.

### Example 20

Water flows in a tank $60 \text{ m} \times 40 \text{ m}$ at the base through a pipe whose cross section is 2 dm by 1.5 dm at the speed of 8 km per hour. In what time will the water be 5 metres deep?

#### Solution:

Let the water be 5 m deep in the tank in $x$ hours.
Volume of water required in the tank
$$
(60 \times 40 \times 5) \text{ m}^{3}
$$
Volume of water flown in the tank in $x$ hours
$$
= \left(\frac{2}{10} \times \frac{1.5}{10} \times 8000 \times x\right) \text{ m}^{3}
$$
Equating the volumes:
$$
\frac{2}{10} \times \frac{1.5}{10} \times 8000 \times x = 60 \times 40 \times 5
$$
$$
240x = 60 \times 40 \times 5 \implies x = \frac{60 \times 40 \times 5}{240} \implies x=50
$$
Hence, the required time is **50 hours**.

### Example 21

A storage tank is in the form of a cube. When it is full of water, the volume of water is $15.625 \text{ m}^{3}$. If the present depth of water is 1.3 m, find the volume of water used from the tank.

#### Solution:

Let each edge of the tank be $a$ metres.
Volume of water in the tank when full $= (a^{3}) \text{ m}^{3}$.
Given volume of water in full tank $= 15.625 \text{ m}^{3}$.
$$
a^{3} = 15.625 = \frac{15625}{1000} = \frac{125}{8} = \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{3}
$$
$$
a = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5
$$
Thus, each edge of the tank is 2.5 m.

Present depth of water $= 1.3 \text{ m}$.
Present volume of water in the tank
$$
= (2.5 \times 2.5 \times 1.3) \text{ m}^{3}
$$
$$
= \left(\frac{5}{2} \times \frac{5}{2} \times \frac{13}{10}\right) \text{ m}^{3} = \frac{65}{8} \text{ m}^{3} = 8.125 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
Volume of water used from the tank
$$
= (15.625-8.125) \text{ m}^{3} = 7.5 \text{ m}^{3}
$$
