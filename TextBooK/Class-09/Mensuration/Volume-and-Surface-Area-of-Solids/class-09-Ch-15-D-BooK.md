## SPHERE

Objects like a football, volleyball, throwball, etc., are said to have the shape of a **sphere**.

In geometry, the solid generated by revolving a circular lamina about any of its diameters is called a **sphere**.

The **centre** and **radius** of this circle are called respectively the centre and the radius of the sphere.
![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Mensuration/Volume-and-Surface-Area-of-Solids/class-09-Ch-15-D-BooK-001.jpg)

**Formulae:**
For a solid sphere of radius $r$ units, we have:
- **Volume of the sphere** = $\left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right)$ cubic units.
- **Surface area of the sphere** = $\left(4 \pi r^{2}\right)$ square units.

---

## SPHERICAL SHELL

A **spherical shell** is the solid enclosed between two concentric spheres.

For a spherical shell with **external radius** $R$ and **internal radius** $r$, we have:
**Volume of the spherical shell**:
$$
V = \frac{4}{3} \pi\left(R^{3}-r^{3}\right) \text{ cubic units.}
$$

---

## HEMISPHERE

When a plane through the centre of a sphere cuts it into two equal parts, then each part is called a **hemisphere**.

For a hemisphere of **radius** $r$, we have:
- **Volume of the hemisphere** = $\left(\frac{2}{3} \pi r^{3}\right)$ cubic units.
- **Curved surface area of the hemisphere** = $\left(2 \pi r^{2}\right)$ square units.
- **Total surface area of the hemisphere** = $\left(3 \pi r^{2}\right)$ square units.

### Example: 1

Find the volume and surface area of a sphere of radius 21 cm.

#### Solution:

Radius of the sphere, $r = 21 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of the sphere:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
V &= \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) \\
&= \left(\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 21 \times 21 \times 21\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= 38808 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Surface area of the sphere:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
A &= \left(4 \pi r^{2}\right) \\
&= \left(4 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 21 \times 21\right) \mathrm{cm}^{2} \\
&= 5544 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 2

Find the surface area of a sphere whose volume is $4851 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}$.

#### Solution:

Let the radius of the sphere be $r$ cm. Then, its volume is $\left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} &= 4851 \\
\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times r^{3} &= 4851 \\
r^{3} &= \left(4851 \times \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{7}{22}\right) = \left(\frac{441 \times 21}{8}\right) = \left(\frac{21}{2}\right)^{3} \\
r &= \frac{21}{2} = 10.5
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, the radius of the sphere is **10.5 cm**.

**Surface area of the sphere:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
A &= \left(4 \pi r^{2}\right) \\
&= \left(4 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{21}{2} \times \frac{21}{2}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{2} \\
&= 1386 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the surface area of the given sphere is **1386 cm²**.

### Example: 3

The surface area of a sphere is $346.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$. Find its radius and hence its volume.

#### Solution:

Let the radius of the sphere be $r$ cm. Then, its surface area is $\left(4 \pi r^{2}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{2}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
4 \pi r^{2} &= 346.5 \\
4 \times \frac{22}{7} \times r^{2} &= 346.5 \\
r^{2} &= \left(346.5 \times \frac{7}{88}\right) = \left(\frac{3465 \times 7}{10 \times 88}\right) = \frac{441}{16} \\
r &= \frac{21}{4} = 5.25
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, the radius of the sphere is **5.25 cm**.

**Volume of the sphere:**
$$
\begin{aligned}
V &= \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) \\
&= \left(\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 5.25 \times 5.25 \times 5.25\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= \left(\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{525}{100} \times \frac{525}{100} \times \frac{525}{100}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= \frac{4851}{8} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} = 606.375 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the volume of the sphere is **606.375 cm³**.

### Example: 4

How many spherical bullets can be made out of a solid cube of lead whose edge measures 44 cm, each bullet being 4 cm in diameter?

#### Solution:

Length of each edge of the cube = **44 cm**.
Volume of the cube = $(44 \times 44 \times 44) \mathrm{cm}^{3}$.

Radius of each bullet, $r = 2 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Volume of each bullet:
$$
V_{bullet} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) = \left(\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 2 \times 2 \times 2\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \frac{704}{21} \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
$$

**Number of bullets formed:**
$$
\text{Number} = \frac{\text{volume of the cube}}{\text{volume of each bullet}} = \left(44 \times 44 \times 44 \times \frac{21}{704}\right) = 2541
$$

Hence, the number of bullets formed is **2541**.

### Example: 5

The diameter of a copper sphere is 6 cm. The sphere is melted and drawn into a long wire of uniform circular cross section. If the length of the wire is 36 cm, find its radius.

#### Solution:

Radius of the sphere, $R = 3 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of the sphere:**
$$
V_{sphere} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi R^{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi \times 3 \times 3 \times 3\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = (36 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$

Let the radius of the wire formed be $r$ cm.
Volume of the wire (cylinder), with height $h = 36$ cm:
$$
V_{wire} = \left(\pi r^{2} h\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\pi r^{2} \times 36\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$

Since the volume remains the same:
$$
36 \pi r^{2} = 36 \pi \implies r^{2} = 1 \implies r = 1
$$

Hence, the radius of the wire is **1 cm**.

### Example: 6

A metallic sphere of radius 21 cm is dropped into a cylindrical vessel, which is partly filled with water. The diameter of the vessel is 1.68 m. If the sphere is completely submerged, find by how much the surface of water will rise.

#### Solution:

Radius of the sphere, $r_{sphere} = 21 \mathrm{~cm}$.
**Volume of the sphere:**
$$
V_{sphere} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r_{sphere}^{3}\right) = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi \times 21 \times 21 \times 21\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = (12348 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$

The volume of water displaced by the sphere is $(12348 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{3}$.
Diameter of the cylindrical vessel = $1.68 \mathrm{~m} = 168 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Radius of the vessel, $r_{vessel} = 84 \mathrm{~cm}$.

Let the water rise by $h$ cm. The volume of the displaced water is equal to the volume of a cylinder with radius 84 cm and height $h$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
V_{displaced} &= \pi \times (r_{vessel})^2 \times h \\
12348 \pi &= \pi \times 84 \times 84 \times h \\
h &= \frac{12348}{84 \times 84} = \frac{7}{4} \mathrm{~cm} = 1.75 \mathrm{~cm}
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the surface of water will rise by **1.75 cm**.

### Example: 7

A hollow sphere of external and internal diameters 8 cm and 4 cm respectively is melted into a cone of base diameter 8 cm. Find the height of the cone.

#### Solution:

External radius of the sphere, $R = 4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Internal radius of the sphere, $r = 2 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of the material of the sphere:**
$$
V_{sphere} = \frac{4}{3} \pi\left(R^{3}-r^{3}\right) = \frac{4}{3} \pi\left(4^{3}-2^{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{224 \pi}{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$

Radius of the resulting cone, $r_{cone} = 4 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Let the height of this cone be $h$ cm.

**Volume of the cone:**
$$
V_{cone} = \left(\frac{1}{3} \pi r_{cone}^{2} h\right) = \left(\frac{1}{3} \pi \times 4 \times 4 \times h\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{16 \pi h}{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$

Since the volumes are equal:
$$
\frac{16 \pi h}{3} = \frac{224 \pi}{3} \implies h = \frac{224}{16} = 14
$$

Hence, the height of the cone is **14 cm**.

### Example: 8

Find the volume, curved surface area and the total surface area of a hemisphere of diameter 7 cm.

#### Solution:

Radius of the hemisphere, $r = 3.5 \mathrm{~cm} = \frac{7}{2} \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of the hemisphere:**
$$
V = \left(\frac{2}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) = \left(\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{7}{2} \times \frac{7}{2} \times \frac{7}{2}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \frac{539}{6} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} \approx 89.83 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
$$

**Curved surface area of the hemisphere:**
$$
A_{curved} = \left(2 \pi r^{2}\right) = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{7}{2} \times \frac{7}{2}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = 77 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
$$

**Total surface area of the hemisphere:**
$$
A_{total} = \left(3 \pi r^{2}\right) = \left(3 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{7}{2} \times \frac{7}{2}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = \frac{231}{2} \mathrm{~cm}^{2} = 115.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
$$

### Example: 9

The internal and external diameters of a hollow hemispherical vessel are 20 cm and 28 cm respectively. Find the cost of painting the vessel all over at 35 paise per $\mathrm{cm}^{2}$.

#### Solution:

Outer radius of the vessel, $R = 14 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Inner radius of the vessel, $r = 10 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Area of the outer surface:**
$$
A_{outer} = \left(2 \pi R^{2}\right) = (2 \pi \times 14 \times 14) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (392 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

**Area of the inner surface:**
$$
A_{inner} = \left(2 \pi r^{2}\right) = (2 \pi \times 10 \times 10) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (200 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

**Area of the ring at the top:**
$$
A_{ring} = \pi\left(R^{2}-r^{2}\right) = \pi\left(14^{2}-10^{2}\right) = \pi(14+10)(14-10) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (96 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

**Total area to be painted:**
$$
A_{total} = (392 \pi + 200 \pi + 96 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (688 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

**Cost of painting:**
The rate is ₹0.35 per $\mathrm{cm}^{2}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Cost of painting } & =\text{₹}\left(688 \pi \times \frac{35}{100}\right) \\
& =\text{₹}\left(688 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{35}{100}\right) \\
& =\text{₹} \frac{3784}{5}=\text{₹} 756.80
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 10

On increasing the radius of a sphere by 10%, find the percentage increase in its volume.

#### Solution:

Let the original radius of the sphere be $r$ units.
Original volume, $V_{1} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right)$ cubic units.

The new radius is increased by 10%:
$$
r_{new} = r + 10\% \text{ of } r = r + 0.1r = 1.1r = \frac{11r}{10}
$$

Volume of the new sphere:
$$
V_{2} = \frac{4}{3} \pi \left(\frac{11r}{10}\right)^{3} = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} \times \frac{1331}{1000}
$$

Increase in volume:
$$
\Delta V = V_2 - V_1 = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} \times \frac{1331}{1000}\right) - \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}\right) = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} \left(\frac{1331}{1000}-1\right) = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} \left(\frac{331}{1000}\right)
$$

Percentage increase in volume:
$$
\% \text{ Increase} = \frac{\Delta V}{V_1} \times 100 = \frac{\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} \times \frac{331}{1000}}{\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}} \times 100 = \left(\frac{331}{1000} \times 100\right)\% = 33.1\%
$$

### Example: 11

The surface area of a sphere of radius 5 cm is five times the area of the curved surface of a cone of radius 4 cm. Find the height and the volume of the cone. (Given $\pi=3.14$).

#### Solution:

Surface area of the sphere with radius $R=5$ cm:
$$
A_{sphere} = 4 \pi R^{2} = (4 \pi \times 5 \times 5) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (100 \pi) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

Radius of the base of the cone, $r = 4$ cm. Let the slant height be $l$ cm.
Curved surface area of the cone:
$$
A_{cone} = \pi r l = (\pi \times 4 \times l) \mathrm{cm}^{2} = (4 \pi l) \mathrm{cm}^{2}
$$

According to the problem, $A_{sphere} = 5 \times A_{cone}$:
$$
100 \pi = 5 \times (4 \pi l) \implies 100 \pi = 20 \pi l \implies l=5 \mathrm{~cm}
$$

Let the height of the cone be $h$ cm. We know $l^{2}=h^{2}+r^{2}$.
$$
h^{2} = l^{2} - r^{2} = 5^{2} - 4^{2} = 25 - 16 = 9 \implies h = 3 \mathrm{~cm}
$$

**Volume of the cone:**
$$
V_{cone} = \frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h = \left(\frac{1}{3} \times 3.14 \times 4 \times 4 \times 3\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = 50.24 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
$$

Hence, the height of the cone is **3 cm** and its volume is **50.24 cm³**.

### Example: 12

The volumes of two spheres are in the ratio $64:27$. Find the ratio of their surface areas.

#### Solution:

Let the radii of the spheres be $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ and their volumes be $V_{1}$ and $V_{2}$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{V_{1}}{V_{2}} &= \frac{64}{27} \\
\frac{\frac{4}{3} \pi R_{1}^{3}}{\frac{4}{3} \pi R_{2}^{3}} &= \frac{64}{27} \\
\frac{R_{1}^{3}}{R_{2}^{3}} &= \frac{64}{27} \\
\left(\frac{R_{1}}{R_{2}}\right)^{3} &= \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^{3} \\
\frac{R_{1}}{R_{2}} &= \frac{4}{3} \quad \text{(i)}
\end{aligned}
$$

Let their surface areas be $S_{1}$ and $S_{2}$.
$$
\frac{S_{1}}{S_{2}} = \frac{4 \pi R_{1}^{2}}{4 \pi R_{2}^{2}} = \left(\frac{R_{1}}{R_{2}}\right)^{2} = \left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^{2} = \frac{16}{9} \quad \text{[using (i)]}
$$
Hence, $S_{1}: S_{2} = 16:9$.

### Example: 13

A sphere and a right circular cylinder of the same radius have equal volumes. By what percentage does the diameter of the cylinder exceed its height?

#### Solution:

Let the sphere and cylinder have the same radius $r$, and let the height of the cylinder be $h$.

Given: *volume of sphere = volume of cylinder*
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} &= \pi r^{2} h \\
\frac{4}{3} r &= h \implies r = \frac{3h}{4}
\end{aligned}
$$
The diameter of the cylinder is $d = 2r$.
$$
d = 2 \left(\frac{3h}{4}\right) = \frac{3h}{2}
$$
Excess of diameter over height:
$$
\text{Excess} = d - h = \frac{3h}{2} - h = \frac{h}{2}
$$
Percentage excess:
$$
\text{Excess} \% = \left(\frac{\text{Excess}}{\text{height}} \times 100\right) = \left(\frac{h/2}{h} \times 100\right)\% = 50\%
$$

### Example: 14

The water for a factory is stored in a hemispherical tank whose internal diameter is 14 m. The tank contains 50 kilolitres of water. Water is pumped into the tank to fill it to its capacity. Calculate the volume of water pumped into the tank.

#### Solution:

Radius of the hemispherical tank, $r = \frac{14}{2} = 7 \mathrm{~m}$.

**Capacity of the full tank (volume):**
$$
V_{tank} = \frac{2}{3} \pi r^{3} = \left(\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \times 7\right) \mathrm{m}^{3} = \frac{2156}{3} \mathrm{~m}^{3}
$$
Volume of water already in the tank = $50 \mathrm{~kL} = 50 \mathrm{~m}^{3}$ (since $1 \mathrm{~kL} = 1 \mathrm{~m}^{3}$).

**Volume of water pumped into the tank:**
$$
V_{pumped} = V_{tank} - V_{initial} = \left(\frac{2156}{3} - 50\right) \mathrm{m}^{3} = \frac{2156 - 150}{3} \mathrm{m}^{3} = \frac{2006}{3} \mathrm{~m}^{3} \approx 668.67 \mathrm{~m}^{3}
$$

### Example: 15

A shopkeeper has one spherical laddoo of radius 5 cm. With the same amount of material, how many laddoos of radius 2.5 cm can be made?

#### Solution:

Volume of the large laddoo (radius $R=5$ cm):
$$
V_{large} = \left\{\frac{4}{3} \pi \times (5)^{3}\right\} \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{500 \pi}{3}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$
Volume of one small laddoo (radius $r=2.5$ cm or $\frac{5}{2}$ cm):
$$
V_{small} = \left\{\frac{4}{3} \pi \times \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{3}\right\} \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \pi \times \frac{125}{8}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{125 \pi}{6}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3}
$$
Let $n$ be the number of small laddoos that can be made.
$$
n = \frac{V_{large}}{V_{small}} = \frac{\frac{500 \pi}{3}}{\frac{125 \pi}{6}} = \frac{500 \pi}{3} \times \frac{6}{125 \pi} = \frac{500 \times 2}{125} = 4 \times 2 = 8
$$
Hence, the required number of laddoos is **8**.

### Example: 16

A shot-put is a metallic sphere of radius 4.9 cm. If the density of the metal is 7.8 g per $\mathrm{cm}^{3}$, find the mass of the shot-put.

#### Solution:

A shot-put is a solid sphere. Its mass is the product of its volume and density.
Radius, $r = 4.9 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of the shot-put:**
$$
V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3} = \left(\frac{4}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{49}{10} \times \frac{49}{10} \times \frac{49}{10}\right) \mathrm{cm}^{3} = \frac{1479016}{3000} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} \approx 493 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
$$
Density of the metal = $7.8 \mathrm{~g/cm}^{3}$.

**Mass of the shot-put:**
$$
\text{Mass} = \text{Volume} \times \text{Density} = (493 \times 7.8) \mathrm{g} = 3845.4 \mathrm{~g} \approx 3.85 \mathrm{~kg}
$$
Hence, the mass of the given shot-put is approximately **3.85 kg**.

### Example: 17

Two solid spheres made of the same metal have weights 5920 g and 740 g respectively. Determine the radius of the larger sphere, if the diameter of the smaller one is 5 cm.

#### Solution:

Let the density of the metal be $d$ $\mathrm{g/cm}^{3}$. Let the radius of the larger sphere be $R$ cm.
Radius of the smaller sphere, $r = \frac{5}{2} = 2.5 \mathrm{~cm}$.

The ratio of weights is equal to the ratio of volumes since the density is the same.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\text{Weight}_{large}}{\text{Weight}_{small}} &= \frac{\text{Volume}_{large} \times d}{\text{Volume}_{small} \times d} = \frac{5920}{740} = 8 \\
\frac{\frac{4}{3} \pi R^{3}}{\frac{4}{3} \pi r^{3}} &= 8 \\
\frac{R^{3}}{r^{3}} &= 8 \\
R^{3} &= 8 \times r^{3} = 8 \times \left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{3} = 8 \times \frac{125}{8} = 125 \\
R^{3} &= 5^{3} \implies R = 5 \mathrm{~cm}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the radius of the larger sphere is **5 cm**.

### Example: 18

A hemispherical tank is made up of an iron sheet 1 cm thick. If the inner radius of the tank is 1 m then find the volume of iron used in the tank.

#### Solution:

Inner radius of the tank, $r = 1 \mathrm{~m} = 100 \mathrm{~cm}$.
Thickness of the iron sheet = 1 cm.
Outer radius of the tank, $R = (100+1) \mathrm{cm} = 101 \mathrm{~cm}$.

**Volume of iron used (volume of the hemispherical shell):**
$$
\begin{aligned}
V_{iron} &= \frac{2}{3} \pi \left(R^{3}-r^{3}\right) \\
&= \frac{2}{3} \pi \times \left\{(101)^{3}-(100)^{3}\right\} \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times (1030301-1000000) \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{22}{7} \times 30301 \mathrm{cm}^{3} \\
&= \frac{1333244}{21} \mathrm{~cm}^{3} \\
&\approx 63487.81 \mathrm{~cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the volume of iron used in the tank is **63487.81 cm³**.
