## Cylinders

Solids like circular pillars, circular pipes, circular pencils, measuring jars, road rollers and gas cylinders, etc., are said to be in cylindrical shapes.

### Formulae

Let us consider a cylinder whose height is $h$ units and the radius of whose base is $r$ units. Then, we have:

- (i) **Volume of the cylinder** = $(\pi r^{2} h)$ cubic units.
- (ii) **Curved surface area of the cylinder** = $(2 \pi r h)$ sq units.
- (iii) **Total surface area of the cylinder**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{area of curved surface}) + 2(\text{base area}) \\
& = (2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^{2}) \text{ sq units.}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Hollow Cylinders

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Mensuration/Volume-and-Surface-Area-of-Solids/class-09-Ch-15-B-BooK-001.jpg)

Solids like iron pipes, rubber tubes, etc., are in the shape of hollow cylinders.

### Formulae

For a hollow cylinder with external radius $R$ units, internal radius $r$ units and height (or length) $h$ units, we have:

- (i) **Volume of material** = (exterior volume) $-$ (interior volume)
$$
= (\pi R^{2} h - \pi r^{2} h) \text{ cubic units.}
$$
- (ii) **Curved surface area** = (external surface) + (internal surface)
$$
= (2 \pi R h + 2 \pi r h) \text{ sq units.}
$$
- (iii) **Total surface area** = (curve surface) + 2(area of base ring)
$$
= \left[(2 \pi R h + 2 \pi r h) + 2(\pi R^{2} - \pi r^{2})\right] \text{ sq units.}
$$

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1

Find the (i) volume, (ii) area of curved surface and (iii) total surface area of a cylinder having radius of the base 14 cm and height 30 cm.

#### Solution:

Here, $r = 14 \text{ cm}$ and $h = 30 \text{ cm}$.

(i) **Volume of the cylinder** = $(\pi r^{2} h)$ cubic units
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times 14 \times 30\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = 18480 \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
(ii) **Curved surface area of the cylinder** = $(2 \pi r h)$ sq units
$$
= \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times 30\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = 2640 \text{ cm}^{2}.
$$
(iii) **Total surface area of the cylinder**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^{2}) \text{ sq units} = 2 \pi r(r+h) \text{ sq units} \\
& = \left\{2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times (14+30)\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} = 3872 \text{ cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 2

The total surface area of a cylinder of radius 7 cm is $880 \text{ cm}^{2}$. Find the height and the volume of the cylinder.

#### Solution:

Given, $r = 7 \text{ cm}$. Let the height of the cylinder be $h \text{ cm}$.
Then, **total surface area** = $(2 \pi r^{2} + 2 \pi r h)$ sq units
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = 2 \pi r(r+h) \text{ sq units} \\
& = 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times (7+h) \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = 44(7+h) \text{ cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\therefore \quad 44(7+h) = 880 \Rightarrow (7+h) = 20 \Rightarrow h = (20-7) = 13.
$$
Hence, the height of the cylinder is **13 cm**.

**Volume of the cylinder** = $(\pi r^{2} h)$ cubic units
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \times 13\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = 2002 \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
Hence, the volume of the cylinder is **$2002 \text{ cm}^{3}$**.

### Example: 3

The curved surface area and the volume of a pillar are $264 \text{ m}^{2}$ and $396 \text{ m}^{3}$ respectively. Find the diameter and the height of the pillar.

#### Solution:

Let the radius of the pillar be $r$ metres and its height be $h$ metres. Then,
$$
\begin{align*}
2 \pi r h & = 264 \tag{i}\\
\text{and } \pi r^{2} h & = 396 \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$
On dividing (ii) by (i), we get
$$
\frac{\pi r^{2} h}{2 \pi r h} = \frac{396}{264} \Rightarrow r = \left(\frac{2 \times 396}{264}\right) = 3 \Rightarrow 2r = 6
$$
Hence, the diameter of the pillar is **6 m**.

Putting $r=3$ in (i), we get
$$
2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 3 \times h = 264 \Rightarrow h = \left(264 \times \frac{7}{132}\right) = 14
$$
Hence, the height of the pillar is **14 m**.

### Example: 4

How many cubic metres of earth must be dug out to sink a well 14 m deep and having a radius of 4 m? If the earth taken out is spread over a plot of dimensions ($25 \text{ m} \times 16 \text{ m}$), what is the height of the platform so formed?

#### Solution:

Clearly, we have $r=4 \text{ m}$ and $h=14 \text{ m}$.

**Volume of the earth dug out of the well**
$$
= (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ cubic units} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 4 \times 4 \times 14\right) \text{ m}^{3} = 704 \text{ m}^{3}.
$$
**Area of the given plot** = $(25 \times 16) \text{ m}^{2} = 400 \text{ m}^{2}$.

**Volume of the platform formed** = volume of the earth dug out = $704 \text{ m}^{3}$.

**Height of the platform** = $\left(\frac{\text{volume in m}^{3}}{\text{area in m}^{2}}\right)$
$$
= \left(\frac{704}{400}\right) \text{ m} = \frac{176}{100} \text{ m} = 1.76 \text{ m}.
$$
Hence, the height of the platform so formed = **1.76 m**.

### Example: 5

A well of inner diameter 14 m is dug to a depth of 15 m. Earth taken out of it has been evenly spread all around it to a width of 7 m to form an embankment. Find the height of the embankment so formed.

#### Solution:

Radius of the well, $r = 7 \text{ m}$, and its depth, $h = 15 \text{ m}$.

**Volume of the earth dug out** = volume of the well
$$
= (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ cubic units} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \times 15\right) \text{ m}^{3} = 2310 \text{ m}^{3}.
$$

Width of the embankment = $7 \text{ m}$.
External radius of the embankment = $(7+7) \text{ m} = 14 \text{ m}$.
Internal radius of the embankment = $7 \text{ m}$.

**Area of the embankment** = $\pi \times [(14)^{2} - 7^{2}] \text{ m}^{2}$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \left[\frac{22}{7} \times (14+7) \times (14-7)\right] \text{ m}^{2} \\
& = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 21 \times 7\right) \text{ m}^{2} = 462 \text{ m}^{2}

**Volume of the embankment** = volume of the earth dug out = $2310 \text{ m}^{3}$.

**Height of the embankment**
$$
= \left(\frac{\text{volume of the embankment in m}^{3}}{\text{area of the embankment in m}^{2}}\right) = \left(\frac{2310}{462}\right) \text{ m} = 5 \text{ m}.
$$
Hence, the height of the embankment formed = **5 m**.

### Example: 6

The diameter of a roller, 120 cm long, is 84 cm. If it takes 500 complete revolutions to level a playground, find the cost of levelling it at ₹5 per square metre.

#### Solution:

Radius of the roller, $r = 42 \text{ cm}$, and its length, $h = 120 \text{ cm}$.

**Area covered by the roller in 1 revolution** = curved surface area of the roller
$$
= (2 \pi r h) \text{ sq units} = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 42 \times 120\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = 31680 \text{ cm}^{2}.
$$
**Area covered by the roller in 500 revolutions**
$$
= (31680 \times 500) \text{ cm}^{2} = \left(\frac{31680 \times 500}{100 \times 100}\right) \text{ m}^{2} = 1584 \text{ m}^{2}.
$$
$\therefore$ **area of the playground** = $1584 \text{ m}^{2}$.

**Cost of levelling the playground** = ₹$(1584 \times 5)$ = **₹7920**.

### Example: 7

Find the number of coins 1.5 cm in diameter and 0.2 cm thick to be melted to form a right circular cylinder of height 5 cm and diameter 4.5 cm.

#### Solution:

Each coin is cylindrical in shape.
Radius of each coin, $r = \frac{1.5}{2} \text{ cm} = 0.75 \text{ cm}$.
Thickness of each coin, $h = 0.2 \text{ cm}$.
$$
\text{Volume of each coin} = (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ cubic units} = (\pi \times 0.75 \times 0.75 \times 0.2) \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
Radius of the new cylinder formed, $R = \frac{4.5}{2} \text{ cm} = 2.25 \text{ cm}$.
Height of the new cylinder formed, $H = 5 \text{ cm}$.
$$
\text{Volume of the new cylinder formed} = \pi R^{2} H = (\pi \times 2.25 \times 2.25 \times 5) \text{ cm}^{3}
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Number of coins} & = \left(\frac{\text{volume of new cylinder}}{\text{volume of 1 coin}}\right) \\
& = \left(\frac{\pi \times 2.25 \times 2.25 \times 5}{\pi \times 0.75 \times 0.75 \times 0.2}\right) \\
& = \left(\frac{225 \times 225 \times 5 \times 10}{75 \times 75 \times 2}\right) = 225.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the number of coins required = **225**.

### Example: 8

2.2 cu dm of brass is to be drawn into a cylindrical wire of diameter 0.50 cm. Find the length of the wire.

#### Solution:

Volume of brass = $(2.2 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10) \text{ cm}^{3} = 2200 \text{ cm}^{3}$.
Radius of the wire = $0.25 \text{ cm}$.
Let the required length of the wire be $x \text{ cm}$.
Then, **volume of the wire** = $(\pi r^{2} x)$ cu units
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 0.25 \times 0.25 \times x\right) \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \frac{22}{7} \times 0.25 \times 0.25 \times x = 2200
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad x = \left(\frac{2200 \times 7}{22 \times 0.25 \times 0.25}\right) = 11200 \text{ cm} = 112 \text{ m}.
$$
Hence, the length of the wire is **112 m**.

### Example: 9

The external diameter of a lead pipe is 2.4 cm and the thickness of the lead is 2 mm. Find the weight of a pipe of length 7 m, it being given that 1 cu cm of lead weighs 10 g.

#### Solution:

Length of the pipe, $h = (7 \times 100) \text{ cm} = 700 \text{ cm}$.
External radius of the pipe, $R = 1.2 \text{ cm}$.
Internal radius of the pipe, $r = (1.2 - 0.2) \text{ cm} = 1 \text{ cm}$.

**External volume of the pipe**
$$
= (\pi R^{2} h) \text{ cu units} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 1.2 \times 1.2 \times 700\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = 3168 \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
**Internal volume of the pipe**
$$
= (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ cu units} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 1 \times 1 \times 700\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = 2200 \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
**Volume of lead** = (external volume) $-$ (internal volume)
$$
= (3168 - 2200) \text{ cm}^{3} = 968 \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
**Weight of the pipe** = $\left(\frac{968 \times 10}{1000}\right) \text{ kg} = \textbf{9.68 kg}$.

### Example: 10

The external diameter of an iron pipe is 25 cm and its length is 20 cm. If the thickness of the pipe is 1 cm, find the total surface area of the pipe.

#### Solution:

Length of the pipe, $h = 20 \text{ cm}$.
External radius of the pipe, $R = \frac{25}{2} \text{ cm} = 12.5 \text{ cm}$.
Thickness of the pipe = $1 \text{ cm}$.
Internal radius of the pipe, $r = (12.5 - 1) \text{ cm} = 11.5 \text{ cm}$.

**Total surface area of the pipe**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{external curved surface area}) + (\text{internal curved surface area}) + 2(\text{area of the base ring}) \\
& = [2 \pi R h + 2 \pi r h + 2(\pi R^{2} - \pi r^{2})] \text{ sq units} \\
& = [2 \pi h(R+r) + 2 \pi(R^{2} - r^{2})] \text{ sq units} \\
& = 2 \pi(R+r)[h+(R-r)] \text{ sq units} \\
& = 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times (12.5+11.5)(20+12.5-11.5) \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 24 \times 21\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = 3168 \text{ cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the total surface area of the pipe is **$3168 \text{ cm}^{2}$**.

### Example: 11

A cylindrical metallic pipe is 14 cm long. The difference between the outside and inside surfaces is $44 \text{ cm}^{2}$. If the pipe is made up of 99 cu cm of metal, find the outer and inner radii of the pipe.

#### Solution:

Let the outer and inner radii be $R \text{ cm}$ and $r \text{ cm}$ respectively. Also, it is given that $h = 14 \text{ cm}$. Then,
**Outside surface area** = $(2 \pi R h)$ sq units
$$
= \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times R \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = (88 R) \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
**Inside surface area** = $(2 \pi r h)$ sq units
$$
= \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times r \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = (88 r) \text{ cm}^{2}.
$$
$$
\therefore \quad (88R - 88r) = 44 \Rightarrow 88(R-r) = 44 \Rightarrow (R-r) = \frac{1}{2} \tag{i}
$$
**External volume** = $(\pi R^{2} h)$
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times R^{2} \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = (44 R^{2}) \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
**Internal volume** = $(\pi r^{2} h)$ cubic units
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times r^{2} \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = (44 r^{2}) \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
**Volume of metal** = (external volume) $-$ (internal volume)
$$
= (44 R^{2} - 44 r^{2}) \text{ cm}^{3} = 44(R^{2} - r^{2}) \text{ cm}^{3}.
$$
$$
\therefore \quad 44(R^{2} - r^{2}) = 99 \Rightarrow (R^{2} - r^{2}) = \frac{99}{44} \Rightarrow (R^{2} - r^{2}) = \frac{9}{4} \tag{ii}
$$
On dividing (ii) by (i), we get $(R+r) = \frac{9}{2}$ (iii)

On solving (i) and (iii), we get $R = \frac{5}{2}$ and $r=2$.
Hence, the outer radius = **2.5 cm** and the inner radius = **2 cm**.

### Example: 12

A solid iron rectangular block of dimensions ($2.2 \text{ m} \times 1.2 \text{ m} \times 1 \text{ m}$) is cast into a hollow cylindrical pipe of internal radius 35 cm and thickness 5 cm. Find the length of the pipe.

#### Solution:

**Volume of the iron block** = $(220 \times 120 \times 100) \text{ cm}^{3}$.
**Volume of iron in hollow pipe formed** = $(220 \times 120 \times 100) \text{ cm}^{3}$.

Internal radius of the pipe, $r = 35 \text{ cm}$.
External radius of the pipe, $R = (35+5) \text{ cm} = 40 \text{ cm}$.
Let the length of the pipe be $x \text{ cm}$.

**Volume of iron in hollow pipe** = $(\pi R^{2} h - \pi r^{2} h)$ cu units
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \pi h(R^{2} - r^{2}) \text{ cu units} \\
& = \pi h(R+r)(R-r) \text{ cu units} \\
& = \frac{22}{7} \times x \times (40+35)(40-35) \text{ cm}^{3} \\
& = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times x \times 75 \times 5\right) \text{ cm}^{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \frac{22}{7} \times x \times 75 \times 5 = 220 \times 120 \times 100
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad x = \left(\frac{220 \times 120 \times 100 \times 7}{75 \times 5 \times 22}\right) = 2240.
$$
Hence, the length of the pipe is $2240 \text{ cm} = \textbf{22.4 m}$.

### Example: 13

A copper wire of diameter 6 mm is evenly wrapped on a cylinder of length 15 cm and diameter 49 cm to cover its whole surface. Find the length and volume of the wire. If the specific gravity of copper be 9 g per cu cm, find the weight of the wire.

#### Solution:

Length of the cylinder = $15 \text{ cm}$ and its radius = $24.5 \text{ cm}$.
Diameter of the wire = $0.6 \text{ cm}$.

**Number of turns** = $\frac{\text{length of the cylinder}}{\text{diameter of the wire}} = \frac{15}{0.6} = 25$.

**Length of wire in 1 turn** = circumference of the base of cylinder
$$
= \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 24.5\right) \text{ cm} = 154 \text{ cm}.
$$
**Length of the wire in 25 turns** = $(154 \times 25) \text{ cm} = 3850 \text{ cm} = \textbf{38.5 m}$.

**Volume of the wire** = $\pi r^{2} h$
$$
= \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 0.3 \times 0.3 \times 3850\right) \text{ cu cm} = \textbf{1089 cu cm}
$$
**Weight of the wire** = $\left(\frac{1089 \times 9}{1000}\right) \text{ kg} = \textbf{9.801 kg}$.

### Example: 15

The sum of radius of the base and height of a cylinder is 37 m. If the total surface area of the cylinder is $1628 \text{ m}^{2}$, find the curved surface area and volume of the cylinder.

#### Solution:

Let the radius of the base of the given cylinder be $r$ metres and its height be $h$ metres.
Then, its **total surface area**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^{2}) \text{ m}^{2} \\
& = 2 \pi r(h+r) \text{ m}^{2} \\
& = (2 \pi r \times 37) \text{ m}^{2} \quad [\because (h+r)=37 \text{ (given)}]
\end{aligned}
$$
But, total surface area = $1628 \text{ m}^{2}$.
$$
\therefore \quad 2 \pi r \times 37 = 1628 \Rightarrow 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times r \times 37 = 1628
$$
$$
\Rightarrow r = \left(1628 \times \frac{7}{44} \times \frac{1}{37}\right) \text{ m} = 7 \text{ m}
$$
Now, $h+r = 37 \Rightarrow h+7 = 37 \Rightarrow h=30$.

$\therefore$ **Curved surface area of the cylinder**
$$
= (2 \pi r h) \text{ m}^{2} = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 30\right) \text{ m}^{2} = \textbf{1320 m}^{2}
$$
**Volume of the cylinder**
$$
= (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ m}^{3} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \times 30\right) \text{ m}^{3} = \textbf{4620 m}^{3}
$$

### Example: 16

A metal pipe is 77 cm long. The inner diameter of a cross section is 4 cm, the outer diameter being 4.4 cm. Find its
(i) inner curved surface area
(ii) outer curved surface area
(iii) total surface area.

#### Solution:

Let us denote the inner radius by $r \text{ cm}$, outer radius by $R \text{ cm}$ and length of the metal pipe by $h \text{ cm}$. Then,
$$
r=2, R=2.2 \text{ and } h=77
$$
(i) **Inner curved surface area**
$$
= (2 \pi r h) \text{ cm}^{2} = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 2 \times 77\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = \textbf{968 cm}^{2}
$$
(ii) **Outer curved surface area**
$$
= (2 \pi R h) \text{ cm}^{2} = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 2.2 \times 77\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = \textbf{1064.8 cm}^{2}.
$$
(iii) **Total surface area**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{inner curved surface area}) + (\text{outer curved surface area}) + 2(\text{area of the two base rings}) \\
& = \left\{968 + 1064.8 + 2(\pi R^{2} - \pi r^{2})\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2032.8 + 2\pi(R^{2} - r^{2})\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2032.8 + 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times (R+r)(R-r)\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2032.8 + \frac{44}{7} \times (2.2+2) \times (2.2-2)\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2032.8 + \frac{44}{7} \times 4.2 \times 0.2\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left(2032.8 + \frac{44}{7} \times \frac{42}{10} \times \frac{2}{10}\right) \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left(2032.8 + \frac{132}{25}\right) \text{ cm}^{2} = (2032.8 + 5.28) \text{ cm}^{2} = \textbf{2038.08 cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 17

Rainwater, which falls on a flat rectangular rooftop of dimensions 22 m by 10 m, is transferred into a cylindrical vessel of internal radius 50 cm through a circular pipe. A certain day recorded a rainfall of 2.5 cm. Find the (i) volume and (ii) height of the water, filled in the cylindrical vessel.

#### Solution:

(i) **Volume of water filled in the cylindrical vessel**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \text{volume of water collected on the rooftop} \\
& = \left(22 \times 10 \times \frac{2.5}{100}\right) \text{ m}^{3} = \frac{11}{2} \text{ m}^{3} = \textbf{5.5 m}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
(ii) Let the required height of water in the vessel be $h$ metres.
Radius of the vessel, $r = \frac{50}{100} \text{ m} = \frac{1}{2} \text{ m}$.
**Volume of water in the cylindrical vessel**
$$
= (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ m}^{3} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} \times h\right) \text{ m}^{3} = \frac{11h}{14} \text{ m}^{3}.
$$
$$
\therefore \quad \frac{11h}{14} = \frac{11}{2} \Rightarrow h = \left(\frac{11}{2} \times \frac{14}{11}\right) \text{ m} = 7 \text{ m}.
$$
Hence, the height of water in the cylindrical vessel is **7 m**.

### Example: 18

(i) Find the lateral, or curved, surface area of a closed cylindrical storage tank which is 4.2 m in diameter and 4.5 m high.
(ii) How much steel was actually used in it, if $\frac{1}{12}$ of the steel actually used was wasted in making the tank?

#### Solution:

(i) Radius of the cylinder, $r = \frac{4.2}{2} \text{ m} = 2.1 \text{ m}$.
Height of the cylinder, $h = 4.5 \text{ m}$.
**Curved surface area of the cylindrical tank**
$$
= (2 \pi r h) \text{ m}^{2} = \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 2.1 \times 4.5\right) \text{ m}^{2}
$$
$$
= \left(2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times \frac{21}{10} \times \frac{45}{10}\right) \text{ m}^{2} = \frac{297}{5} \text{ m}^{2} = \textbf{59.4 m}^{2}
$$
(ii) **Total surface area of the tank**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^{2}) \text{ m}^{2} = 2 \pi r(h+r) \text{ m}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 2.1 \times (4.5+2.1)\right\} \text{ m}^{2} = \left(\frac{44}{7} \times \frac{21}{10} \times \frac{66}{10}\right) \text{ m}^{2} \\
& = \frac{8712}{100} \text{ m}^{2} = 87.12 \text{ m}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Let the actual area of steel used be $x \text{ m}^{2}$.
Then, wasted steel = $\frac{x}{12} \text{ m}^{2}$.
Area of steel used in the tank = $(x - \frac{x}{12}) \text{ m}^{2} = \frac{11x}{12} \text{ m}^{2}$.
$$
\therefore \quad \frac{11x}{12} = 87.12 \Rightarrow x = \left(\frac{87.12 \times 12}{11}\right) \text{ m}^{2} = (7.92 \times 12) \text{ m}^{2} = 95.04 \text{ m}^{2}
$$
Hence, **$95.04 \text{ m}^{2}$** of steel was actually used in the tank.

### Example: 19

Find the area of the chart paper used to make the curved surface of a model of cylindrical kaleidoscope of length 25 cm and radius 3.5 cm.

#### Solution:

For the given cylindrical kaleidoscope, we have radius of the base, $r=3.5 \text{ cm}$ and height, $h=25 \text{ cm}$.

**Area of the chart paper required**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \text{curved surface area of the kaleidoscope} \\
& = (2 \pi r h) \text{ cm}^{2} \\

How much cardboard is required to make 35 penholders in the shape of cylinders, each of radius 3 cm and height 10.5 cm?

### Example: 21

A rectangular sheet of paper $44 \text{ cm} \times 20 \text{ cm}$ is rolled along its length and a cylinder is formed. Find the volume of the cylinder formed. (Take $\pi = \frac{22}{7}$)

#### Solution:

When a rectangular sheet is rolled along its length then the length of the sheet becomes the circumference of the base of the cylinder and breadth of the sheet becomes the height of the cylinder.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-09/Mensuration/Volume-and-Surface-Area-of-Solids/class-09-Ch-15-B-BooK-004.jpg)

Clearly, the height of the cylinder is $20 \text{ cm}$.
Let the radius of its base be $r \text{ cm}$.
$\therefore$ circumference of the base of the cylinder = length of the sheet
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 2 \pi r = 44
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times r = 44 \Rightarrow r = \left(44 \times \frac{7}{44}\right) = 7.
$$
Thus, $r=7 \text{ cm}$ and $h=20 \text{ cm}$.
$\therefore$ **volume of the cylinder formed**
$$
\begin{aligned}

& = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 7 \times 7 \times 20\right) \text{ cm}^{3} = \textbf{3080 cm}^{3}.

### Example: 22

The radii of two cylinders are in the ratio $2:3$ and their heights are in the ratio $5:3$. Calculate the ratio of their volumes and the ratio of their curved surfaces.

#### Solution:

Let their radii be $2R, 3R$ and their heights be $5H, 3H$.
Then,
$$
\frac{V_{1}}{V_{2}} = \frac{\pi \times (2R)^{2} \times 5H}{\pi \times (3R)^{2} \times 3H} = \frac{20}{27} \Rightarrow V_{1}:V_{2} = 20:27.
$$
and
$$
\frac{S_{1}}{S_{2}} = \frac{2\pi(2R)(5H)}{2\pi(3R)(3H)} = \frac{10}{9} \Rightarrow S_{1}:S_{2} = 10:9.
$$
Hence, their volumes are in the ratio **$20:27$** and their surface areas are in the ratio **$10:9$**.

### Example: 23

The volume of a metallic cylindrical pipe is $748 \text{ cm}^{3}$. Its length is 14 cm and its external radius is 9 cm. Find its thickness.

#### Solution:

Volume of the pipe, $V = 748 \text{ cm}^{3}$.
Length of the pipe, $h = 14 \text{ cm}$.
External radius of the pipe, $R = 9 \text{ cm}$.
Let the internal radius of the pipe be $r \text{ cm}$. Then,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{volume of the pipe} & = (\pi R^{2} h - \pi r^{2} h) \\
& = \left\{\pi (R^{2} - r^{2}) h\right\} \text{ cm}^{3} \\
& = \left\{\frac{22}{7} \times (81 - r^{2}) \times 14\right\} \text{ cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
But, volume of the pipe = $748 \text{ cm}^{3}$.
$$
\therefore \quad \frac{22}{7} \times (81 - r^{2}) \times 14 = 748
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 44 \times (81 - r^{2}) = 748
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad (81 - r^{2}) = \frac{748}{44} = 17 \Rightarrow r^{2} = (81-17) = 64 = 8^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow r = 8 \text{ cm}.
$$
$\therefore$ **thickness** = $(R-r) \text{ cm} = (9-8) \text{ cm} = \textbf{1 cm}$.

### Example: 24

A lead pencil consists of a cylinder of wood with a solid cylinder of graphite filled into it. The diameter of the pencil is 7 mm, the diameter of the graphite is 1 mm and the length of the pencil is 14 cm. Find the (i) volume of the graphite, (ii) volume of the wood, (iii) weight of the whole pencil, if the specific gravity of wood is $0.7 \text{ g/cm}^{3}$ and that of the graphite is $2.1 \text{ g/cm}^{3}$.

#### Solution:

(i) Diameter of the graphite cylinder = $1 \text{ mm} = \frac{1}{10} \text{ cm}$.
Radius of the graphite cylinder = $\left(\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{10}\right) \text{ cm} = \frac{1}{20} \text{ cm}$.
Length of the graphite cylinder = $14 \text{ cm}$.
**Volume of the graphite cylinder**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\pi r^{2} h) \text{ cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times \frac{1}{20} \times \frac{1}{20} \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{3} \\
& = \frac{11}{100} \text{ cm}^{3} = \textbf{0.11 cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
(ii) Diameter of the pencil = $7 \text{ mm} = \frac{7}{10} \text{ cm}$.
Radius of the pencil = $\left(\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{7}{10}\right) \text{ cm} = \frac{7}{20} \text{ cm}$.
Length of the pencil = $14 \text{ cm}$.
**Volume of the pencil**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\pi R^{2} h) \text{ cm}^{3} = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times \frac{7}{20} \times \frac{7}{20} \times 14\right) \text{ cm}^{3} \\
& = \frac{539}{100} \text{ cm}^{3} = 5.39 \text{ cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore$ **volume of wood in the pencil**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{volume of the pencil}) - (\text{volume of graphite}) \\
& = (5.39 - 0.11) \text{ cm}^{3} = \textbf{5.28 cm}^{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
(iii) **Weight of the pencil**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (\text{volume of wood}) \times (\text{specific gravity of wood}) + (\text{volume of graphite}) \times (\text{specific gravity of graphite}) \\
& = (5.28 \times 0.7) \text{ g} + (0.11 \times 2.1) \text{ g} \\
& = (3.696 + 0.231) \text{ g} = \textbf{3.927 g}.
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 25

30 circular plates, each of radius 14 cm and thickness 3 cm, are placed one above the other to form a cylindrical solid. Find (i) the total surface area, (ii) volume of the cylinder so formed.

#### Solution:

Clearly, the cylinder formed has base radius, $r=14 \text{ cm}$ and height, $h=(3 \times 30) \text{ cm} = 90 \text{ cm}$.

(i) **Total surface area of the cylinder formed**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = (2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^{2}) = 2 \pi r(h+r) \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = \left\{2 \times \frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times (90+14)\right\} \text{ cm}^{2} \\
& = (88 \times 104) \text{ cm}^{2} = \textbf{9152 cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
(ii) **Volume of the cylinder formed**
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \pi r^{2} h = \left(\frac{22}{7} \times 14 \times 14 \times 90\right) \text{ cm}^{3} \\
& = \textbf{55440 cm}^{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$
