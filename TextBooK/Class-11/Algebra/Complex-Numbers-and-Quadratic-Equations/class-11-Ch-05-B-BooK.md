## Complex Numbers

The numbers of the form **$(a+i b)$**, where **$a$** and **$b$** are real numbers and **$i=\sqrt{-1}$**, are known as **complex numbers**. The set of all complex numbers is denoted by **$C$**.

$$
C=\{(a+i b): a, b \in R\}
$$

**Examples**: Each of the numbers $(5+8 i)$, $(-3+\sqrt{2} i)$ and $(\frac{2}{3}-\frac{5}{7} i)$ is a complex number.

For a complex number, $z=(a+i b)$, we have:
- **$a$** = **real part** of $z$, written as $\operatorname{Re}(z)$
- **$b$** = **imaginary part** of $z$, written as $\operatorname{Im}(z)$

**Examples**:
- (i) If $z=(5+9 i)$ then $\operatorname{Re}(z)=5$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z)=9$.
- (ii) If $z=(\frac{2}{3}-3 i)$ then $\operatorname{Re}(z)=\frac{2}{3}$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z)=-3$.
- (iii) If $z=(-7+\frac{5}{8} i)$ then $\operatorname{Re}(z)=-7$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z)=\frac{5}{8}$.

---

## Purely Real and Purely Imaginary Numbers

A complex number $z$ is said to be:
- (i) **purely real**, if $\operatorname{Im}(z)=0$.
- (ii) **purely imaginary**, if $\operatorname{Re}(z)=0$.

Thus, each of the numbers $2, -7, \sqrt{3}$ is **purely real**.
And, each of the numbers $2 i, (\sqrt{3} i), (\frac{-3}{2} i)$ is **purely imaginary**.

---

## Conjugate of a Complex Number

The **conjugate** of a complex number $z=(a+i b)$ is defined as **$\bar{z}=(a-i b)$**.

**Examples**:
- (i) $\overline{(3+8 i)}=(3-8 i)$
- (ii) $\overline{(-6-2 i)}=(-6+2 i)$
- (iii) $\overline{-3}=-3$.

---

## Modulus of a Complex Number

The **modulus** of a complex number $z=(a+i b)$, denoted by **$|z|$**, is defined as **$|z|=\sqrt{a^{2}+b^{2}}$**.

**Examples**:
- (i) If $z=(2+3 i)$ then $|z|=\sqrt{2^{2}+3^{2}}=\sqrt{13}$.
- (ii) If $z=(-5-4 i)$ then $|z|=\sqrt{(-5)^{2}+(-4)^{2}}=\sqrt{41}$.

---

## Equality of Complex Numbers

If $z_{1}=a_{1}+i b_{1}$ and $z_{2}=a_{2}+i b_{2}$ then:

$$
z_{1}=z_{2} \Leftrightarrow a_{1}=a_{2} \text { and } b_{1}=b_{2}
$$

### Example:

If $2 y+(3 x-y) i=(5-2 i)$, find the values of $x$ and $y$.

#### Solution:

Equating the real and imaginary parts, we get:

$$
2 y+(3 x-y) i=(5-2 i)
$$
$$
\Leftrightarrow \quad 2 y=5 \quad \text { and } \quad 3 x-y=-2
$$
$$
\Leftrightarrow \quad y=\frac{5}{2} \quad \text { and } \quad 3 x-\frac{5}{2}=-2
$$
$$
\Leftrightarrow \quad y=\frac{5}{2} \quad \text { and } \quad x=\frac{1}{6}
$$

Hence, $x=\frac{1}{6}$ and $y=\frac{5}{2}$.

## Sum and Difference of Complex Numbers

If $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ then we define:
- (i) $z_{1}+z_{2}=(a_{1}+a_{2})+i(b_{1}+b_{2})$
- (ii) $z_{1}-z_{2}=(a_{1}-a_{2})+i(b_{1}-b_{2})$

**Examples**:
- (i) If $z_{1}=(3+5 i)$ and $z_{2}=(-5+2 i)$ then:
$$
z_{1}+z_{2}=\{3+(-5)\}+i\{5+2\}=(-2+7 i)
$$
and
$$
z_{1}-z_{2}=\{3-(-5)\}+i(5-2)=(8+3 i)
$$
- (ii) If $z_{1}=(-6-2 i)$ and $z_{2}=(-3-5 i)$ then:
$$
z_{1}+z_{2} = \{(-6)+(-3)\}+i\{(-2)+(-5)\}=(-9-7 i)
$$
and
$$
z_{1}-z_{2} = \{-6-(-3)\}+i\{(-2)-(-5)\}=(-3+3 i)
$$

**Remarks**:
- (i) $\operatorname{Re}(z_{1}+z_{2})=\operatorname{Re}(z_{1})+\operatorname{Re}(z_{2})$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z_{1}+z_{2})=\operatorname{Im}(z_{1})+\operatorname{Im}(z_{2})$.
- (ii) $\operatorname{Re}(z_{1}-z_{2})=\operatorname{Re}(z_{1})-\operatorname{Re}(z_{2})$ and $\operatorname{Im}(z_{1}-z_{2})=\operatorname{Im}(z_{1})-\operatorname{Im}(z_{2})$.

## Properties of Addition of Complex Numbers

### (i) Closure Property

The sum of two complex numbers is always a complex number.

Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ be any two complex numbers.
Then, $z_{1}+z_{2}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})+(a_{2}+i b_{2}) = (a_{1}+a_{2})+i(b_{1}+b_{2})$, which is a complex number.

Thus, if $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ are any two complex numbers then $(z_{1}+z_{2})$ is also a complex number.

### (ii) Commutative Law

For any two complex numbers $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$, prove that:

$$
z_{1}+z_{2}=z_{2}+z_{1}
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$, where $a_{1}, a_{2}, b_{1}, b_{2}$ are real numbers.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad z_{1}+z_{2} & =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)+\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(a_{1}+a_{2}\right)+i\left(b_{1}+b_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(a_{2}+a_{1}\right)+i\left(b_{2}+b_{1}\right) \quad [\text { by commutative law of addition in } R] \\
& =\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)+\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)=z_{2}+z_{1}
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, $z_{1}+z_{2}=z_{2}+z_{1}$ for all $z_{1}, z_{2} \in C$.
Hence, addition of complex numbers is **commutative**.

### (iii) Associative Law

For any complex numbers $z_{1}, z_{2}$ and $z_{3}$, prove that:

$$
\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)+z_{3}=z_{1}+\left(z_{2}+z_{3}\right)
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$, $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ and $z_{3}=(a_{3}+i b_{3})$, where $a_{1}, a_{2}, a_{3}$ and $b_{1}, b_{2}, b_{3}$ are all real numbers.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)+z_{3} & =\left\{\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)+\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)\right\}+\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right) \\
& =\left\{\left(a_{1}+a_{2}\right)+i\left(b_{1}+b_{2}\right)\right\}+\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right) \\
& =\left\{\left(a_{1}+a_{2}\right)+a_{3}\right\}+i\left\{\left(b_{1}+b_{2}\right)+b_{3}\right\} \\
& =\left\{a_{1}+\left(a_{2}+a_{3}\right)\right\}+i\left\{b_{1}+\left(b_{2}+b_{3}\right)\right\} \quad [\text{by associative law of addition in } R] \\
& =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)+\left\{\left(a_{2}+a_{3}\right)+i\left(b_{2}+b_{3}\right)\right\} \\
& =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)+\left\{\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)+\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right)\right\} \\
& =z_{1}+\left(z_{2}+z_{3}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore (z_{1}+z_{2})+z_{3} = z_{1}+(z_{2}+z_{3})$ for all $z_{1}, z_{2}, z_{3} \in C$.
Hence, addition of complex numbers is **associative**.

### (iv) Existence of Additive Identity

For any complex number $z$, prove that:

$$
z+0=0+z=z
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z=(a+i b)$ and we may write, $0=(0+i 0)$. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
z+0 & =(a+i b)+(0+i 0)=(a+0)+i(b+0)=(a+i b) \\
\text{and } 0+z & =(0+i 0)+(a+i b)=(0+a)+i(0+b)=(a+i b)
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore z+0 = 0+z=z$ for all values of $z \in C$.
Thus, **0** is the **additive identity** for complex numbers.

### (v) Existence of Additive Inverse

For every complex number $z$, prove that:

$$
z+(-z)=(-z)+z=0
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z=(a+i b)$. Then, $(-z)=(-a)+i(-b)$.

$$
\therefore \quad z+(-z) = (a+i b)+\{(-a)+i(-b)\} = \{a+(-a)\}+i\{b+(-b)\}=(0+i 0)
$$

Similarly, $(-z)+z=(0+i 0)=0$.
Hence, $z+(-z)=(-z)+z=0$.
Thus, every complex number $z$ has **$(-z)$** as its **additive inverse**.

## Multiplication of Complex Numbers

Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$. Then, we define:

$$
z_{1} z_{2} = (a_{1}+i b_{1})(a_{2}+i b_{2})=(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2})+i(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2})
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad z_{1} z_{2}= & \left\{\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1}\right) \cdot \operatorname{Re}\left(z_{2}\right)-\operatorname{Im}\left(z_{1}\right) \cdot \operatorname{Im}\left(z_{2}\right)\right\} \\
& +i\left\{\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{1}\right) \cdot \operatorname{Im}\left(z_{2}\right)+\operatorname{Re}\left(z_{2}\right) \cdot \operatorname{Im}\left(z_{1}\right)\right\}
\end{aligned}
$$

**Examples**:
- (i) Let $z_{1}=(3+2 i)$ and $z_{2}=(5+4 i)$. Then,
$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1} z_{2} & =\{(3 \times 5)-(2 \times 4)\}+i\{(3 \times 4)+(2 \times 5)\} \\
& =(15-8)+i(12+10)=(7+22 i)
\end{aligned}
$$
- (ii) Let $z_{1}=(-2+3 i)$ and $z_{2}=(7-5 i)$. Then,
$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1} z_{2} & =\{(-2) \times 7-3 \times(-5)\}+i\{(-2) \times(-5)+3 \times 7\} \\
& =(-14+15)+i(10+21)=(1+31 i)
\end{aligned}
$$

## Properties of Multiplication of Complex Numbers

### (i) Closure Property

The product of two complex numbers is always a complex number.

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ be any two complex numbers. Then, $a_{1}, a_{2}$ and $b_{1}, b_{2}$ are real numbers.

$$
\therefore \quad z_{1} z_{2} = (a_{1}+i b_{1})(a_{2}+i b_{2}) = (a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2})+i(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}), \text { which is a complex number. }
$$

Thus, the product of two complex numbers is always a complex number.

### (ii) Commutative Law

For any two complex numbers $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$, prove that:

$$
z_{1} z_{2}=z_{2} z_{1}
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1})$ and $z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
z_{1} z_{2} & =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)=\left(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2}\right)+i\left(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(a_{2} a_{1}-b_{2} b_{1}\right)+i\left(b_{2} a_{1}+a_{2} b_{1}\right) \quad [\text{by commutativity of multiplication on } R] \\
& =\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)=z_{2} z_{1}
\end{aligned}
$$

$\therefore z_{1} z_{2}=z_{2} z_{1}$ for all $z_{1}, z_{2} \in C$.
Hence, multiplication of complex numbers is **commutative**.

### (iii) Associative Law

For any three complex numbers $z_{1}, z_{2}$ and $z_{3}$, prove that:

$$
\left(z_{1} z_{2}\right) z_{3}=z_{1}\left(z_{2} z_{3}\right)
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1}), z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ and $z_{3}=(a_{3}+i b_{3})$ be any three complex numbers. Then $a_{1}, a_{2}, a_{3}$ and $b_{1}, b_{2}, b_{3}$ are all real numbers.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad\left(z_{1} z_{2}\right) z_{3} & =\left\{\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)\right\}\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right) \\
& =\left\{\left(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2}\right)+i\left(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}\right)\right\}\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right) \\
& =\left\{\left(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2}\right) a_{3}-\left(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}\right) b_{3}\right\} + i\left\{\left(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2}\right) b_{3}+\left(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}\right) a_{3}\right\} \\
& =\left\{a_{1}\left(a_{2} a_{3}-b_{2} b_{3}\right)-b_{1}\left(a_{2} b_{3}+b_{2} a_{3}\right)\right\} + i\left\{a_{1}\left(a_{2} b_{3}+a_{3} b_{2}\right)+b_{1}\left(a_{2} a_{3}-b_{2} b_{3}\right)\right\} \\
& =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left\{\left(a_{2} a_{3}-b_{2} b_{3}\right)+i\left(a_{2} b_{3}+a_{3} b_{2}\right)\right\} \\
& =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left\{\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right)\right\}=z_{1}\left(z_{2} z_{3}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, $(z_{1} z_{2}) z_{3}=z_{1}(z_{2} z_{3})$ for all $z_{1}, z_{2}, z_{3} \in C$.
Hence, multiplication of complex numbers is **associative**.

### (iv) Existence of Multiplicative Identity

The complex number $(1+i 0)$ is the **multiplicative identity** in $C$.
Let $z=(a+i b)$. Then,

$$
z \times 1=(a+i b)(1+i 0)=\{(a \times 1)-(b \times 0)\}+i\{(a \times 0)+(b \times 1)\}=(a+i b)=z
$$

Similarly, $1 \times z=z$.
Thus, $(z \times 1)=(1 \times z)=z$ for all $z \in C$.
Hence, the complex number **$1=(1+i 0)$** is the multiplicative identity.

### (v) Existence of Multiplicative Inverse

Let $z=(a+i b)$. Then,

$$
z^{-1}=\frac{1}{z}=\frac{1}{(a+i b)}=\frac{1}{(a+i b)} \times \frac{(a-i b)}{(a-i b)}=\frac{(a-i b)}{\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right)}
$$

Clearly, $z \times z^{-1}=z^{-1} \times z=1$.
Thus, every $z=(a+i b)$ has its **multiplicative inverse**, given by:

$$
z^{-1} = \frac{1}{z}=\frac{(a-i b)}{\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right)}=\frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^{2}}
$$

**Remember**: $z^{-1}=\frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^{2}}$
$\therefore \quad z \bar{z}=|z|^{2}$.

**Things to Remember**
- (i) $z=(a+i b) \Rightarrow \bar{z}=(a-i b)$ and $|z|^{2}=(a^{2}+b^{2})$.
- (ii) $z=(a+i b) \Rightarrow z^{-1}=\frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^{2}}=\frac{(a-i b)}{\left(a^{2}+b^{2}\right)}$.

### (vi) Distributive Laws

For complex numbers $z_{1}, z_{2}, z_{3}$, prove that:

$$
z_{1} \cdot\left(z_{2}+z_{3}\right)=z_{1} z_{2}+z_{1} z_{3}
$$
$$
\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) \cdot z_{3}=z_{1} z_{3}+z_{2} z_{3}
$$

**PROOF**: Let $z_{1}=(a_{1}+i b_{1}), z_{2}=(a_{2}+i b_{2})$ and $z_{3}=(a_{3}+i b_{3})$ be any three complex numbers. Then, $a_{1}, a_{2}, a_{3}$ and $b_{1}, b_{2}, b_{3}$ are real numbers.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad z_{1} \cdot\left(z_{2}+z_{3}\right) & =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left[\left(a_{2}+i b_{2}\right)+\left(a_{3}+i b_{3}\right)\right] \\
& =\left(a_{1}+i b_{1}\right)\left[\left(a_{2}+a_{3}\right)+i\left(b_{2}+b_{3}\right)\right] \\
& =\left\{a_{1}\left(a_{2}+a_{3}\right)-b_{1}\left(b_{2}+b_{3}\right)\right\}+i\left\{a_{1}\left(b_{2}+b_{3}\right)+b_{1}\left(a_{2}+a_{3}\right)\right\} \\
& =\left\{\left(a_{1} a_{2}-b_{1} b_{2}\right)+i\left(a_{1} b_{2}+b_{1} a_{2}\right)\right\}+\left\{\left(a_{1} a_{3}-b_{1} b_{3}\right)+i\left(a_{1} b_{3}+b_{1} a_{3}\right)\right\} \\
& =z_{1} z_{2}+z_{1} z_{3}
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, $z_{1}(z_{2}+z_{3})=z_{1} z_{2}+z_{1} z_{3}$ for all $z_{1}, z_{2}, z_{3} \in C$.
Similarly, we can prove that:
$$
\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) z_{3}=z_{1} z_{3}+z_{2} z_{3}
$$

## Division of Two Complex Numbers

Let $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$ be complex numbers such that $z_{2} \neq 0$.
Then,
$$
\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=z_{1} \cdot \frac{1}{z_{2}}=z_{1} z_{2}^{-1}
$$

### Example:

Find $\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}$, when $z_{1}=(6+3 i)$ and $z_{2}=(3-i)$.

#### Solution:

We have $\frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=z_{1} z_{2}^{-1}$.

Now, $z_{2}^{-1}=\frac{\bar{z}_{2}}{|z_{2}|^{2}}=\frac{\overline{(3-i)}}{|3-i|^{2}}=\frac{(3+i)}{\{3^{2}+(-1)^{2}\}}=\frac{(3+i)}{10}$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \frac{z_{1}}{z_{2}}=z_{1} \cdot z_{2}^{-1} & =(6+3 i) \cdot \frac{(3+i)}{10}=\frac{(6+3 i)(3+i)}{10} \\
& =\frac{15+15 i}{10}=\frac{15(1+i)}{10}=\frac{3(1+i)}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$

## Some Identities on Complex Numbers

**THEOREM 1**: For any complex numbers $z_{1}$ and $z_{2}$, prove that:
- (i) $(z_{1}+z_{2})^{2}=z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}$
- (ii) $(z_{1}-z_{2})^{2}=z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}$
- (iii) $(z_{1}^{2}-z_{2}^{2})=(z_{1}+z_{2})(z_{1}-z_{2})$
- (iv) $(z_{1}+z_{2})^{3}=z_{1}^{3}+z_{2}^{3}+3 z_{1} z_{2}(z_{1}+z_{2})$
- (v) $(z_{1}-z_{2})^{3}=z_{1}^{3}-z_{2}^{3}-3 z_{1} z_{2}(z_{1}-z_{2})$

**PROOF**:
- (i)
$$
\begin{aligned}
(z_{1}+z_{2})^{2} & =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) z_{1}+\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) z_{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}+z_{2} z_{1}+z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2} \quad [\because z_{2} z_{1}=z_{1} z_{2}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore (z_{1}+z_{2})^{2}=z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}$

- (ii)
$$
\begin{aligned}
(z_{1}-z_{2})^{2} & =\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right) z_{1}-\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right) z_{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}-z_{2} z_{1}-z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2} \quad [\because z_{2} z_{1}=z_{1} z_{2}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore (z_{1}-z_{2})^{2}=z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}$

- (iii)
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{RHS} & =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) z_{1}-\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) z_{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{2}+z_{2} z_{1}-z_{1} z_{2}-z_{2}^{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =(z_{1}^{2}-z_{2}^{2}) = \text{LHS} \quad [\because z_{2} z_{1}=z_{1} z_{2}]
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore (z_{1}^{2}-z_{2}^{2})=(z_{1}+z_{2})(z_{1}-z_{2})$

- (iv)
$$
\begin{aligned}
(z_{1}+z_{2})^{3} & =\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right)^{2} \cdot\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}\right)\left(z_{1}+z_{2}\right) \quad [\text{expanding } (z_{1}+z_{2})^{2}] \\
& =\left(z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}\right) z_{1}+\left(z_{1}^{2}+z_{2}^{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}\right) z_{2} \quad [\text{by distributive law}] \\
& =z_{1}^{3}+z_{2}^{2} z_{1}+2 z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+z_{2}^{3}+2 z_{1} z_{2}^{2} \\
& =z_{1}^{3}+z_{2}^{3}+3 z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+3 z_{1} z_{2}^{2}=z_{1}^{3}+z_{2}^{3}+3 z_{1} z_{2}(z_{1}+z_{2})
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore (z_{1}+z_{2})^{3}=z_{1}^{3}+z_{2}^{3}+3 z_{1} z_{2}(z_{1}+z_{2})$

- (v)
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{LHS} & =\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)^{3} \\
& =\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)^{2}\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right)=\left(z_{1}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2}\right)\left(z_{1}-z_{2}\right) \\
& =\left(z_{1}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2}\right) z_{1}-\left(z_{1}^{2}-2 z_{1} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2}\right) z_{2} \\
& =z_{1}^{3}-2 z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+z_{2}^{2} z_{1}-z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+2 z_{1} z_{2}^{2}-z_{2}^{3} \\
& =z_{1}^{3}-3 z_{1}^{2} z_{2}+3 z_{1} z_{2}^{2}-z_{2}^{3} = \text{RHS}
\end{aligned}
$$
This may be written as $(z_{1}-z_{2})^{3}=z_{1}^{3}-z_{2}^{3}-3 z_{1} z_{2}(z_{1}-z_{2})$.

Simplify:
