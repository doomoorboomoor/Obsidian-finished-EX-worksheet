## COEFFICIENT OF VARIATION

Let the mean and standard deviation of the given data be $\bar{x}$ and $\sigma$ respectively. Then, the **coefficient of variation** is defined as:

$$
\mathrm{CV}=\left(\frac{\sigma}{\bar{x}} \times 100\right), \text{ where } \bar{x} \neq 0.
$$

For comparing the dispersion of two series, we calculate the coefficient of variation for each series.

The series whose **CV is more**, is **more variable** than the other.

---

## COMPARISON OF TWO FREQUENCY DISTRIBUTIONS WITH SAME MEAN

Let there be two frequency distributions with the same mean $\bar{x}$ and having standard deviations $\sigma_{1}$ and $\sigma_{2}$ respectively.

Then, CV for the 1st distribution is $\left(\frac{\sigma_{1}}{\bar{x}} \times 100\right)\%$.
And, CV for the 2nd distribution is $\left(\frac{\sigma_{2}}{\bar{x}} \times 100\right)\%$.

Thus, $\frac{CV_{1}}{CV_{2}}=\frac{\sigma_{1}}{\sigma_{2}}$, where $CV_{1}$ and $CV_{2}$ denote the CV for the 1st and 2nd distributions respectively.

Thus, in two series having the same mean, the one having **more standard deviation is more variable**.

---

### Example: 1

The following results show the number of workers and the wages paid to them in two factories $A$ and $B$.

| Factory | $\boldsymbol{A}$ | $\boldsymbol{B}$ |
| :--- | :---: | :---: |
| Number of workers | 4000 | 5000 |
| Mean wages | Rs 3500 | Rs 3500 |
| Variance of distribution <br> of wages | 64 | 81 |

Which factory has more variation in wages?

#### Solution:

We have:
- Variance of distribution of wages in factory $A$, $\sigma_{1}^{2}=64$.
- Variance of distribution of wages in factory $B$, $\sigma_{2}^{2}=81$.

$\therefore \quad (\sigma_{1}^{2}=64 \text{ and } \sigma_{2}^{2}=81) \Rightarrow (\sigma_{1}=8 \text{ and } \sigma_{2}=9)$.

Thus, the SD of the distribution of wages in factory $A$ is **8**.
And, the SD of the distribution of wages in factory $B$ is **9**.

Since the monthly mean wages in the two factories are the same and $\sigma_{1}<\sigma_{2}$, factory **B** has more variation in wages.

### Example: 2

Coefficient of variation of two distributions are $60\%$ and $75\%$, and their standard deviations are 18 and 15 respectively. Find their arithmetic means.

#### Solution:

Given: $\mathrm{CV}_{1}=60$, $\mathrm{CV}_{2}=75$, $\sigma_{1}=18$ and $\sigma_{2}=15$.

Let $\bar{x}_{1}$ and $\bar{x}_{2}$ be the means of the 1st and 2nd distribution respectively. Then,

For the first distribution:
$$
\mathrm{CV}_{1}=\frac{\sigma_{1}}{\bar{x}_{1}} \times 100 \Rightarrow \bar{x}_{1}=\frac{\sigma_{1} \times 100}{\mathrm{CV}_{1}}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \bar{x}_{1}=\frac{18 \times 100}{60}=30
$$

For the second distribution:
$$
\mathrm{CV}_{2}=\frac{\sigma_{2}}{\bar{x}_{2}} \times 100 \Rightarrow \bar{x}_{2}=\frac{\sigma_{2} \times 100}{\mathrm{CV}_{2}}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \bar{x}_{2}=\frac{15 \times 100}{75}=20
$$

Hence, $\bar{x}_{1}=\mathbf{30}$ and $\bar{x}_{2}=\mathbf{20}$.

### Example: 3

The mean and variance of the heights and weights of the students of a class are given below.

| | **Height** | **Weight** |
| :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Mean** | 160 cm | 50.4 kg |
| **Variance** | $116.64 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}$ | $17.64 \mathrm{~kg}^{2}$ |

Show that the weights are more variable than heights.

#### Solution:

For **height**, we have:
- Variance, $\sigma^{2}=116.64 \mathrm{~cm}^{2} \Rightarrow \mathrm{SD}, \sigma=\sqrt{116.64} \mathrm{~cm}=10.8 \mathrm{~cm}$.
- Mean height $=160 \mathrm{~cm}$.

$\therefore \quad \mathrm{CV}_{1}=\left(\frac{\mathrm{SD}}{\text {mean}} \times 100\right) \%=\left(\frac{10.8}{160} \times 100\right) \% = 6.75 \%$

For **weight**, we have:
- Variance, $\sigma^{2}=17.64 \mathrm{~kg}^{2} \Rightarrow \mathrm{SD}, \sigma=\sqrt{17.64} \mathrm{~kg}=4.2 \mathrm{~kg}$.
- Mean weight $=50.4 \mathrm{~kg}$.

$\therefore \quad \mathrm{CV}_{2}=\left(\frac{\mathrm{SD}}{\text {mean}} \times 100\right) \%=\left(\frac{4.2}{50.4} \times 100\right) \%=\frac{25}{3} \% \approx 8.33 \%$

Clearly, (CV in weights) > (CV in heights).

Hence, **weights are more variable than heights**.
