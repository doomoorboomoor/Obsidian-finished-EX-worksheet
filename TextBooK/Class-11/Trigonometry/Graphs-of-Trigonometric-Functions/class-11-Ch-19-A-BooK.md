## Graph of $y = \sin x$

We know that $\sin x$ is a **periodic function** with a **period** of $2\pi$. So, we first draw the graph of $\sin x$ in the interval $[0, 2\pi]$. We can then extend it by repeating the pattern over intervals of length $2\pi$.

For this, we construct the table given below.

| $x$ | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ | $\pi$ | $\frac{7\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{4\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{11\pi}{6}$ | $2\pi$ |
| :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **$\sin x$** | $0$ | $\frac{1}{2}$<br>$=0.5$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=0.87$ | $1$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=0.87$ | $\frac{1}{2}$<br>$=0.5$ | $0$ | $-\frac{1}{2}$<br>$=-0.5$ | $-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=-0.87$ | $-1$ | $-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=-0.87$ | $-\frac{1}{2}$<br>$=-0.5$ | $0$ |

On a graph paper, we take $1 \text{ cm}$ as $\frac{\pi}{6}$ units on the **$x$-axis**, and $5 \text{ cm}$ as $1$ unit on the **$y$-axis**.

Taking the values of $x$ along the $x$-axis and the corresponding values of $\sin x$ along the $y$-axis, we plot the points $(0, 0)$, $(\frac{\pi}{6}, 0.5)$, $(\frac{\pi}{3}, 0.87)$, $(\frac{\pi}{2}, 1)$, $(\frac{2\pi}{3}, 0.87)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{6}, 0.5)$, $(\pi, 0)$, $(\frac{7\pi}{6}, -0.5)$, $(\frac{4\pi}{3}, -0.87)$, $(\frac{3\pi}{2}, -1)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{3}, -0.87)$, $(\frac{11\pi}{6}, -0.5)$, and $(2\pi, 0)$.

We join these points by a freehand curve to obtain the graph of $y = \sin x$, as shown below.

## Graph of $y = \tan x$

We know that $y = \tan x$ is defined for all real values of $x$, except when $x$ is an odd multiple of $\frac{\pi}{2}$. Also, $\tan x$ is a **periodic function** with a **period** of $\pi$. So, we draw the graph of $y = \tan x$ in $[0, \pi]$ and then extend it over other intervals.

As $x$ increases from $0$ to $\frac{\pi}{2}$, $\tan x$ increases from $0$ to $\infty$. As $x$ crosses the value of $\frac{\pi}{2}$, $\tan x$ becomes negative and is arbitrarily large in magnitude. As $x$ approaches $\pi$, $\tan x$ approaches $0$.

Now, we can construct the table, given below.

| $x$ | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ | $\pi$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$\tan x$** | $0$ | $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$<br>$=0.58$ | $1$ | $\sqrt{3}$<br>$=1.73$ | $\infty$ | $-\sqrt{3}$<br>$=-1.73$ | $-1$ | $-\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}$<br>$=-0.58$ | $0$ |

On a graph paper, we take $1 \text{ cm}$ as $\frac{\pi}{6}$ units on the **$x$-axis**, and $2 \text{ cm}$ as $1$ unit on the **$y$-axis**.

Taking the values of $x$ on the $x$-axis and the values of $\tan x$ along the $y$-axis, we plot the points $(0, 0)$, $(\frac{\pi}{6}, 0.58)$, $(\frac{\pi}{4}, 1)$, $(\frac{\pi}{3}, 1.73)$, $(\frac{2\pi}{3}, -1.73)$, $(\frac{3\pi}{4}, -1)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{6}, -0.58)$ and $(\pi, 0)$.

We join these points by a freehand curve and obtain the graph of $\tan x$ as shown below.

## Graph of $y = a \sin bx$

We know that:
$$
-1 \leq \sin bx \leq 1
$$
$$
\therefore -a \leq a \sin bx \leq a
$$
Also, $\sin x$ is periodic with period $2\pi$. Therefore, $\sin bx$ is periodic with **period** $\frac{2\pi}{b}$. Hence, $a \sin bx$ is periodic with **period** $\frac{2\pi}{b}$.

### Example:

Draw the graph of $y = 3 \sin 2x$.

#### Solution:

The range of the function is given by:
$$
-1 \leq \sin 2x \leq 1 \implies -3 \leq 3 \sin 2x \leq 3
$$
Also, $3 \sin 2x$ is periodic with **period** $\frac{2\pi}{2} = \pi$. So, we draw the graph of $y = 3 \sin 2x$ in the interval $[0, \pi]$.

We thus prepare the table given below.

| $x$ | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ | $\pi$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$2x$** | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\pi$ | $\frac{4\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{3}$ | $2\pi$ |
| **$\sin 2x$** | $0$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=0.87$ | $1$ | $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=0.87$ | $0$ | $-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=-0.87$ | $-1$ | $-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$<br>$=-0.87$ | $0$ |
| **$3 \sin 2x$** | $0$ | $2.61$ | $3$ | $2.61$ | $0$ | $-2.61$ | $-3$ | $-2.61$ | $0$ |

On a graph paper, we take $4 \text{ cm} = \frac{\pi}{3}$ units on the **$x$-axis** and $1 \text{ cm} = 1$ unit on the **$y$-axis**.

Now, we plot the points $(0, 0)$, $(\frac{\pi}{6}, 2.61)$, $(\frac{\pi}{4}, 3)$, $(\frac{\pi}{3}, 2.61)$, $(\frac{\pi}{2}, 0)$, $(\frac{2\pi}{3}, -2.61)$, $(\frac{3\pi}{4}, -3)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{6}, -2.61)$, and $(\pi, 0)$.

Join these points with a freehand curve to obtain the graph of $y = 3 \sin 2x$, given below.

### Example:

Draw the graphs of $y = \cos x$ and $y = \cos \frac{1}{2}x$ in $[0, 2\pi]$ on the same scale.

#### Solution:

We know that:
- $y = \cos x$ is a periodic function with **period** $2\pi$ and **amplitude** $1$.
- $y = \cos \frac{1}{2}x$ is a periodic function with **period** $4\pi$ and **amplitude** $1$.

We may prepare the table as under:

| $x$ | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ | $\pi$ | $\frac{7\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{4\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{11\pi}{6}$ | $2\pi$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **$\cos x$** | $1$ | $0.87$ | $0.5$ | $0$ | $-0.5$ | $-0.87$ | $-1$ | $-0.87$ | $-0.5$ | $0$ | $0.5$ | $0.87$ | $1$ |
| **$\frac{1}{2} x$** | $0$ | $\frac{\pi}{12}$ | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{12}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\frac{7\pi}{12}$ | $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{3\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{5\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{11\pi}{12}$ | $\pi$ |
| **$\cos \frac{1}{2} x$** | $1$ | $0.97$ | $0.87$ | $0.7$ | $0.5$ | $0.26$ | $0$ | $-0.26$ | $-0.5$ | $-0.7$ | $-0.87$ | $-0.97$ | $-1$ |

On a graph paper, we take $1 \text{ cm} = \frac{\pi}{6}$ units on the **$x$-axis**, and $0.5 \text{ cm} = 0.1$ unit on the **$y$-axis**.

For $y = \cos x$, we plot the points $(0, 1)$, $(\frac{\pi}{6}, 0.87)$, $(\frac{\pi}{3}, 0.5)$, $(\frac{\pi}{2}, 0)$, $(\frac{2\pi}{3}, -0.5)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{6}, -0.87)$, $(\pi, -1)$, $(\frac{7\pi}{6}, -0.87)$, $(\frac{4\pi}{3}, -0.5)$, $(\frac{3\pi}{2}, 0)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{3}, 0.5)$, $(\frac{11\pi}{6}, 0.87)$ and $(2\pi, 1)$. We join these points by a freehand curve to obtain the curve $y = \cos x$.

For $y = \cos \frac{1}{2}x$, we plot the points $(0, 1)$, $(\frac{\pi}{6}, 0.97)$, $(\frac{\pi}{3}, 0.87)$, $(\frac{\pi}{2}, 0.7)$, $(\frac{2\pi}{3}, 0.5)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{6}, 0.26)$, $(\pi, 0)$, $(\frac{7\pi}{6}, -0.26)$, $(\frac{4\pi}{3}, -0.5)$, $(\frac{3\pi}{2}, -0.7)$, $(\frac{5\pi}{3}, -0.87)$, $(\frac{11\pi}{6}, -0.97)$ and $(2\pi, -1)$. We join these points by a freehand curve to obtain the curve $y = \cos \frac{1}{2}x$.
