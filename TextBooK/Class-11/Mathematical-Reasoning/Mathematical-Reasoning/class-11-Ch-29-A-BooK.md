## Mathematical Reasoning

### Introduction

**Logic** is the science of reasoning. By **reasoning**, we draw valid inferences from some well-known facts. In mathematical language, there are two kinds of reasoning, namely **inductive** and **deductive**.

Mathematical induction is some sort of inductive reasoning, which we have studied in an earlier chapter.

Now, in this chapter we shall discuss **deductive reasoning**.

---

#### Sentences

A group of words which makes complete sense is called a **sentence**. We communicate our ideas to others through sentences. Sentences are classified as **declarative** (or assertive); **imperative**; **exclamatory** and **interrogative**.

#### Declarative Sentences

A sentence that makes an assertion is called a **declarative** or **assertive sentence**.

**Examples:** Each of the following is a declarative sentence.
1. The earth revolves round the sun.
2. The sum of the angles of a triangle is $180^{\circ}$.
3. 2 is the only even prime number.

#### Imperative Sentences

A sentence that expresses a request or a command is called an **imperative sentence**.

**Examples:** Each of the following is an imperative sentence.
1. Switch on the light.
2. Do your homework.
3. Give me a glass of water.
4. Keep quiet.

#### Interrogative Sentences

A sentence that asks some question is called an **interrogative sentence**.

**Examples:** Each of the following is an interrogative sentence.
1. Where are you coming from?
2. How far is Mumbai from here?
3. Is every set finite?

#### Exclamatory Sentences

A sentence that expresses some strong feeling is called an **exclamatory sentence**.

**Examples:** Each of the following is an exclamatory sentence.
1. What a beautiful girl!
2. Listen to me, Tanvy!
3. May you live long!
4. Hurrah! We have won.

#### Statements

A declarative sentence which can be judged to be **true** or **false** but not both, is called a mathematically acceptable **statement** or simply a **statement**.

### Example 1:

Check whether the following sentences are statements. Give reasons for your answer.

1. The earth is a planet.
2. There are 35 days in a month.
3. Mathematics is difficult.
4. Every square is a rectangle.
5. Every set is a finite set.
6. Every real number is a complex number.
7. 9 is less than 7.
8. The square of a natural number is an even number.

#### Solution:

1. It is a scientifically established fact that the earth is a planet. So, the given sentence is **true**. Hence, it is a **statement**.
2. We know that the maximum number of days in a given month is 31. So, the given sentence is **false**. Therefore, it is a **statement**.
3. For some people mathematics can be easy and for some others it can be difficult. So, we cannot judge whether the given sentence is true or false. Hence, it is **not a statement**.
4. In a square, every angle measures $90^{\circ}$ and its opposite sides are equal. So, it is a rectangle. Thus, the given sentence is **true**. Hence, it is a **statement**.
5. Since there are sets which are not finite, so the given sentence is **false**. Therefore, it is a **statement**.
6. Every real number $a$ can be written as $a=(a+0i)$. So, every real number is a complex number. Therefore, the given sentence is **true**. Hence, it is a **statement**.
7. Since 9 is greater than 7, so the given sentence is **false**. Hence, it is a **statement**.
8. We know that 2 and 3 are natural numbers such that $2^{2}=4$, which is even and $3^{2}=9$, which is odd. So, the given sentence is sometimes true and sometimes false. Therefore, it is **not a statement**.

---

#### Remark (I)

Sentences involving variable time such as 'yesterday', 'today' and 'tomorrow' are **not statements**.

**Examples:** None of the sentences given below is a statement.
1. Today is Sunday.
2. Yesterday was a windy day.
3. Tomorrow is a holiday.

#### Remark (II)

Sentences involving variable places such as 'here', 'there', etc., are **not statements**.

**Examples:** None of the sentences given below is a statement.
1. Meerut is far from here.
2. This place is very hot.
3. Rohit will reach there in time.

#### Remark (III)

Sentences involving variable pronouns such as 'he', 'she', etc., are **not statements**.

**Examples:** None of the sentences given below is a statement.
1. He is a doctor.
2. She is a graduate.

### Example 2:

Check whether the following sentences are statements.

1. Answer this question.
2. Everyone in this room is rich.
3. There is no rain without clouds.
4. Mathematics is fun.
5. She is a commerce graduate.
6. $\sqrt{2}$ is a rational number.
7. Fire is always hot.
8. The sides of a quadrilateral have equal length.

#### Solution:

1. It is an order and therefore, it is **not a statement**.
2. From the context it is not clear which room is referred here and also the term rich is not well defined. So, the given sentence is **not a statement**.
3. This sentence is always **true** and therefore, it is a **statement**.
4. For those who like mathematics it may be a fun but for others it may not be. So, the given sentence cannot be judged to be true or false. Hence, it is **not a statement**.
5. It is not known who 'she' is. So, the given sentence is **not a statement**.
6. Clearly, $\sqrt{2}$ is an irrational number. So, the given sentence is **false**. Therefore, it is a **statement**.
7. Clearly, the given sentence is always **true**. Hence, it is a **statement**.
8. All the sides of a square as well as that of a rhombus are equal. But, the sides of a trapezium as well as that of a rectangle are inequal. So, the given sentence is sometimes true and sometimes false. Hence, it is **not a statement**.

## Negation of a Statement

The denial of an assertion contained in a statement is called its **negation**. We use the symbol **$\sim p$** to denote the negation or denial of a statement $p$. The denial of a statement can be expressed in various ways.

### Example 3:

Let $p$: Kolkata is a city. Express the denial of $p$ in three different ways.

#### Solution:

This can be done as shown below.
- $\sim p$: Kolkata is **not** a city.
- $\sim p$: It is **false** that Kolkata is a city.
- $\sim p$: It is **not the case** that Kolkata is a city.

---

### Example 4:

Write the negation of each of the following statements in two different ways.

1. Africa is a continent.
2. $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
3. All integers are rational numbers.
4. Some prime numbers are odd numbers.
5. Everyone in Germany speaks German.

#### Solution:

Given below are the required negations:

1. Africa is **not** a continent.
   Or, It is **false** that Africa is a continent.
2. $\sqrt{5}$ is **not** rational.
   Or, It is **false** that $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
3. There exists an integer which is **not** a rational number.
   Or, It is **false** that all integers are rational numbers.
4. **No** prime number is an odd number.
   Or, It is **false** that some prime numbers are odd numbers.
5. **At least one** person in Germany does **not** speak German.
   Or, It is **false** that everyone in Germany speaks German.

### Example 5:

Write the negation of each of the following statements:

- $p$: All birds have wings.
- $q$: For every real number $x$, $x^{2} > x$.
- $r$: There exists a real number $x$ such that $0 < x < 1$.
- $s$: For every real number $x$, either $x > 1$ or $x < 1$.

#### Solution:

We have:
- $\sim p$: There exists a bird which has **no wings**.
- $\sim q$: There exists a real number $x$ such that $x^{2}$ is **not greater than** $x$.
- $\sim r$: There **does not exist** a real number $x$ such that $0 < x < 1$.
- $\sim s$: There exists a real number $x$ such that **neither** $x > 1$ **nor** $x < 1$.
