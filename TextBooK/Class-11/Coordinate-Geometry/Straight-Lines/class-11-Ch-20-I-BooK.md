## Miscellaneous Problems

It may be mentioned here that when the equations of two lines are given, then on solving them simultaneously, we get the **point of intersection** of the given lines.

---

### Example 1: Find the point of intersection of the lines $5 x+7 y=3$ and $2 x-3 y=7$.

#### Solution:
The given equations are:
$$
\begin{align*}
& 5 x+7 y-3=0 \tag{i}\\
& 2 x-3 y-7=0 \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii), by cross multiplication, we get
$$
\frac{x}{(-49-9)}=\frac{y}{(-6+35)}=\frac{1}{(-15-14)}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad x=\left(\frac{-58}{-29}\right)=2 \quad \text{and} \quad y=\left(\frac{29}{-29}\right)=-1.
$$
Hence, the point of intersection of the given lines is $P(2, -1)$.

---

### Example 2: Find the equation of the line parallel to the $y$-axis and drawn through the point of intersection of the lines $x-7 y+15=0$ and $2 x+y=0$.

#### Solution:
The given equations are:
$$
\begin{align*}
x-7 y+15=0 \tag{i} \\
2 x+y=0 \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii), we get $x=-1$ and $y=2$.

Thus, the given lines intersect at the point $P(-1, 2)$.

The line parallel to the $y$-axis and drawn through $P(-1, 2)$ is given by $x=-1$, i.e., $x+1=0$.

Hence, the required equation is $x+1=0$.

### Example 3: Find the equation of the line passing through the intersection of the lines $x+2 y+3=0$ and $3 x+4 y+7=0$, and parallel to the line $y-x=8$.

#### Solution:
The given intersecting lines are:
$$
\begin{align*}
x+2 y+3=0 \tag{i} \\
3 x+4 y+7=0 \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$
By cross multiplication, we get
$$
\frac{x}{(14-12)}=\frac{y}{(9-7)}=\frac{1}{(4-6)} \Rightarrow \frac{x}{2}=\frac{y}{2}=\frac{1}{-2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x=\frac{2}{-2}=-1 \text{ and } y=\frac{2}{-2}=-1
$$
Thus, the lines (i) and (ii) intersect at the point $P(-1, -1)$.

Now, $y-x=8 \Rightarrow y=x+8$.
The slope of this line is $1$.
The slope of the line parallel to this line is $1$.

So, the equation of the required line is $\frac{y+1}{x+1}=1 \Rightarrow x-y=0$.

### Example 4: Find the value of $k$ for which the lines $3 x+y=2, k x+2 y=3$ and $2 x-y=3$ may intersect at a point.

#### Solution:
The given lines are:
$$
\begin{align*}
3 x+y-2=0 \tag{i} \\
k x+2 y-3=0 \tag{ii} \\
2 x-y-3=0 \tag{iii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (iii) by cross multiplication, we get
$$
\frac{x}{(-3-2)}=\frac{y}{(-4+9)}=\frac{1}{(-3-2)} \Rightarrow \frac{x}{-5}=\frac{y}{5}=\frac{1}{-5}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x=\left(\frac{-5}{-5}\right)=1 \quad \text{and} \quad y=\left(\frac{5}{-5}\right)=-1
$$
Thus, the point of intersection of (i) and (iii) is $P(1, -1)$.

For the given lines to intersect at a point, $x=1$ and $y=-1$ must satisfy (ii) also.
$$
\therefore \quad(k \times 1)+2 \times(-1)-3=0 \Rightarrow k-5=0 \Rightarrow k=5.
$$
Hence, $k=5$.

### Example 5: Show that the lines $x-y=6, 4 x-3 y=20$ and $6 x+5 y+8=0$ are concurrent. Also find their point of intersection.

#### Solution:
The given equations are:
$$
\begin{align*}
x-y-6=0 \tag{i} \\
4 x-3 y-20=0 \tag{ii} \\
6 x+5 y+8=0 \tag{iii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii) by cross multiplication, we get
$$
\frac{x}{(20-18)}=\frac{y}{(-24+20)}=\frac{1}{(-3+4)} \Rightarrow \frac{x}{2}=\frac{y}{-4}=\frac{1}{1} \Rightarrow x=2, y=-4.
$$
Thus, the lines (i) and (ii) intersect at the point $P(2, -4)$.

Putting $x=2$ and $y=-4$ in (iii), we get
$$
\mathrm{LHS}=6 \times 2+5 \times(-4)+8=0=\mathrm{RHS}.
$$
This shows that the point $P(2, -4)$ also lies on (iii).

Thus, all the given three lines intersect at the same point.
Hence, the given lines are **concurrent** and their point of intersection is $P(2, -4)$.

### Example 6: If the three lines $y=m_{1} x+c_{1}, y=m_{2} x+c_{2}$ and $y=m_{3} x+c_{3}$ are concurrent then show that $m_{1}\left(c_{2}-c_{3}\right)+m_{2}\left(c_{3}-c_{1}\right)+m_{3}\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)=0$.

#### Solution:
The given lines are:
$$
\begin{align*}
& m_{1} x-y+c_{1}=0 \tag{i}\\
& m_{2} x-y+c_{2}=0 \tag{ii}\\
& m_{3} x-y+c_{3}=0 \tag{iii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii) by cross multiplication, we get
$$
\frac{x}{\left(-c_{2}+c_{1}\right)}=\frac{y}{\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)}=\frac{1}{\left(-m_{1}+m_{2}\right)}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad x=\frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)} \quad \text{and} \quad y=\frac{\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)}.
$$
Thus, the point of intersection of (i) and (ii) is $P\left(\frac{c_{1}-c_{2}}{m_{2}-m_{1}}, \frac{m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}}{m_{2}-m_{1}}\right)$.

Since the given three lines meet at a point, the point $P$ must therefore lie on (iii) also.
$$
\therefore \quad m_{3} \cdot \frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)}-\frac{\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)}+c_{3}=0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow m_{3}\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)-\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)+c_{3}\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)=0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow m_{1}\left(c_{2}-c_{3}\right)+m_{2}\left(c_{3}-c_{1}\right)+m_{3}\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)=0.
$$

### Example 7: Find the area of the triangle formed by the lines $y-x=0, x+y=0$ and $x-c=0$.

#### Solution:
Let the sides $AC$, $AB$ and $BC$ of $\triangle ABC$ be represented by $y-x=0, x+y=0$ and $x-c=0$ respectively.

On solving these equations pairwise, we get the points $A(0,0)$, $B(c,-c)$ and $C(c, c)$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \text{area of } \triangle ABC \text{ is given by } \Delta & =\frac{1}{2}\left|x_{1}\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)+x_{2}\left(y_{3}-y_{1}\right)+x_{3}\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)\right| \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \cdot|0 \cdot(-c-c)+c(c-0)+c(0+c)| \text{ sq units} \\
& =\left(\frac{1}{2} \times 2 c^{2}\right) \text{ sq units }=c^{2} \text{ sq units.}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the required area is $c^{2}$ sq units.

### Example 8: Find the area of the triangle formed by the lines $y=m_{1} x+c_{1}$, $y=m_{2} x+c_{2}$ and $x=0$.

#### Solution:
Let the sides $AC$, $AB$ and $BC$ of $\triangle ABC$ be represented by the equations:
$$
\begin{align*}
m_{1} x-y+c_{1} & =0 \tag{i}\\
m_{2} x-y+c_{2} & =0 \tag{ii}\\
x & =0 \tag{iii}
\end{align*}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii) by cross multiplication, we have
$$
\frac{x}{\left(-c_{2}+c_{1}\right)}=\frac{y}{\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)}=\frac{1}{\left(-m_{1}+m_{2}\right)}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad x=\frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)} \quad \text{and} \quad y=\frac{\left(m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)}.
$$
Thus, the lines $AC$ and $AB$ intersect at $A\left(\frac{c_{1}-c_{2}}{m_{2}-m_{1}}, \frac{m_{2} c_{1}-m_{1} c_{2}}{m_{2}-m_{1}}\right)$.

On solving (ii) and (iii), we get $B(0, c_{2})$.
On solving (i) and (iii), we get $C(0, c_{1})$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \text{area of } \triangle ABC \text{ is given by } \Delta & =\frac{1}{2} \cdot\left|x_{1}\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)+x_{2}\left(y_{3}-y_{1}\right)+x_{3}\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)\right| \\
& =\frac{1}{2} \cdot\left|\frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)}{\left(m_{2}-m_{1}\right)} \cdot\left(c_{2}-c_{1}\right)+0+0\right|=\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)^{2}}{\left|m_{1}-m_{2}\right|}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the area of the triangle formed by the given lines is $\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{\left(c_{1}-c_{2}\right)^{2}}{\left|m_{1}-m_{2}\right|}$.

### Example 9: Find the image of the point $P(3,8)$ with respect to the line $x+3 y=7$, assuming the given line to be a plane mirror.

#### Solution:
Let $AB$ be the given line whose equation is $x+3y-7=0$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_85dce451269043be4163g-70.jpg?height=393&width=454&top_left_y=473&top_left_x=743)

Now, $x+3 y=7 \Rightarrow y=-\frac{1}{3} x+\frac{7}{3}$.

The slope of $AB$ is $-\frac{1}{3}$.

Let $P(3,8)$ be the given point and let $Q(x_{1}, y_{1})$ be its image in $AB$. Join $PQ$, intersecting $AB$ at $N$. Then, $PN \perp AB$ and $PN=QN$.

Let the slope of $PQ$ be $m$. Then,
$$
m \times\left(-\frac{1}{3}\right)=-1 \Rightarrow m=3 \quad[\because AB \perp PQ]
$$
The equation of line $PQ$ is given by
$$
\frac{y-8}{x-3}=3 \Rightarrow y-8=3 x-9 \Rightarrow 3 x-y-1=0 \tag{ii}
$$
On solving (i) and (ii), we get
$$
\frac{x}{(-3-7)}=\frac{y}{(-21+1)}=\frac{1}{(-1-9)} \Rightarrow \frac{x}{-10}=\frac{y}{-20}=\frac{1}{-10}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x=1 \text{ and } y=2
$$
Thus, $AB$ and $PQ$ intersect at the point $N(1,2)$. Also, $N$ bisects $PQ$.
$$
\therefore \quad\left(\frac{3+x_{1}}{2}=1, \frac{8+y_{1}}{2}=2\right) \Rightarrow x_{1}=-1 \quad \text{and} \quad y_{1}=-4.
$$
Hence, the image of the given point $P(3,8)$ is $Q(-1,-4)$.
