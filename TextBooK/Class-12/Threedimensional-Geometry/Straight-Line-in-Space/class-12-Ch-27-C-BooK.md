## Angle between Two Lines

### (i) When the Given Lines are in Vector Form

Let the equations of the given lines in vector form be $\vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{1}}+\lambda \overrightarrow{m_{1}}$ and $\vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{2}}+\mu \overrightarrow{m_{2}}$, where $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are scalars.

Let $\theta$ be the angle between these lines.
Since the given lines are parallel to $\overrightarrow{m_{1}}$ and $\overrightarrow{m_{2}}$ respectively, the angle between the given lines must be equal to the angle between $\overrightarrow{m_{1}}$ and $\overrightarrow{m_{2}}$.

$$
\therefore \quad \cos \theta=\frac{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}} \cdot \overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|}{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}}\right| \cdot\left|\overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|}
$$

### (ii) When the Given Lines are in Cartesian Form

Let the Cartesian equations of two given lines be

$$
\frac{x-x_{1}}{a_{1}}=\frac{y-y_{1}}{b_{1}}=\frac{z-z_{1}}{c_{1}} \text { and } \frac{x-x_{2}}{a_{2}}=\frac{y-y_{2}}{b_{2}}=\frac{z-z_{2}}{c_{2}} .
$$

Then, the **direction ratios** of these lines are $a_{1}, b_{1}, c_{1}$, and $a_{2}, b_{2}, c_{2}$ respectively.
Let $\theta$ be the angle between these lines. Then,

$$
\cos \theta=\frac{\left|a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}\right|}{\left(\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+b_{1}^{2}+c_{1}^{2}}\right)\left(\sqrt{a_{2}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}+c_{2}^{2}}\right)} .
$$

---

## SUMMARY

- (i) If $\theta$ is the angle between the lines $\vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{1}}+\lambda \overrightarrow{m_{1}}$ and $\vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{2}}+\mu \overrightarrow{m_{2}}$ then

$$
\cos \theta=\frac{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}} \cdot \overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|}{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}}\right| \cdot\left|\overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|}
$$

**REMARK:** $\theta=\frac{\pi}{2} \Leftrightarrow m_{1} \cdot m_{2}=0$.

- (ii) If $\theta$ is the angle between the lines whose Cartesian equations are

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{x-x_{1}}{a_{1}}=\frac{y-y_{1}}{b_{1}}=\frac{z-z_{1}}{c_{1}} \text { and } \frac{x-x_{2}}{a_{2}}=\frac{y-y_{2}}{b_{2}}=\frac{z-z_{2}}{c_{2}} \text { then } \\
& \cos \theta=\frac{\left|a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}\right|}{\left(\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+b_{1}^{2}+c_{1}^{2}}\right)\left(\sqrt{a_{2}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}+c_{2}^{2}}\right)}
\end{aligned}
$$

**REMARK:** $\theta=\frac{\pi}{2} \Leftrightarrow a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}=0$.

## SOLVED EXAMPLES

### Example 1

Find the angle between the lines

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \vec{r}=(2 \hat{i}-5 \hat{j}+\hat{k})+\lambda(3 \hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+6 \hat{k}) \text { and } \\
& \vec{r}=(7 \hat{i}-6 \hat{k})+\mu(\hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+2 \hat{k}) .
\end{aligned}
$$

*[CBSE 2014]*

#### Solution

The given lines are of the form

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{1}}+\lambda \overrightarrow{m_{1}} \text { and } \vec{r}=\overrightarrow{r_{2}}+\lambda \overrightarrow{m_{2}}, \text { where } \\
& \overrightarrow{m_{1}}=(3 \hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+6 \hat{k}) \text { and } \overrightarrow{m_{2}}=(\hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+2 \hat{k})
\end{aligned}
$$

Let $\theta$ be the angle between the given lines. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos \theta & =\frac{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}} \cdot \overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|}{\left|\overrightarrow{m_{1}}\right|\left|\overrightarrow{m_{2}}\right|} \\
& =\frac{|(3 \hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+6 \hat{k}) \cdot(\hat{i}+2 \hat{j}+2 \hat{k})|}{\left(\sqrt{3^{2}+2^{2}+6^{2}}\right) \cdot\left(\sqrt{1^{2}+2^{2}+2^{2}}\right)}=\frac{(3+4+12)}{(\sqrt{49})(\sqrt{9})} \\
& =\frac{19}{(7 \times 3)}=\frac{19}{21} \\
\therefore \quad \theta & =\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{19}{21}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the angle between the given lines is $\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{19}{21}\right)$.

### Example 2

Find the angle between the lines

$$
\begin{equation*}
\frac{-x+2}{-2}=\frac{y-1}{7}=\frac{z+3}{-3} \text { and } \frac{x+2}{-1}=\frac{2 y-8}{4}=\frac{z-5}{4} . \tag{CBSE2011}
\end{equation*}
$$

#### Solution

The given equations in **standard form** are

$$
\frac{x-2}{2}=\frac{y-1}{7}=\frac{z+3}{-3} \text { and } \frac{x+2}{-1}=\frac{y-4}{2}=\frac{z-5}{4} .
$$

Here $a_{1}=2, b_{1}=7, c_{1}=-3$ and $a_{2}=-1, b_{2}=2, c_{2}=4$.
Let $\theta$ be the angle between the given lines. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos \theta & =\frac{\left|a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}\right|}{\left(\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+b_{1}^{2}+c_{1}^{2}}\right)\left(\sqrt{a_{2}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}+c_{2}^{2}}\right)} \\
& =\frac{|2 \times(-1)+7 \times 2+(-3) \times 4|}{\left\{\sqrt{2^{2}+7^{2}+(-3)^{2}}\right\}\left\{\sqrt{(-1)^{2}+2^{2}+4^{2}}\right\}} \\
& =\frac{|(-2)+14+(-12)|}{(\sqrt{62})(\sqrt{21})}=0 . \\
\therefore \quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{2} & .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the angle between the given lines is $\frac{\pi}{2}$.

### Example 3

Find the value of $\lambda$ so that the following lines are **perpendicular** to each other:

$$
\frac{x-5}{5 \lambda+2}=\frac{2-y}{5}=\frac{1-z}{-1} \text { and } \frac{x}{1}=\frac{2 y+1}{4 \lambda}=\frac{1-z}{-3} .
$$

*[CBSE 2009]*

#### Solution

The given equations in **standard form** are

$$
\frac{x-5}{5 \lambda+2}=\frac{y-2}{-5}=\frac{z-1}{1} \text { and } \frac{x}{1}=\frac{y+\frac{1}{2}}{2 \lambda}=\frac{z-1}{3} .
$$

Here $a_{1}=5 \lambda+2, b_{1}=-5, c_{1}=1$ and $a_{2}=1, b_{2}=2 \lambda$ and $c_{2}=3$.
Since the given lines are perpendicular to each other, we must have $a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}=0$.

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore \quad(5 \lambda+2) \times 1+(-5) \times(2 \lambda)+1 \times 3=0 \\
& \Rightarrow 5 \lambda+2-10 \lambda+3=0 \Rightarrow 5 \lambda=5 \Rightarrow \lambda=1 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the required value of $\lambda$ is 1.

### Example 4

Find the angle between the lines

$$
\frac{x+1}{1}=\frac{2 y-3}{3}=\frac{z-6}{2} \text { and } \frac{x-4}{3}=\frac{y+3}{-2}, z=5 .
$$

#### Solution

The given equations may be written as

$$
\begin{equation*}
\frac{x+1}{1}=\frac{y-\frac{3}{2}}{\frac{3}{2}}=\frac{z-6}{2} \Rightarrow \frac{x+1}{2}=\frac{y-\frac{3}{2}}{3}=\frac{z-6}{4} \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

and $\quad \frac{x-4}{3}=\frac{y+3}{-2}=\frac{z-5}{0}$.

Here $a_{1}=2, b_{1}=3, c_{1}=4$ and $a_{2}=3, b_{2}=-2, c_{2}=0$.
Let $\theta$ be the angle between the given lines. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos \theta & =\frac{\left|a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}\right|}{\left(\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+b_{1}^{2}+c_{1}^{2}}\right)\left(\sqrt{a_{2}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}+c_{2}^{2}}\right)} \\
& =\frac{|(2 \times 3)+3 \times(-2)+4 \times 0|}{(\sqrt{4+9+16})(\sqrt{9+4+0})}=\frac{0}{(\sqrt{29} \times \sqrt{13})}=0 . \\
\therefore \quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{2} & .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the angle between the given lines is $\frac{\pi}{2}$.

### Example 5

Find the angle between the lines

$$
\frac{5-x}{3}=\frac{y+3}{-4}, z=7 \text { and } \frac{x}{1}=\frac{1-y}{2}=\frac{z-6}{2} .
$$

#### Solution

The given lines in **standard form** are

$$
\frac{x-5}{-3}=\frac{y+3}{-4}=\frac{z-7}{0} \text { and } \frac{x}{1}=\frac{y-1}{-2}=\frac{z-6}{2}
$$

Here $a_{1}=-3, b_{1}=-4, c_{1}=0$ and $a_{2}=1, b_{2}=-2, c_{2}=2$.
Let $\theta$ be the angle between the given lines. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\cos \theta & =\frac{\left|a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}\right|}{\left\{\sqrt{a_{1}^{2}+b_{1}^{2}+c_{1}^{2}}\right\}\left\{\sqrt{a_{2}^{2}+b_{2}^{2}+c_{2}^{2}}\right\}} \\
& =\frac{|(-3) \times 1+(-4) \times(-2)+0 \times 2|}{\{\sqrt{9+16+0}\}\{\sqrt{1+4+4}\}}=\frac{5}{5 \times 3}=\frac{1}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\therefore \quad \theta=\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right).
$$

Hence, the angle between the given lines is $\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right)$.

### Example 6

Find the value of $k$ so that the lines

$$
\frac{1-x}{3}=\frac{7 y-14}{2 k}=\frac{z-3}{2} \text { and } \frac{7-7 x}{3 k}=\frac{5-y}{1}=\frac{6-z}{5}
$$

are at **right angles**.

#### Solution

The given equation in **standard form** are

$$
\begin{align*}
& \frac{x-1}{-3}=\frac{y-2}{\left(\frac{2 k}{7}\right)}=\frac{z-3}{2} \Rightarrow \frac{x-1}{-21}=\frac{y-2}{2 k}=\frac{z-3}{14}  \tag{i}\\
& \frac{x-1}{\left(\frac{-3 k}{7}\right)}=\frac{y-5}{-1}=\frac{z-6}{-5} \Rightarrow \frac{x-1}{-3 k}=\frac{y-5}{-7}=\frac{z-6}{-35} \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$

Here, $a_{1}=-21, b_{1}=2 k, c_{1}=14$ and $a_{2}=-3 k, b_{2}=-7, c_{2}=-35$.
Since the given lines are at right angles, we have

$$
\begin{aligned}
& a_{1} a_{2}+b_{1} b_{2}+c_{1} c_{2}=0 \\
\Rightarrow & (-21) \times(-3 k)+(2 k) \times(-7)+14 \times(-35)=0 \\
\Rightarrow & 63 k-14 k-490=0 \Rightarrow 49 k=490 \Rightarrow k=10 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $k=10$.

### Example 7

Prove that the lines $x=a y+b, z=c y+d$, and $x=a^{\prime} y+b^{\prime}, z=c^{\prime} y+d^{\prime}$ are **perpendicular** if $a a^{\prime}+c c^{\prime}+1=0$.

#### Solution

The equations of the first line are

$$
\begin{align*}
& x=a y+b, z=c y+d \\
\Leftrightarrow & \frac{x-b}{a}=y, \frac{z-d}{c}=y \\
\Leftrightarrow & \frac{x-b}{a}=\frac{y}{1}=\frac{z-d}{c} \tag{i}
\end{align*}
$$

Similarly, the equations of the second line are

$$
\begin{equation*}
\frac{x-b^{\prime}}{a^{\prime}}=\frac{y}{1}=\frac{z-d^{\prime}}{c^{\prime}} . \tag{ii}
\end{equation*}
$$

The given lines are perpendicular to each other

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \Leftrightarrow \quad a a^{\prime}+1 \times 1+c c^{\prime}=0 \\
& \Leftrightarrow \quad a a^{\prime}+c c^{\prime}+1=0 .
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 8

Find the angle between the two lines, one of which has **direction ratios** $2,2,1$, and the other is obtained by joining the points $(3,1,4)$ and $(7, 2, 12)$.

#### Solution

Let $L_{1}$ and $L_{2}$ be the given lines.
Then, d.r.'s of $L_{1}$ are $2, 2, 1$.
D.r.'s of $L_{2}$ are $(7-3), (2-1), (12-4)$, i.e., $4, 1, 8$.

Let $\theta$ be the angle between the given lines. Then,

$$
\cos \theta=\frac{|(2 \times 4+2 \times 1+1 \times 8)|}{\left(\sqrt{2^{2}+2^{2}+1^{2}}\right)\left(\sqrt{4^{2}+1^{2}+8^{2}}\right)}=\frac{18}{27}=\frac{2}{3}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \theta=\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right).
$$

Hence, the angle between the given lines is $\cos ^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)$.
