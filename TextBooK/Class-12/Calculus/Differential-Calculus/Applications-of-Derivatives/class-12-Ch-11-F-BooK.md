### Example 1

Amongst all pairs of positive numbers with sum 24, find those whose product is maximum.

#### Solution:

Let the numbers be $x$ and $(24-x)$.
Let $\quad P=x(24-x)=\left(24 x-x^{2}\right)$.
Then, $\frac{d P}{d x}=(24-2 x)$ and $\frac{d^{2} P}{d x^{2}}=-2$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d P}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow(24-2 x)=0 \Rightarrow x=12$.
Thus, $\left\{\frac{d^{2} P}{d x^{2}}\right\}_{x=12}=-2<0$.
$\therefore \quad x=12$ is a point of maximum.
Hence, the required numbers are 12 and 12.

### Example 2

Amongst all pairs of positive numbers with product 256, find those whose sum is the least.

#### Solution:

Let the required numbers be $x$ and $\frac{256}{x}$.
Let $S=\left(x+\frac{256}{x}\right)$. Then, $\frac{d S}{d x}=\left(1-\frac{256}{x^{2}}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} S}{d x^{2}}=\frac{512}{x^{3}}$.
For a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d S}{d x}=0$.
Now, $\frac{d S}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow\left(1-\frac{256}{x^{2}}\right)=0 \Rightarrow x^{2}=256$ or $x=16$.
Also, $\left[\frac{d^{2} S}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=16}=\left(\frac{512}{16 \times 16 \times 16}\right)=\frac{1}{8}>0$.
So, $x=16$ is a point of minimum.
Hence, the required numbers are 16 and 16.

### Example 3

Find two positive numbers $x$ and $y$ such that $(x+y)=60$ and $xy^{3}$ is maximum.

#### Solution:

Let $(x+y)=60$ and let $P=xy^{3}$.
Then, $P=(60-y)y^{3} \quad[\because x=(60-y)]$.
$\therefore \quad \frac{d P}{d y}=3 y^{2}(60-y)+y^{3}(-1)=\left(180 y^{2}-4 y^{3}\right)=4 y^{2}(45-y)$
and, $\quad \frac{d^{2} P}{d y^{2}}=\left(360 y-12 y^{2}\right)=12 y(30-y)$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d P}{d y}=0 \Rightarrow 4 y^{2}(45-y)=0 \Rightarrow y=0$ or $y=45$.

Neglecting $y=0$, we are left with $y=45$.
Now, $\left[\frac{d^{2} p}{d y^{2}}\right]_{y=45}=(12 \times 45)(30-45)=-8100<0$.
So, $y=45$ is a point of maximum.
Hence, the numbers are 45 and 15.

### Example 4

Find two numbers whose sum is 16 and the sum of whose cubes is minimum.

#### Solution:

Let the numbers be $x$ and $(16-x)$.
Let $S=x^{3}+(16-x)^{3}$.
Then, $\frac{d S}{d x}=3 x^{2}-3(16-x)^{2}$ and $\frac{d^{2} S}{d x^{2}}=6 x+6(16-x)=96$.
Now, $\frac{d S}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow 3 x^{2}-3(16-x)^{2}=0 \Rightarrow x=8$.
And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} S}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=8}=96>0$.
$\therefore x=8$ is a point of minimum.
Hence, the required numbers are 8 and 8.

### Example 5

Show that of all the rectangles with a given perimeter, the square has the largest area.

#### Solution:

Let $a$ be the fixed perimeter.
Consider a rectangle with sides $x$ and $y$ and perimeter $a$.
Let $A$ be the area of the rectangle.
Then, $2 x+2 y=a$

And, $A=x y=x\left(\frac{a-2 x}{2}\right) \quad[$ using (i) $]$.
$\therefore \quad \frac{d A}{d x}=\left(\frac{1}{2} a-2 x\right)=\frac{1}{2}(a-4 x)$ and $\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=-2$.
Now, $\frac{d A}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2}(a-4 x)=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{a}{4}$.
Also, $\left[\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=(a / 4)}=-2<0$.
$\therefore \quad x=(a / 4)$ is a point of maximum.
Also, from (i), we have $y=\frac{1}{2}(a-2 x)=\frac{1}{2}\left(a-\frac{a}{2}\right)=\frac{1}{4} a$, when $x=\frac{a}{4}$.
Thus, $x=y \quad[$ each $=(a / 4)]$.
Hence, the rectangle is a square.

### Example 6

Show that of all the rectangles of a given area, the square has the smallest perimeter.
[CBSE 2011]

#### Solution:

Let $A$ be the fixed area.
Consider a rectangle with sides $x$ and $y$, and area $A$.
Let $P$ be the perimeter of this rectangle.
Then, $\quad A=x y \Rightarrow y=\frac{A}{x}$

And, $\quad P=2 x+2 y=2 x+\frac{2 A}{x} \quad[$ using (i) $]$.
Now, $\quad P=\left(2 x+\frac{2 A}{x}\right) \Rightarrow \frac{d P}{d x}=\left(2-\frac{2 A}{x^{2}}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} P}{d x^{2}}=\frac{4 A}{x^{3}}$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d P}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow 2-\frac{2 A}{x^{2}}=0 \Rightarrow x=\sqrt{A}$
and, $\quad\left[\frac{d^{2} P}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=\sqrt{A}}=\frac{4 A}{A^{3 / 2}}=\frac{4}{\sqrt{A}}>0$.
So, $x=\sqrt{A}$ is a point of minimum.
Moreover, $x=\sqrt{A} \Rightarrow y=\frac{A}{x}=\frac{A}{\sqrt{A}}=\sqrt{A}=x$.
So, when the perimeter is smallest, the rectangle is a square.

### Example 7

Prove that the area of a right-angled triangle of a given hypotenuse is maximum when the triangle is isosceles.
[CBSE 2012C]

#### Solution:

Let $h$ be the hypotenuse of the triangle and $x$ be its altitude. Then, the base of the triangle $=\sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}$.
Now, $\quad A=\frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}$
$\therefore \quad \frac{d A}{d x}=\frac{1}{2}\left\{x \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left(h^{2}-x^{2}\right)^{-1 / 2}(-2 x)+\sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}\right\}$

$$
=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{h^{2}-2 x^{2}}{\sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}}\right)
$$

and, $\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=\frac{\left(\sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}\right)(-4 x)-\left(h^{2}-2 x^{2}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2}\left(h^{2}-x^{2}\right)^{-1 / 2}(-2 x)}{2\left(h^{2}-x^{2}\right)}$

$$
=\frac{\left(2 x^{3}-3 x h^{2}\right)}{2\left(h^{2}-x^{2}\right)^{3 / 2}} .
$$

Now, $\frac{d A}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow\left(h^{2}-2 x^{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}}$.
$\therefore\left[\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}}}=-2<0$.

Thus, $A$ is maximum at $x=(h / \sqrt{2})$.
$\therefore$ base $=\sqrt{h^{2}-x^{2}}=\sqrt{\left(h^{2}-\frac{h^{2}}{2}\right)}=\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}}=$ altitude.
Hence, the triangle is isosceles.

### Example 8

If the sum of the lengths of the hypotenuse and a side of a right-angled triangle is given, show that the area of the triangle is maximum when the angle between them is $(\pi / 3)$.
[CBSE 2009]

#### Solution:

Let us consider a right-angled triangle with base $=x$ and hypotenuse $=y$. Let $x+y=k$, where $k$ is a constant. Let $\theta$ be the angle between the base and the hypotenuse. Let $A$ be the area of the triangle. Then,

$$
\begin{align*}
A & =\frac{1}{2} \times B C \times A C=\frac{1}{2} x \sqrt{y^{2}-x^{2}} . \\
\therefore \quad A^{2} & =\frac{x^{2}\left(y^{2}-x^{2}\right)}{4}=\frac{x^{2}\left[(k-x)^{2}-x^{2}\right]}{4}[\because y=(k-x)] \\
\text { or } A^{2} & =\frac{k^{2} x^{2}-2 k x^{3}}{4} \tag{i}
\end{align*}
$$

On differentiating (i), we get $2 A \cdot \frac{d A}{d x}=\frac{2 k^{2} x-6 k x^{2}}{4}$
or $\frac{d A}{d x}=\frac{k^{2} x-3 k x^{2}}{4 A}$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d A}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow\left(k^{2} x-3 k x^{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{k}{3}$ [neglecting $x=0$ ].
Now, differentiating (ii), we get

$$
\begin{equation*}
2\left(\frac{d A}{d x}\right)^{2}+2 A \cdot \frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=\frac{2 k^{2}-12 k x}{4} \tag{iii}
\end{equation*}
$$

Putting $\frac{d A}{d x}=0$ and $x=\frac{k}{3}$ in (iii), we get $\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=\frac{-k^{2}}{4 A}<0$.
Thus, $A$ is maximum when $x=(k / 3)$.
Now, $\quad x=\frac{k}{3} \Rightarrow y=\left(k-\frac{k}{3}\right)=\frac{2 k}{3}$.
$\therefore \quad \frac{x}{y}=\cos \theta \Rightarrow \cos \theta=\frac{(k / 3)}{(2 k / 3)}=\frac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \theta=\frac{\pi}{3}$.
Hence, the area is maximum when $\theta=\frac{\pi}{3}$.

### Example 9

Two sides of a triangle are given. Find the angle between them such that the area is maximum.

#### Solution:

Let $a$ and $b$ be the lengths of the given sides and let $\theta$ be the angle between them. Let $A$ be the area of the triangle.
Then, $A=\frac{1}{2} a b \sin \theta$.
$\therefore \quad \frac{d A}{d \theta}=\frac{1}{2} a b \cos \theta$ and $\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}=-\frac{1}{2} a b \sin \theta$.
Now, $\frac{d A}{d \theta}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{2} a b \cos \theta=0 \Rightarrow \cos \theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta=\frac{\pi}{2}$.
And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}\right]_{\theta=(\pi / 2)}=-\frac{1}{2} a b \sin \frac{\pi}{2}=-\frac{1}{2} a b<0$.
$\therefore \quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{2}$ is a point of maximum.

Hence, the area of the triangle is maximum when the angle between the given sides is $(\pi / 2)$.

### Example 10

Show that of all the rectangles inscribed in a given fixed circle, the square has the maximum area.
[CBSE 2008, '13C]

#### Solution:

Let $A B C D$ be a rectangle inscribed in a given circle with centre $O$ and radius $r$.
Let $\angle C A B=\theta$.
Then, $A C=2 r, A B=2 r \cos \theta$ and $B C=2 r \sin \theta$.
Let $A$ be the area of rectangle $A B C D$.
Then, $A=A B \times B C=4 r^{2} \sin \theta \cos \theta=2 r^{2} \sin 2 \theta$.
Thus, $A=2 r^{2} \sin 2 \theta$, where $r$ is constant.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_4c9510ea0d4c9f57f674g-190.jpg?height=204&width=209&top_left_y=840&top_left_x=998)

$\therefore \quad \frac{d A}{d \theta}=4 r^{2} \cos 2 \theta$ and $\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}=-8 r^{2} \sin 2 \theta$.
Now, $\frac{d A}{d \theta}=0 \Rightarrow 4 r^{2} \cos 2 \theta=0 \Rightarrow \cos 2 \theta=0 \Rightarrow 2 \theta=\frac{\pi}{2}$, i.e., $\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}$.
And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}\right]_{\theta=(\pi / 4)}=-8 r^{2} \sin \frac{\pi}{2}=-8 r^{2}<0$.
$\therefore \quad \theta=(\pi / 4)$ is a point of maximum.
Thus, area is maximum when $\theta=(\pi / 4)$.
In this case, $A B=2 r \cos \frac{\pi}{4}=r \sqrt{2}$
and,

$$
B C=2 r \sin \frac{\pi}{4}=r \sqrt{2}
$$

Thus, $A B=B C$ and therefore, $A B C D$ is a square.

### Example 11

Show that the triangle of maximum area that can be inscribed in a given circle is an equilateral triangle.

#### Solution:

Let $A B C$ be a triangle inscribed in a given circle with centre $O$ and radius $r$.

For maximum area, the vertex $A$ should be at a maximum distance from the base $B C$.

Therefore, $A$ must lie on the diameter, perpendicular to $B C$. Thus, $A D \perp B C$.

So, triangle $A B C$ must be isosceles.
Let $\angle C A D=\theta$.
Now, $B C=2 C D=2 O C \sin 2 \theta=2 r \sin 2 \theta$
and, $A D=(O A+O D)=(r+r \cos 2 \theta)$.
Let $A$ be the area of the triangle.
Then, $\quad A=\frac{1}{2} B C \times A D=r^{2} \sin 2 \theta(1+\cos 2 \theta)$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_4c9510ea0d4c9f57f674g-191.jpg?height=227&width=209&top_left_y=251&top_left_x=996)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore \quad \frac{d A}{d \theta} & =r^{2}[\sin 2 \theta(-2 \sin 2 \theta)+(1+\cos 2 \theta) \cdot 2 \cos 2 \theta] \\
& =r^{2}\left[2\left(\cos ^{2} 2 \theta-\sin ^{2} 2 \theta\right)+2 \cos 2 \theta\right]=2 r^{2}[\cos 4 \theta+\cos 2 \theta] .
\end{aligned}
$$

And, $\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}=2 r^{2}[-4 \sin 4 \theta-2 \sin 2 \theta]=-4 r^{2}(2 \sin 4 \theta+\sin 2 \theta)$.
Now, $\frac{d A}{d \theta}=0 \Rightarrow \cos 4 \theta+\cos 2 \theta=0$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \Rightarrow \cos 4 \theta=-\cos 2 \theta=\cos (\pi-2 \theta) \\
& \Rightarrow 4 \theta=\pi-2 \theta \Rightarrow \theta=\frac{\pi}{6}
\end{aligned}
$$

And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} A}{d \theta^{2}}\right]_{\theta=(\pi / 6)}=-4 r^{2}\left(2 \sin \frac{2 \pi}{3}+\sin \frac{\pi}{3}\right)=-6 r^{2} \sqrt{3}<0$.
$\therefore \quad \theta=\frac{\pi}{6}$ is a point of maximum.
So, in this case, each angle of the triangle is $(\pi / 3)$.
Hence, $A B C$ is an equilateral triangle.

### Example 12

The combined resistance $R$ of two resistors $R_{1}$ and $R_{2}$ where $R_{1}, R_{2}>0$ is given by $\frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{R_{1}}+\frac{1}{R_{2}}$.
If $R_{1}+R_{2}=C$ (constant), show that the maximum resistance $R$ is obtained by choosing $R_{1}=R_{2}$.

#### Solution:

We have $\quad R=\frac{R_{1} R_{2}}{\left(R_{1}+R_{2}\right)}$ and $R_{1}+R_{2}=C$.
$\therefore \quad R=\frac{R_{1}\left(C-R_{1}\right)}{C} \quad\left[\because R_{2}=\left(C-R_{1}\right)\right]$.
So, $\quad \frac{d R}{d R_{1}}=\frac{C-2 R_{1}}{C}$ and $\frac{d^{2} R}{d R_{1}{ }^{2}}=-\frac{2}{C}$.
For a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d R}{d R_{1}}=0$.

Now, $\frac{d R}{d R_{1}}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{C-2 R_{1}}{C}=0$ or $R_{1}=\frac{C}{2}$.
And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} R}{d R_{1}^{2}}\right]_{R_{1}=(C / 2)}=\frac{-2}{C}<0$.
Thus, $\quad R_{1}=\frac{C}{2}$ is a point of maximum.
When, $R_{1}=\frac{C}{2}$, we have $R_{2}=\left(C-\frac{C}{2}\right)=\frac{C}{2}$. Hence, $R_{1}=R_{2}$.

### Example 13

A beam of length $l$ is supported at one end. If $W$ is the uniform load per unit length, the bending moment $M$ at a distance $x$ from the end is given by $M=\left(\frac{l x}{2}-\frac{W x^{2}}{2}\right)$. Find the point on the beam at which the bending moment has the maximum value.

#### Solution:

$M=\left(\frac{l x}{2}-\frac{W x^{2}}{2}\right) \Rightarrow \frac{d M}{d x}=\left(\frac{l}{2}-W x\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} M}{d x^{2}}=-W$.
For a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d M}{d x}=0$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d M}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{l}{2}-W x=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{l}{2 W}$.
Also, $\frac{d^{2} M}{d x^{2}}=-W<0$ for all values of $x$.
$\therefore \quad x=\frac{l}{2 W}$ is a point of maxima.
So, the required point is at a distance of $(l / 2 W)$ from the supporting end.

### Example 14

A wire of length 25 m is to be cut into two pieces. One of the wires is to be made into a square and the other into a circle. What should be the lengths of the two pieces so that the combined area of the square and the circle is minimum?
[CBSE 1996]

#### Solution:

Let $x$ metres and $(25-x)$ metres be the required lengths.
Let $a$ be the side of the square formed and $r$ be the radius of the circle formed. Then,

$$
4 a=x \text { and } 2 \pi r=25-x \quad \text { or } \quad a=\frac{x}{4} \text { and } r=\frac{25-x}{2 \pi} .
$$

$\therefore$ area of the square $=\left(\frac{x^{2}}{16}\right)$ sq metres.
Area of the circle $=\pi\left(\frac{25-x}{2 \pi}\right)^{2}=\frac{(25-x)^{2}}{4 \pi}$.

Now, the combined area $A=\frac{x^{2}}{16}+\frac{(25-x)^{2}}{4 \pi}$.
$\therefore \frac{d A}{d x}=\frac{x}{8}-\frac{(25-x)}{2 \pi}=\frac{(\pi+4) x-100}{8 \pi}$, and $\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=\frac{(\pi+4)}{8 \pi}$.
For a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d A}{d x}=0$.
Now, $\frac{d A}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow(\pi+4) x-100=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{100}{(\pi+4)}$.
And, $\frac{d^{2} A}{d x^{2}}=\frac{(\pi+4)}{8 \pi}>0$ for all values of $x$.
$\therefore \quad x=\frac{100}{(\pi+4)}$ is a point of minimum.
$\therefore$ lengths of the pieces are $\left(\frac{100}{\pi+4}\right)$ metres and $\left(\frac{25 \pi}{\pi+4}\right)$ metres.

### Example 15

Show that a cylinder of a given volume which is open at the top has minimum total surface area, provided its height is equal to the radius of its base.
[CBSE 2009C, '14]

#### Solution:

Let $r$ be the radius and $h$ the height of the cylinder of given volume $V$.
Then, $\quad V=\pi r^{2} h \Rightarrow h=\frac{V}{\pi r^{2}}$

Let the total surface area be $S$. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
S & =\pi r^{2}+2 \pi r h=\left(\pi r^{2}+2 \pi r \cdot \frac{V}{\pi r^{2}}\right) \quad \text { [using (i)]. } \\
\therefore \quad S & =\left(\pi r^{2}+\frac{2 V}{r}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$

So, $\frac{d S}{d r}=\left(2 \pi r-\frac{2 V}{r^{2}}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} S}{d r^{2}}=\left(2 \pi+\frac{4 V}{r^{3}}\right)$.
For a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d S}{d r}=0$.
Now, $\frac{d S}{d r}=0 \Rightarrow 2 \pi r-\frac{2 V}{r^{2}}=0 \Rightarrow 2 \pi r^{3}=2 V=2 \pi r^{2} h \Rightarrow r=h$.

$$
\left(\frac{d^{2} S}{d r^{2}}\right)_{r=h}=\left(2 \pi+\frac{4 V}{h^{3}}\right)>0
$$

Thus, $r=h$ is a point of minimum.
This shows that the total surface area of the cylinder is minimum when $h=r$, i.e., when the height of the cylinder is equal to the radius of the base.

### Example 16

Show that the height of a cylinder which is open at the top, having a given surface and the greatest volume, is equal to the radius of its base.
[CBSE 2004, 2010]

#### Solution:

Let $r$ be the radius, $h$ the height and $S$ the given surface area of an open cylinder. Then, $S=\left(\pi r^{2}+2 \pi r h\right)$ or $h=\left(\frac{S-\pi r^{2}}{2 \pi r}\right)$ (i)

Let $V$ be the volume of the cylinder.
Then, $V=\pi r^{2} h=\pi r^{2} \cdot\left(\frac{S-\pi r^{2}}{2 \pi r}\right)=\left(\frac{r S-\pi r^{3}}{2}\right) \quad$ [using (i)].
$\therefore \quad \frac{d V}{d r}=\left(\frac{S-3 \pi r^{2}}{2}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}=-3 \pi r$.
Now, $\frac{d V}{d r}=0 \Rightarrow S-3 \pi r^{2}=0 \Rightarrow \pi r^{2}+2 \pi r h-3 \pi r^{2}=0 \Rightarrow h=r$.
And, $\left(\frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}\right)_{r=h}=-3 \pi h<0$. Thus, $r=h$ is a point of maximum.
So, $V$ is maximum when $r=h$, i.e., when the height of the cylinder is equal to the radius of its base.

### Example 17

Show that the semivertical angle of a cone of maximum volume and of given slant height is $\cos ^{-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$.
[CBSE 2008C, '14]

#### Solution:

Let $\alpha$ be the semivertical angle of a cone of given slant height $l$. Then, height of the cone $=l \cos \alpha$, radius of the base $=l \sin \alpha$. Let $V$ be the volume of the cone. Then,

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \qquad \begin{aligned}
V & =\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h=\frac{1}{3} \pi\left(l^{2} \sin ^{2} \alpha\right)(l \cos \alpha) \\
\text { or } V & =\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \sin ^{2} \alpha \cos \alpha .
\end{aligned} \\
& \therefore \frac{d V}{d \alpha}=\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3}\left(-\sin ^{3} \alpha+2 \sin \alpha \cos ^{2} \alpha\right)=\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \sin \alpha\left(2 \cos ^{2} \alpha-\sin ^{2} \alpha\right) . \\
& \text { And, } \frac{d^{2} V}{d \alpha^{2}}=\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \cos \alpha\left(2 \cos ^{2} \alpha-\sin ^{2} \alpha\right) \\
& \quad+\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \sin \alpha(-4 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha-2 \sin \alpha \cos \alpha) \\
& \quad=\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \cos ^{3} \alpha\left(2-7 \tan ^{2} \alpha\right) .
\end{aligned}
$$

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_4c9510ea0d4c9f57f674g-194.jpg?height=333&width=312&top_left_y=1076&top_left_x=895)

Now, for a maxima or minima, we have $\frac{d V}{d \alpha}=0$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d V}{d \alpha}=0 \Rightarrow \frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \sin \alpha\left(2 \cos ^{2} \alpha-\sin ^{2} \alpha\right)=0$

$$
\Rightarrow \sin \alpha=0 \text { or } 2 \cos ^{2} \alpha-\sin ^{2} \alpha=0
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \alpha=0 \text { or } \alpha=\tan ^{-1} \sqrt{2} .
$$

Neglecting $\alpha=0$, we have $\alpha=\tan ^{-1} \sqrt{2}$, i.e., $\tan \alpha=\sqrt{2}$.
Now, $\tan \alpha=\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow \sec ^{2} \alpha=3 \Rightarrow \cos \alpha=\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \Rightarrow \alpha=\cos ^{-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$
$\therefore \quad\left[\frac{d^{2} V}{d \alpha^{2}}\right]_{\alpha=\tan ^{-1} \sqrt{2}}=\frac{1}{3} \pi l^{3} \cdot\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right)^{3}(2-14)=-\frac{4 \pi l^{3}}{3 \sqrt{3}}<0$.
Thus, $V$ is maximum when $\alpha=\cos ^{-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$, i.e., when the semivertical angle of the cone is $\cos ^{-1} \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$.

### Example 18

Show that the semivertical angle of a right circular cone of given surface area and maximum volume is $\sin ^{-1}(1 / 3)$.

#### Solution:

Let $r$ be the radius, $l$ the slant height and $h$ the height of the cone.
Let $S$ denote the surface area and $V$ the volume of the cone.
Then, $S=\left(\pi r^{2}+\pi r l\right)=$ constant.

$$
\begin{equation*}
\therefore \quad l=\left(\frac{S}{\pi r}-r\right) \tag{i}
\end{equation*}
$$

Now, $\quad V=\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} h=\frac{1}{3} \pi r^{2} \sqrt{l^{2}-r^{2}}$.
$\therefore \quad V^{2}=\frac{1}{9} \pi^{2} r^{4}\left(l^{2}-r^{2}\right)=\frac{1}{9} \pi^{2} r^{4} \cdot\left\{\left(\frac{S}{\pi r}-r\right)^{2}-r^{2}\right\}$ [using (i)] $=\frac{1}{9} S\left(S r^{2}-2 \pi r^{4}\right)$.
Thus, $V^{2}=\left(\frac{1}{9} S^{2} r^{2}-\frac{2 \pi}{9} S r^{4}\right)$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\therefore 2 V \cdot \frac{d V}{d r}=\left(\frac{2}{9} S^{2} r-\frac{8 \pi S}{9} r^{3}\right)=\frac{2 r S}{9}\left(S-4 \pi r^{2}\right) \tag{ii}
\end{equation*}
$$

$\therefore \frac{d V}{d r}=0 \Rightarrow r=0$ or $\left(S-4 \pi r^{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow r^{2}=\frac{S}{4 \pi}$ (neglecting $r=0$).
On differentiating (ii), we get $2\left(\frac{d V}{d r}\right)^{2}+2 V \cdot \frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}=\frac{1}{9} S\left(2 S-24 \pi r^{2}\right)$.
Putting $\frac{d V}{d r}=0$ and $r^{2}=\frac{S}{4 \pi}$, we get

$$
2 V \cdot \frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}=\frac{1}{9} S(2 S-6 S)=-\frac{4}{9} S^{2}<0
$$

$\therefore$ when the volume is maximum, we have

$$
r^{2}=\frac{S}{4 \pi}=\frac{\left(\pi r^{2}+\pi r l\right)}{4 \pi} \Rightarrow l=3 r
$$

Now, if $\alpha$ is the semivertical angle of the cone then

$$
\frac{r}{l}=\sin \alpha \Rightarrow \frac{r}{3 r}=\sin \alpha \Rightarrow \sin \alpha=\frac{1}{3} \Rightarrow \alpha=\sin ^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) .
$$

Hence, the semivertical angle of a right cone of a given surface and maximum volume is $\sin ^{-1}(1 / 3)$.

### Example 19

Show that the surface area of a closed cuboid with square base and given volume is minimum when it is a cube.
[CBSE 2005]

#### Solution:

Let $V$ be the fixed volume of a closed cuboid with length $a$, breadth $a$ and height $h$.
Let $S$ be its surface area.
Then, $V=(a \times a \times h)$ or $h=\frac{V}{a^{2}}$ (i)

Now, $S=2\left(a^{2}+a h+a h\right)=2\left(a^{2}+2 a h\right)=2\left(a^{2}+\frac{2 V}{a}\right) \quad$ [using (i)]
i.e., $\quad S=2\left(a^{2}+\frac{2 V}{a}\right) . \quad \therefore \quad \frac{d S}{d a}=2\left(2 a-\frac{2 V}{a^{2}}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} S}{d a^{2}}=\left(4+\frac{8 V}{a^{3}}\right)$.
Now, $\quad \frac{d S}{d a}=0 \Rightarrow V=a^{3} \Rightarrow a \times a \times h=a^{3} \Rightarrow h=a$.
Now, when $h=a$, we have $V=a^{3}$.
$\therefore \quad\left[\frac{d^{2} S}{d a^{2}}\right]_{h=a}=\left(4+\frac{8 a^{3}}{a^{3}}\right)=12>0$.
So, $S$ is minimum when length $=a$, breadth $=a$ and height $=a$, i.e., when it is a cube.

### Example 20

Show that the height of a closed cylinder of given surface and maximum volume is equal to the diameter of its base.

#### Solution:

Let $S$ be the fixed surface area of a closed cylinder of radius $r$ and height $h$. Let $V$ be its volume.
Then, $\quad S=\left(2 \pi r^{2}+2 \pi r h\right) \Rightarrow h=\left(\frac{S-2 \pi r^{2}}{2 \pi r}\right)$ (i)

And, $\quad V=\pi r^{2} h=\pi r^{2}\left(\frac{S-2 \pi r^{2}}{2 \pi r}\right)=\left(\frac{r S-2 \pi r^{3}}{2}\right) \quad$ [using (i)].
$\Rightarrow \quad \frac{d V}{d r}=\left(\frac{S-6 \pi r^{2}}{2}\right)$ and $\frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}=-6 \pi r$.
Now, $\frac{d V}{d r}=0 \Rightarrow\left(S-6 \pi r^{2}\right)=0 \Rightarrow S=6 \pi r^{2}$

$$
\Rightarrow\left(2 \pi r^{2}+2 \pi r h\right)=6 \pi r^{2} \Rightarrow h=2 r, \text { i.e., } r=\frac{1}{2} h .
$$

And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} V}{d r^{2}}\right]_{r=(h / 2)}=-6 \pi \times \frac{1}{2} h=-3 \pi h<0$.
So, $V$ is maximum when $h=2 r$.

### Example 21

A closed right circular cylinder has a volume of 2156 cubic units. What should be the radius of its base so that its total surface area may be maximum?

#### Solution:

Let $r$ be the radius and $h$ the height of the cylinder.

And, $\left[\frac{d^{2} S}{d r^{2}}\right]_{r=7}=\left(4 \pi+\frac{8624}{343}\right)>0 . \quad \therefore \quad S$ is maximum when $r=7$.

### Example 22

Show that the height of the cylinder of maximum volume that can be inscribed in a sphere of radius $R$ is $\frac{2 R}{\sqrt{3}}$. Find the volume of the largest cylinder inscribed in a sphere of radius $R$. [CBSE 2006,'09C,'12C,'13C]

#### Solution:

Let $r$ be the radius and $h$ the height of the inscribed cylinder $A B C D$.
Let $V$ be its volume.
Then, $\quad V=\pi r^{2} h$ (i)

Clearly, $A C=2 R$.
Also, $\quad A C^{2}=A B^{2}+B C^{2}$

$$
\begin{align*}
& \Rightarrow(2 R)^{2}=(2 r)^{2}+h^{2} \\
& \Rightarrow r^{2}=\frac{1}{4}\left(4 R^{2}-h^{2}\right) \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$

$$

### Example 24

Prove that the perimeter of a right-angled triangle of given hypotenuse is maximum when the triangle is isosceles.

#### Solution:

Let $h$ be the hypotenuse and $\theta$ be the angle between the hypotenuse and the base.
Then, base $=h \cos \theta$ and altitude $=h \sin \theta$.
Let $P$ be the perimeter.
Then, $\quad P=h+h \cos \theta+h \sin \theta$.
$\therefore \frac{d P}{d \theta}=-h \sin \theta+h \cos \theta=h(\cos \theta-\sin \theta)$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_4c9510ea0d4c9f57f674g-198.jpg?height=247&width=217&top_left_y=1646&top_left_x=992)

And, $\frac{d^{2} P}{d \theta^{2}}=h(-\sin \theta-\cos \theta)=-h(\sin \theta+\cos \theta)$.
Now, $\frac{d P}{d \theta}=0 \Rightarrow h(\cos \theta-\sin \theta)=0$

$$
\Rightarrow \cos \theta=\sin \theta \text {, i.e., } \tan \theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=(\pi / 4) \text {. }
$$

Also, $\left[\frac{d^{2} P}{d \theta^{2}}\right]_{\theta=(\pi / 4)}=-h\left[\sin \frac{\pi}{4}+\cos \frac{\pi}{4}\right]=-h \sqrt{2}<0$.
$\therefore \quad P$ is maximum when $\theta=(\pi / 4)$.
In this case, base $=h \cos \frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}}$ and altitude $=h \sin \frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{h}{\sqrt{2}}$.
$\therefore$ base $=$ altitude , and hence the triangle is isosceles.

### Example 25

An open box is to be made out of a piece of cardboard measuring ( $24 \mathrm{~cm} \times 24 \mathrm{~cm}$ ) by cutting off equal squares from the corners and turning up the sides. Find the height of the box when it has maximum volume.
[CBSE 2004]

#### Solution:

Let the length of the side of each square cut off from the corners be $x \mathrm{~cm}$.
Then, height of the box $=x \mathrm{~cm}$.
$\therefore \quad V=(24-2 x)^{2} \times x=4 x^{3}-96 x^{2}+576 x$
$\Rightarrow \frac{d V}{d x}=12\left(x^{2}-16 x+48\right)$
and, $\frac{d^{2} V}{d x^{2}}=24(x-8)$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_4c9510ea0d4c9f57f674g-199.jpg?height=306&width=290&top_left_y=907&top_left_x=915)

Now, $\frac{d V}{d x}=0 \Rightarrow x^{2}-16 x+48=0$, i.e., $(x-12)(x-4)=0$

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \Rightarrow x=4 \quad[\because x \neq 12] \\
\left.\frac{d^{2} V}{d x^{2}}\right]_{x=4} & =-96<0 . \quad \therefore V \text { is maximum at } x=4 .
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, the volume of the box is maximum when its height is 4 cm .
