# Coordinate Geometry

## Distance Between Two Points

**Theorem 1:** The distance between two points $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ is given by the formula
$$
AB = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}
$$

**Proof:** Let $X'OX$ and $YOY'$ be the x-axis and y-axis respectively. Then, $O$ is the origin. Let $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ be the given points. Draw $AL \perp OX$, $BM \perp OX$ and $AN \perp BM$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_836ee4d67995a4306a0cg-01.jpg?height=410&width=537&top_left_y=801&top_left_x=378)

Now, $OL = x_1$, $OM = x_2$, $AL = y_1$ and $BM = y_2$.

$\therefore AN = LM = (OM - OL) = (x_2 - x_1)$,
$$
BN = (BM - NM) = (BM - AL) = (y_2 - y_1) \quad [\because NM=AL]
$$
From right $\triangle ANB$, by Pythagoras' theorem, we have
$$
AB^2 = (AN^2 + BN^2)
$$
$$
\Rightarrow AB^2 = (x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow AB = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}
$$
Hence, the distance between the points $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ is given by
$$
AB = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}
$$

**Corollary:** The distance of the point $P(x, y)$ from the origin $O(0,0)$ is given by
$$
OP = \sqrt{(x-0)^2 + (y-0)^2} \text{, i.e., } OP = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
$$

---

## Summary

1. The distance between the points $A(x_1, y_1)$ and $B(x_2, y_2)$ is given by
   $$
   AB = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}
   $$

2. The distance of the point $P(x, y)$ from the origin $O(0,0)$ is given by
   $$
   OP = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}
   $$

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Find the distance between the points $A(7,13)$ and $B(10,9)$.

#### Solution:

The given points are $A(7,13)$ and $B(10,9)$.
Then, $x_1=7, y_1=13$ and $x_2=10, y_2=9$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore AB & = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \\
& = \sqrt{(10-7)^2 + (9-13)^2} = \sqrt{3^2 + (-4)^2} \\
& = \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units.}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 2

Find the distance between the points $P(-4,7)$ and $Q(2,-5)$.

#### Solution:

The given points are $P(-4,7)$ and $Q(2,-5)$.
Then, $x_1=-4, y_1=7$ and $x_2=2, y_2=-5$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\therefore PQ & = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \\
& = \sqrt{\{2-(-4)\}^2 + (-5-7)^2} = \sqrt{6^2 + (-12)^2} \\
& = \sqrt{36+144} = \sqrt{180} = \sqrt{36 \times 5} = 6\sqrt{5} \text{ units.}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 3

Find the distance of the point $P(6,-6)$ from the origin.

#### Solution:

Let $P(6,-6)$ be the given point and $O(0,0)$ be the origin.
Then,
$$
\begin{aligned}
OP &= \sqrt{(6-0)^2 + (-6-0)^2} = \sqrt{6^2 + (-6)^2} \\
&= \sqrt{36+36} = \sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \times 2} = 6\sqrt{2} \text{ units.}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 4 [CBSE 2011]

Find the values of $y$ for which the distance between the points $A(3,-1)$ and $B(11, y)$ is 10 units.

#### Solution:

We have
$$
AB=10 \Rightarrow AB^2 = 100
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (11-3)^2 + (y+1)^2 = 100
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 8^2 + (y+1)^2 = 100
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (y+1)^2 = 100-64 = 36 = 6^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y+1 = \pm 6
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y+1=6 \text{ or } y+1=-6
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y=5 \text{ or } y=-7
$$
Hence, the required values of $y$ are **5** and **-7**.

### Example 5 [CBSE 2014]

If the point $P(k-1,2)$ is equidistant from the points $A(3, k)$ and $B(k, 5)$, find the values of $k$.

#### Solution:

It is being given that $P(k-1,2)$ is equidistant from the points $A(3, k)$ and $B(k, 5)$. So, we have
$$
PA = PB \Rightarrow PA^2 = PB^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (k-1-3)^2 + (2-k)^2 = (k-1-k)^2 + (2-5)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (k-4)^2 + (2-k)^2 = (-1)^2 + (-3)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 2k^2 - 12k + 20 = 1+9 \Rightarrow 2k^2 - 12k + 10 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow k^2 - 6k + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow k^2 - 5k - k + 5 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow k(k-5)-(k-5) = 0 \Rightarrow (k-5)(k-1) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow k=1 \text{ or } k=5
$$
Hence, the required values of $k$ are **1** and **5**.

### Example 6 [CBSE 2012]

Find the relation between $x$ and $y$ such that the point $P(x, y)$ is equidistant from the points $A(1,4)$ and $B(-1,2)$.

#### Solution:

We have
$$
PA = PB \Rightarrow PA^2 = PB^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-1)^2 + (y-4)^2 = (x+1)^2 + (y-2)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x^2 + y^2 - 2x - 8y + 17 = x^2 + y^2 + 2x - 4y + 5
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 4x + 4y - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow x+y=3 \Rightarrow y=3-x
$$
Hence, $y=3-x$ is the desired relation between $x$ and $y$.

## Two Important Notes

1.  Any point on the **x-axis** is of the form $(x, 0)$.
2.  Any point on the **y-axis** is of the form $(0, y)$.

### Example 7 [CBSE 2011]

Find those points on $x$-axis, each of which is at a distance of 5 units from the point $A(5,-3)$.

#### Solution:

Let the required point on the x-axis be $P(x, 0)$. Then,
$$
PA = 5 \Rightarrow PA^2 = 25
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-5)^2 + (0+3)^2 = 25
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x^2 - 10x + 25 + 9 = 25 \Rightarrow x^2 - 10x + 9 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x^2 - x - 9x + 9 = 0 \Rightarrow x(x-1) - 9(x-1) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-1)(x-9) = 0 \Rightarrow x-1=0 \text{ or } x-9=0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow x=1 \text{ or } x=9
$$
Hence, the required points on the x-axis are $B(1,0)$ and $C(9,0)$.

$$
$$
\Rightarrow (y-19)(y+5)=0 \Rightarrow y-19=0 \text{ or } y+5=0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y=19 \text{ or } y=-5

Hence, the required points on the y-axis are $B(0,19)$ and $C(0,-5)$.

### Example 9 [CBSE 2009]

Find the point on $x$-axis which is equidistant from the points $(5,-2)$ and $(-3, 2)$.

#### Solution:

Let the given points be $A(5,-2)$ and $B(-3,2)$ and let the required point be $P(x, 0)$. Then,
$$
PA = PB \Rightarrow PA^2 = PB^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-5)^2 + (0+2)^2 = (x+3)^2 + (0-2)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-5)^2 + 4 = (x+3)^2 + 4
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x-5)^2 - (x+3)^2 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (x^2 - 10x + 25) - (x^2 + 6x + 9) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow -16x + 16 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 16x = 16 \Rightarrow x=1
$$
Hence, the required point is $P(1,0)$.

### Example 11 [CBSE 2006]

Find the coordinates of the point equidistant from three given points $A(5,1), B(-3,-7)$ and $C(7,-1)$.

#### Solution:

Let the required point be $P(x, y)$. Then,
$$
PA = PB = PC \Rightarrow PA^2 = PB^2 = PC^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow PA^2=PB^2 \text{ and } PB^2=PC^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \begin{cases} (x-5)^2 + (y-1)^2 = (x+3)^2 + (y+7)^2, \\ (x+3)^2 + (y+7)^2 = (x-7)^2 + (y+1)^2 \end{cases}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \begin{cases} x^2+y^2-10x-2y+26 = x^2+y^2+6x+14y+58, \\ x^2+y^2+6x+14y+58 = x^2+y^2-14x+2y+50 \end{cases}
$$
Now, $x^2+y^2-10x-2y+26 = x^2+y^2+6x+14y+58$
$$
\Rightarrow 16x + 16y = -32 \Rightarrow x+y=-2 \tag{i}
$$
And, $x^2+y^2+6x+14y+58 = x^2+y^2-14x+2y+50$
$$
\Rightarrow 20x + 12y = -8 \Rightarrow 5x+3y=-2 \tag{ii}
$$
Multiplying (i) by 5 and subtracting (ii) from the result, we get
$$
2y = -8 \Rightarrow y=-4
$$
Putting $y=-4$ in (i), we get $x=2$.
$\therefore x=2$ and $y=-4$.
Hence, the required point is $P(2,-4)$.

### Example 12 [CBSE 2014]

Points $A(-1, y)$ and $B(5,7)$ lie on a circle with centre $O(2,-3 y)$. Find the values of $y$. Hence, find the radius of the circle.

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_836ee4d67995a4306a0cg-05.jpg?height=260&width=378&top_left_y=1504&top_left_x=817)

Since the radii of a circle are equal, we have
$$
OA=OB \Rightarrow OA^2 = OB^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (2+1)^2 + (-3y-y)^2 = (2-5)^2 + (-3y-7)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 3^2 + (-4y)^2 = (-3)^2 + (3y+7)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 9 + 16y^2 = 9 + 9y^2 + 49 + 42y
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 7y^2 - 42y - 49 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y^2 - 6y - 7 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y^2 - 7y + y - 7 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y(y-7) + (y-7) = 0 \Rightarrow (y-7)(y+1) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow y-7=0 \text{ or } y+1=0 \Rightarrow y=7 \text{ or } y=-1
$$
$\therefore$ centre is either $(2, -3(7)) = (2, -21)$ or $(2, -3(-1)) = (2, 3)$.

**Case I:** When centre is $(2,3)$
In this case, radius $= \sqrt{(5-2)^2 + (7-3)^2} = \sqrt{3^2+4^2}$
$$
= \sqrt{9+16} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units.}
$$

**Case II:** When centre is $(2,-21)$
In this case, radius $= \sqrt{(5-2)^2 + \{7-(-21)\}^2}$
$$
= \sqrt{3^2 + (28)^2} = \sqrt{9+784}
$$
$$
= \sqrt{793} \approx 28.1 \text{ units.}
$$

Find the centre of a circle passing through the points $(6,-6)$, $(3,-7)$ and $(3,3)$.

### Example 14 [CBSE 2015]

The points $A(4,7), B(p, 3)$ and $C(7,3)$ are the vertices of a right triangle, right angled at $B$. Find the value of $p$.

#### Solution:

Since $\triangle ABC$ is a right angled at $B$, we have
$$
AB^2 + BC^2 = AC^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \{(p-4)^2+(3-7)^2\} + \{(7-p)^2+(3-3)^2\} = (7-4)^2+(3-7)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (p-4)^2+(-4)^2+(7-p)^2+0 = 3^2+(-4)^2
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (p^2 - 8p + 16) + 16 + (49 - 14p + p^2) = 9 + 16
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 2p^2 - 22p + 81 = 25
$$
$$
\Rightarrow 2p^2 - 22p + 56 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow p^2 - 11p + 28 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow p^2 - 7p - 4p + 28 = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow p(p-7)-4(p-7) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow (p-7)(p-4) = 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow p-7=0 \text{ or } p-4=0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow p=7 \text{ or } p=4
$$
When $p=7$ then the points $B$ and $C$ coincide and so no triangle is formed in this case. So, $p \neq 7$.
Hence, **p = 4**.

### Example 15 [CBSE 2015]

Show that the points $(a, a),(-a,-a)$ and $(-\sqrt{3} a, \sqrt{3} a)$ are the vertices of an equilateral triangle. Find its area.

#### Solution:

Let the given points be $A(a, a), B(-a,-a)$ and $C(-\sqrt{3}a, \sqrt{3}a)$. Then,
$$
AB^2 = (-a-a)^2+(-a-a)^2 = (-2a)^2+(-2a)^2 = 8a^2
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
BC^2 & = (-\sqrt{3}a+a)^2 + (\sqrt{3}a+a)^2 \\
& = (a-\sqrt{3}a)^2+(a+\sqrt{3}a)^2 = 2(a^2+3a^2) = 8a^2
\end{aligned}
$$
and
$$
\begin{aligned}
AC^2 & = (-\sqrt{3}a-a)^2 + (\sqrt{3}a-a)^2 = (\sqrt{3}a+a)^2+(\sqrt{3}a-a)^2 \\
& = 2(3a^2+a^2) = 8a^2
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore AB = BC = AC = \sqrt{8a^2} = 2\sqrt{2}a$.
Hence, $\triangle ABC$ is equilateral and each of its sides is $2\sqrt{2}a$.

Area of equilateral $\triangle ABC$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& = \left\{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \times (\text{side})^2\right\} \text{ sq units} = \left\{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \times (2\sqrt{2}a)^2\right\} \text{ sq units} \\
& = \left\{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \times 8a^2\right\} \text{ sq units} = 2\sqrt{3}a^2 \text{ sq units.}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 16

Prove that the points $A(-3,0), B(1,-3)$ and $C(4,1)$ are the vertices of an isosceles right-angled triangle. Find the area of this triangle.

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_836ee4d67995a4306a0cg-08.jpg?height=250&width=229&top_left_y=165&top_left_x=962)

Let $A(-3,0), B(1,-3)$ and $C(4,1)$ be the given points. Then,
$$
AB = \sqrt{[1-(-3)]^2+(-3-0)^2} = \sqrt{4^2+(-3)^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units}
$$
$$
BC = \sqrt{(4-1)^2+[1-(-3)]^2} = \sqrt{3^2+4^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5 \text{ units}
$$
and
$$
AC = \sqrt{[4-(-3)]^2+(1-0)^2} = \sqrt{7^2+1^2} = \sqrt{50} = 5\sqrt{2} \text{ units}
$$
Thus, $AB = BC = 5$ units.
$\therefore \triangle ABC$ is **isosceles**.

Also, $(AB^2 + BC^2) = (5^2+5^2) = 50$ and $AC^2 = (5\sqrt{2})^2 = 50$.
Thus, $AB^2 + BC^2 = AC^2$.
This shows that $\triangle ABC$ is **right-angled at B**.

In $\triangle ABC$, we have base $= BC = 5$ units and height $= AB = 5$ units.
$\therefore$ area of $\triangle ABC = \left(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 5\right) \text{ sq units} = 12.5 \text{ sq units.}$

## Properties of Various Types of Quadrilaterals

A quadrilateral is a
1.  **rectangle** if its opposite sides are equal and the diagonals are equal.
2.  **square** if all its sides are equal and the diagonals are equal.
3.  **parallelogram** if its opposite sides are equal.
4.  **parallelogram but not a rectangle** if its opposite sides are equal and the diagonals are not equal.
5.  **rhombus but not a square** if all its sides are equal and the diagonals are not equal.

### Example 17 [CBSE 2009C]

Show that the points $(1,1),(-1,5),(7,9)$ and $(9,5)$ taken in that order are the vertices of a rectangle. Also, find the area of the rectangle.

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_836ee4d67995a4306a0cg-08.jpg?height=235&width=370&top_left_y=1403&top_left_x=825)

Let $A(1,1), B(-1,5), C(7,9)$ and $D(9,5)$ be the vertices of quad. $ABCD$. Then,
$$
AB^2 = (-1-1)^2 + (5-1)^2 = (-2)^2+4^2 = 4+16=20 \Rightarrow AB = 2\sqrt{5} \text{ units.}
$$
$$
BC^2 = (7+1)^2 + (9-5)^2 = 8^2+4^2 = 64+16=80 \Rightarrow BC = 4\sqrt{5} \text{ units.}
$$
$$
CD^2 = (9-7)^2 + (5-9)^2 = 2^2+(-4)^2 = 4+16=20 \Rightarrow CD = 2\sqrt{5} \text{ units.}
$$
$$
AD^2 = (9-1)^2 + (5-1)^2 = 8^2+4^2 = 64+16=80 \Rightarrow AD = 4\sqrt{5} \text{ units.}
$$
Thus, $AB = CD = 2\sqrt{5}$ units and $BC = AD = 4\sqrt{5}$ units. The opposite sides are equal.

Also,
$$
AC^2 = (7-1)^2+(9-1)^2 = 6^2+8^2=36+64=100 \Rightarrow AC = 10 \text{ units.}
$$
And,
$$
BD^2 = (9+1)^2+(5-5)^2 = 10^2+0^2 = 100 \Rightarrow BD = 10 \text{ units.}
$$
$\therefore$ diagonal $AC =$ diagonal $BD$.
Thus, $ABCD$ is a quadrilateral whose opposite sides are equal and the diagonals are equal.
Hence, quad. $ABCD$ is a **rectangle**.

Area of rectangle $ABCD =$ length $\times$ breadth
$$
= 4\sqrt{5} \times 2\sqrt{5} \text{ sq units} = 40 \text{ sq units.}
$$

### Example 18 [CBSE 2012]

Show that the points $A(3,5), B(6,0), C(1,-3)$ and $D(-2,2)$ are the vertices of a square $ABCD$.

#### Solution:

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_836ee4d67995a4306a0cg-09.jpg?height=233&width=261&top_left_y=1145&top_left_x=938)

Let $A(3,5), B(6,0), C(1,-3)$ and $D(-2,2)$ be the angular points of a quadrilateral $ABCD$.
$$
AB = \sqrt{(6-3)^2+(0-5)^2} = \sqrt{3^2+(-5)^2} = \sqrt{9+25} = \sqrt{34} \text{ units}
$$
$$
BC = \sqrt{(1-6)^2+(-3-0)^2} = \sqrt{(-5)^2+(-3)^2} = \sqrt{25+9} = \sqrt{34} \text{ units}
$$
$$
CD = \sqrt{(-2-1)^2+(2+3)^2} = \sqrt{(-3)^2+5^2} = \sqrt{9+25} = \sqrt{34} \text{ units}
$$
$$
DA = \sqrt{(3+2)^2+(5-2)^2} = \sqrt{5^2+3^2} = \sqrt{25+9} = \sqrt{34} \text{ units}
$$
Thus, $AB=BC=CD=DA$. All sides are equal.

Diagonals:
$$
AC = \sqrt{(1-3)^2+(-3-5)^2} = \sqrt{(-2)^2+(-8)^2} = \sqrt{4+64} = \sqrt{68} = 2\sqrt{17} \text{ units}
$$
$$
BD = \sqrt{(-2-6)^2+(2-0)^2} = \sqrt{(-8)^2+2^2} = \sqrt{64+4} = \sqrt{68} = 2\sqrt{17} \text{ units}
$$
$\therefore$ diag. $AC =$ diag. $BD$.
Thus, $ABCD$ is a quadrilateral in which all sides are equal and the diagonals are equal.
Hence, quad. $ABCD$ is a **square**.

### Example 19 [CBSE 2013]

If $A(2,-1), B(3,4), C(-2,3)$ and $D(-3,-2)$ be four points in a plane, show that $ABCD$ is a rhombus but not a square. Find the area of the rhombus.

#### Solution:

Let $A(2,-1), B(3,4), C(-2,3)$ and $D(-3,-2)$ be the angular points of a quad. $ABCD$.
$$
AB = \sqrt{(3-2)^2+(4+1)^2} = \sqrt{1^2+5^2} = \sqrt{26} \text{ units}
$$
$$
BC = \sqrt{(-2-3)^2+(3-4)^2} = \sqrt{(-5)^2+(-1)^2} = \sqrt{26} \text{ units}
$$
$$
CD = \sqrt{(-3+2)^2+(-2-3)^2} = \sqrt{(-1)^2+(-5)^2} = \sqrt{26} \text{ units}
$$
$$
DA = \sqrt{(-3-2)^2+(-2+1)^2} = \sqrt{(-5)^2+(-1)^2} = \sqrt{26} \text{ units}
$$
$\therefore AB = BC = CD = DA = \sqrt{26}$ units. All sides are equal.

Now, diagonals:
$$
AC = \sqrt{(-2-2)^2+(3+1)^2} = \sqrt{(-4)^2+4^2} = \sqrt{32} = 4\sqrt{2} \text{ units}
$$
and
$$
BD = \sqrt{(-3-3)^2+(-2-4)^2} = \sqrt{(-6)^2+(-6)^2} = \sqrt{72} = 6\sqrt{2} \text{ units}
$$
$\therefore$ diagonal $AC \neq$ diagonal $BD$.
Thus, $ABCD$ is a quadrilateral in which all sides are equal but diagonals are not equal.
$\therefore ABCD$ is a **rhombus but not a square**.

Area of the rhombus $ABCD = \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{product of diagonals})$
$$
= \left(\frac{1}{2} \times AC \times BD\right)
$$
$$
= \left(\frac{1}{2} \times 4\sqrt{2} \times 6\sqrt{2}\right) \text{ sq units}
$$
$$
= 24 \text{ sq units.}
$$
Hence, the area of rhombus $ABCD$ is **24 sq units**.

## Collinear Points

Three points $A, B, C$ are said to be **collinear** if they lie on the same straight line.

---

## Test for Collinearity of Three Points

In order to show that three given points $A, B, C$ are collinear, we find the distances $AB, BC$ and $AC$. If the sum of any two of these distances is equal to the third distance then the given points are collinear.

---

### Example 20

Prove that the points $A(1,1), B(-2,7)$ and $C(3,-3)$ are collinear.

#### Solution:

Let $A(1,1), B(-2,7)$ and $C(3,-3)$ be the given points. Then,
$$
AB = \sqrt{(-2-1)^2+(7-1)^2} = \sqrt{(-3)^2+6^2} = \sqrt{45} = 3\sqrt{5} \text{ units}
$$
$$
BC = \sqrt{(3+2)^2+(-3-7)^2} = \sqrt{5^2+(-10)^2} = \sqrt{125} = 5\sqrt{5} \text{ units}
$$
$$
AC = \sqrt{(3-1)^2+(-3-1)^2} = \sqrt{2^2+(-4)^2} = \sqrt{20} = 2\sqrt{5} \text{ units}
$$
$\therefore AB + AC = (3\sqrt{5} + 2\sqrt{5}) \text{ units} = 5\sqrt{5} \text{ units} = BC$.
Thus, $AB + AC = BC$.
Hence, the given points $A, B, C$ are **collinear**.
