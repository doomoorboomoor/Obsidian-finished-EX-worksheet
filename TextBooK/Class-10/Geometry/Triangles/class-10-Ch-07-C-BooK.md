## RATIO OF THE AREAS OF TWO SIMILAR TRIANGLES

### THEOREM 1

**The ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.**
[CBSE 2002, '02C, '04, '05, '05C, '06, '06C, '07, '08, '08C, '09C, '10]

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$.

**TO PROVE:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}}$.

**CONSTRUCTION:** Draw $AL \perp BC$ and $DM \perp EF$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-052.jpg?height=180&width=207&top_left_y=1212&top_left_x=396)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-052.jpg?height=231&width=265&top_left_y=1165&top_left_x=636)

**PROOF:** Since $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$, it follows that they are equiangular and their sides are proportional.

$$
\therefore \quad \angle A=\angle D, \angle B=\angle E, \angle C=\angle F \text { and } \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{BC}{EF}=\frac{AC}{DF} \tag{i}
$$

Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times AL\right)$ and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)=\left(\frac{1}{2} \times EF \times DM\right)$.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times AL}{\frac{1}{2} \times EF \times DM}=\frac{BC}{EF} \times \frac{AL}{DM} \tag{ii}
$$

Also, $\frac{AL}{DM}=\frac{BC}{EF}$ ... (iii) $[\because$ in similar triangles, the ratio of the corresponding sides is the same as the ratio of corresponding altitudes]

Using (iii) in (ii), we get

$$
\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\left(\frac{BC}{EF} \times \frac{BC}{EF}\right)=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}}
$$

Similarly, $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}$ and $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}}$.

Hence, $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}}$.

### THEOREM 2

**If the areas of two similar triangles are equal then prove that the triangles are congruent.**
[CBSE 2010]

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ such that $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)$.

**TO PROVE:** $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$.

**PROOF:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ (given)

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}} \tag{i}
$$

Now, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)$ [given]

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=1 \tag{ii}
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\begin{align*}
& \frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}}=1 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & AB^{2}=DE^{2}, AC^{2}=DF^{2} \text { and } BC^{2}=EF^{2} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & AB=DE, AC=DF \text { and } BC=EF \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF \quad \text {[by SSS-congruency]}
\end{align*}
$$

### THEOREM 3

**Prove that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding altitudes.**

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$, $AL \perp BC$ and $DM \perp EF$.

**TO PROVE:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AL^{2}}{DM^{2}}$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-054.jpg?height=215&width=220&top_left_y=388&top_left_x=640)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-054.jpg?height=269&width=293&top_left_y=334&top_left_x=902)

**PROOF:** We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of the corresponding sides.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}} \tag{i}
$$

Now, in $\triangle ALB$ and $\triangle DME$, we have

$\angle ALB=\angle DME=90^{\circ}$ and $\angle B=\angle E$ $[\because \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF]$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad & \triangle ALB \sim \triangle DME \quad \text{[by AA-similarity]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{AL}{DM} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AL^{2}}{DM^{2}} \tag{ii}
\end{align*}
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AL^{2}}{DM^{2}}
$$

### THEOREM 4

**Prove that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding medians.**

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$, $AP$ and $DQ$ are the medians of $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ respectively.

**TO PROVE:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AP^{2}}{DQ^{2}}$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-054.jpg?height=236&width=309&top_left_y=1514&top_left_x=886)

**PROOF:** We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}} \tag{i}
$$

Now, $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{BC}{EF}=\frac{2 BP}{2 EQ}=\frac{BP}{EQ}
$$

$\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{BP}{EQ}$ and $\angle B=\angle E$ $[\because \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF]$

$\Rightarrow \quad \triangle APB \sim \triangle DQE$ [by SAS-similarity]

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{AP}{DQ}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AP^{2}}{DQ^{2}} \tag{ii}
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AP^{2}}{DQ^{2}}
$$

### THEOREM 5

**Prove that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding angle-bisector segments.**

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ in which $AX$ and $DY$ are the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle D$ respectively.

**TO PROVE:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AX^{2}}{DY^{2}}$.

**PROOF:** We know that ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}} \tag{i}
$$

$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$

$\Rightarrow \quad \angle A=\angle D$
$\Rightarrow \quad \frac{1}{2} \angle A=\frac{1}{2} \angle D$
$\Rightarrow \quad \angle BAX=\angle EDY$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-055.jpg?height=164&width=198&top_left_y=1240&top_left_x=644)
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-055.jpg?height=237&width=314&top_left_y=1167&top_left_x=881)

Now, in $\triangle ABX$ and $\triangle DEY$, we have $\angle BAX=\angle EDY$ and $\angle B=\angle E$ $[\because \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF]$.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ABX \sim \triangle DEY$ [by AA-similarity]

$$
\frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{AX}{DY}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{AX^{2}}{DY^{2}} \tag{ii}
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AX^{2}}{DY^{2}}
$$

## SOLVED EXAMPLES

### Example 1

If the areas of two similar triangles are in the ratio $25:64$, find the ratio of their corresponding sides. [CBSE 2009]

#### Solution

Let $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ be similar. Then,

$$
\begin{align*}
& \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{DE^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{EF^{2}}=\frac{AC^{2}}{DF^{2}} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \left(\frac{AB}{DE}\right)^{2}=\left(\frac{BC}{EF}\right)^{2}=\left(\frac{AC}{DF}\right)^{2}=\frac{25}{64}=\left(\frac{5}{8}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AB}{DE}=\frac{BC}{EF}=\frac{AC}{DF}=\frac{5}{8}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, the ratio of their corresponding sides is **5:8**.

In the adjoining figure, $S$ and $T$ are points on the sides $PQ$ and $PR$ respectively of $\triangle PQR$ such that $PT=2 \text{ cm}$, $TR=4 \text{ cm}$ and $ST$ is parallel to $QR$. Find the ratio of the areas of $\triangle PST$ and $\triangle PQR$. [CBSE 2010]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-056.jpg?height=252&width=289&top_left_y=745&top_left_x=906)

#### Solution

$ST \| QR$ (given)

$\therefore \quad \angle PST=\angle PQR$ **[corresponding angles]**
and $\angle PTS=\angle PRQ$ **[corresponding angles]**.

And so, $\triangle PST \sim \triangle PQR$ **[by AA-similarity]**.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle PST)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle PQR)} & =\frac{PT^{2}}{PR^{2}}=\frac{PT^{2}}{(PT+TR)^{2}} \\
& =\frac{2^{2}}{(2+4)^{2}}=\frac{4}{36}=\frac{1}{9}
\end{align*}
$$

### Example 3

The areas of two similar triangles $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle PQR$ are $25 \text{ cm}^{2}$ and $49 \text{ cm}^{2}$ respectively. If $QR=9.8 \text{ cm}$, find $BC$. [CBSE 2006]

#### Solution

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad & \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle PQR)}=\frac{BC^{2}}{QR^{2}} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \left(\frac{BC}{QR}\right)^{2}=\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle PQR)}=\frac{25}{49}=\left(\frac{5}{7}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{BC}{QR}=\frac{5}{7} \Rightarrow \frac{BC}{9.8}=\frac{5}{7} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & BC=\frac{5 \times 9.8}{7} \text{ cm}=5 \times 1.4 \text{ cm}=7 \text{ cm}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, **BC = 7 cm**.

### Example 4

The areas of two similar triangles are $81 \text{ cm}^{2}$ and $49 \text{ cm}^{2}$ respectively. If the altitude of the bigger triangle is $4.5 \text{ cm}$, find the corresponding altitude of the smaller triangle. [CBSE 2002]

#### Solution

Let the given triangles be $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ such that $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=81 \text{ cm}^{2}$ and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)=49 \text{ cm}^{2}$. Let $AL$ and $DM$ be the corresponding altitudes of $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ respectively. Then, $AL=4.5 \text{ cm}$.

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding altitudes.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad & \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AL^{2}}{DM^{2}} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \left(\frac{AL}{DM}\right)^{2}=\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{81}{49}=\left(\frac{9}{7}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AL}{DM}=\frac{9}{7} \Rightarrow \frac{4.5}{DM}=\frac{9}{7} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & DM=\frac{4.5 \times 7}{9} \text{ cm}=\frac{7}{2} \text{ cm}=3.5 \text{ cm}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, the altitude of the smaller triangle is **3.5 cm**.

### Example 5

The areas of two similar triangles are $121 \text{ cm}^{2}$ and $64 \text{ cm}^{2}$ respectively. If the median of first triangle is $12.1 \text{ cm}$, find the corresponding median of the other. [CBSE 2001]

#### Solution

Let $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ such that $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=121 \text{ cm}^{2}$ and $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)=64 \text{ cm}^{2}$.

Let $AP$ and $DQ$ be the corresponding medians of $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ respectively such that $AP=12.1 \text{ cm}$.

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding medians.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad & \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{AP^{2}}{DQ^{2}} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \left(\frac{AP}{DQ}\right)^{2}=\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}=\frac{121}{64}=\left(\frac{11}{8}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AP}{DQ}=\frac{11}{8} \Rightarrow \frac{12.1}{DQ}=\frac{11}{8} \quad [\because AP=12.1 \text{ cm}] \\
\Rightarrow \quad & DQ=\frac{(12.1 \times 8)}{11} \text{ cm}=(1.1 \times 8) \text{ cm}=8.8 \text{ cm}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, the corresponding median is **8.8 cm**.

### Example 6

$\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ in which $AX$ and $DY$ are the bisectors of $\angle A$ and $\angle D$ respectively. If $AX=6.5 \text{ cm}$ and $DY=5.2 \text{ cm}$, find the ratio of the areas of $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$.

#### Solution

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of the corresponding angle-bisector segments.

$$

$$

$\therefore \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC): \operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)=25:16$.

### Example 7

In the given figure, the line segment $XY$ is parallel to side $AC$ of $\triangle ABC$ and it divides the triangle into two parts of equal area. Prove that $AX:AB=(2-\sqrt{2}):2$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-058.jpg?height=277&width=342&top_left_y=789&top_left_x=857)

#### Solution

Since $XY \| AC$, we have

$\angle A=\angle BXY$ and $\angle C=\angle BYX$ **[corresponding angles]**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ABC \sim \triangle XBY$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle XBY)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{XB^{2}} \tag{i}
$$

But, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)=2 \times \operatorname{ar}(\triangle XBY)$ [given]

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle XBY)}=2 \tag{ii}
$$

From (i) and (ii), we get

$$
\begin{align*}
& \frac{AB^{2}}{XB^{2}}=2 \Rightarrow\left(\frac{AB}{XB}\right)^{2}=2 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AB}{XB}=\sqrt{2} \Rightarrow AB=\sqrt{2}(XB) \\
\Rightarrow \quad & AB=\sqrt{2}(AB-AX) \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \sqrt{2} AX=(\sqrt{2}-1) AB \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{AX}{AB}=\frac{(\sqrt{2}-1)}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{\sqrt{2}}{\sqrt{2}}=\frac{(2-\sqrt{2})}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, $AX:AB=(2-\sqrt{2}):2$.

### Example 8

In the given figure, $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DBC$ are on the same base $BC$. If $AD$ intersects $BC$ at $O$, prove that $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)}=\frac{AO}{DO}$. [CBSE 2000, '05, '07C]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-059.jpg?height=315&width=269&top_left_y=171&top_left_x=926)

#### Solution

**GIVEN:** $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DBC$ are on the same base $BC$ and $AD$ intersects $BC$ at $O$.

**TO PROVE:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)}=\frac{AO}{DO}$.

**CONSTRUCTION:** Draw $AL \perp BC$ and $DM \perp BC$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-059.jpg?height=367&width=324&top_left_y=604&top_left_x=871)

**PROOF:** In $\triangle ALO$ and $\triangle DMO$, we have

$\angle ALO=\angle DMO=90^{\circ}$ and $\angle AOL=\angle DOM$ **(vertically opposite angles)**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ALO \sim \triangle DMO$ **[by AA-similarity]**

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{AL}{DM}=\frac{AO}{DO} \tag{i}
$$

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)}=\frac{\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times AL}{\frac{1}{2} \times BC \times DM}=\frac{AL}{DM}=\frac{AO}{DO} \quad [\text{using (i)}]
$$

Hence, $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBC)}=\frac{AO}{DO}$.

### Example 9

$ABCD$ is a trapezium in which $AB \| DC$ and $AB=2DC$. If the diagonals of the trapezium intersect each other at a point $O$, find the ratio of the areas of $\triangle AOB$ and $\triangle COD$.

#### Solution

**GIVEN:** A trapezium $ABCD$ in which $AB \| DC$ and $AB=2DC$. Its diagonals intersect each other at the point $O$.

**TO FIND:** $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle AOB)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle COD)}$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-059.jpg?height=229&width=344&top_left_y=1602&top_left_x=847)

**METHOD OF SOLUTION:** In $\triangle AOB$ and $\triangle COD$, we have

$\angle AOB=\angle COD$ **[vertically opposite angles]**
$\angle OAB=\angle OCD$ **[alternate interior angles]**

$\therefore \quad \triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD$ **[by AA-similarity]**.

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of the corresponding sides.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle AOB)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle COD)} & =\frac{AB^{2}}{DC^{2}}=\frac{(2 \times DC)^{2}}{DC^{2}} \quad [\because AB=2DC] \\
& =\frac{4 \times DC^{2}}{DC^{2}}=\frac{4}{1}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle AOB): \operatorname{ar}(\triangle COD)=4:1$.

### Example 10

In a trapezium $ABCD$, $O$ is the point of intersection of $AC$ and $BD$, $AB \| CD$ and $AB=2 \times CD$. If the area of $\triangle AOB=84 \text{ cm}^{2}$, find the area of $\triangle COD$. [CBSE 2005, '09]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-060.jpg?height=202&width=342&top_left_y=807&top_left_x=853)

#### Solution

In $\triangle AOB$ and $\triangle COD$, we have

$\angle OAB = \angle OCD$ **[alternate interior angles]**
and $\angle OBA = \angle ODC$ **[alternate interior angles]**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD$ **[by AA-similarity]**

$$
\begin{align*}
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle AOB)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle COD)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{CD^{2}}=\frac{(2CD)^{2}}{CD^{2}} \quad [\because AB=2 \times CD] \\
& =\frac{4 \times CD^{2}}{CD^{2}}=4 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \operatorname{ar}(\triangle COD)=\frac{1}{4} \times \operatorname{ar}(\triangle AOB)=\left(\frac{1}{4} \times 84\right) \text{ cm}^{2}=21 \text{ cm}^{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, the area of $\triangle COD$ is **21 cm²**.

### Example 11

$D, E$ and $F$ are respectively the midpoints of sides $AB, BC$ and $CA$ of $\triangle ABC$. Find the ratio of the areas of $\triangle DEF$ and $\triangle ABC$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-060.jpg?height=260&width=346&top_left_y=1643&top_left_x=849)

#### Solution

In $\triangle ABC$, $D$ and $F$ are the midpoints of sides $AB$ and $CA$ respectively.

$\therefore \quad DF \| BC$ **[by midpoint theorem]**
$\Rightarrow DF \| BE$.

Similarly, $EF \| BD$.

$\therefore \quad BEFD$ is a parallelogram
$\Rightarrow \angle B=\angle EFD$, $EF=BD=\frac{1}{2} AB$
and $DF=BE=\frac{1}{2} BC$.

Also, $ECFD$ is a parallelogram
$\Rightarrow \angle EDF=\angle C$.

Now, in $\triangle DEF$ and $\triangle CAB$, we have

$\angle EFD = \angle B$
and $\angle EDF = \angle C$.

$\therefore \quad \triangle DEF \sim \triangle CAB$ **[by AA-similarity]**.

And so, $\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}=\frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DEF)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle CAB)}=\frac{DF^{2}}{BC^{2}}=\frac{\left(\frac{1}{2} BC\right)^{2}}{BC^{2}}=\frac{1}{4}$.

### Example 12

Prove that the area of an equilateral triangle described on one side of a square is equal to half the area of an equilateral triangle described on one of its diagonals. [CBSE 2005C]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-061.jpg?height=337&width=505&top_left_y=1131&top_left_x=694)

#### Solution

**GIVEN:** A square $ABCD$ and equilateral $\triangle BCE$ and $ACF$ have been described on side $BC$ and diagonal $AC$ respectively.

**TO PROVE:** $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BCE)=\frac{1}{2} \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ACF)$.

**PROOF:** Since each of the $\triangle BCE$ and $\triangle ACF$ is an equilateral triangle, so each angle of each one of them is $60^{\circ}$. So, the triangles are equiangular, and hence similar.

$\therefore \quad \triangle BCE \sim \triangle ACF$.

We know that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.

$$
\begin{align*}
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BCE)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ACF)} & =\frac{BC^{2}}{AC^{2}}=\frac{BC^{2}}{2(BC)^{2}} \quad [\because AC=\sqrt{2} BC] \\
& =\frac{1}{2}
\end{align*}
$$

Hence, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle BCE)=\frac{1}{2} \times \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ACF)$.

### Example 13

Prove that the area of an equilateral triangle described on a side of a right-angled isosceles triangle is half the area of the equilateral triangle described on its hypotenuse. [CBSE 2006]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-062.jpg?height=247&width=374&top_left_y=376&top_left_x=823)

#### Solution

**GIVEN:** A $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle ABC=90^{\circ}$ and $AB=BC$. $\triangle ABD$ and $\triangle CAE$ are equilateral triangles.

**TO PROVE:** $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)=\frac{1}{2} \times \operatorname{ar}(\triangle CAE)$.

**PROOF:** Let $AB=BC=x$ units.

$\therefore$ hypotenuse $CA=\sqrt{x^{2}+x^{2}}=x \sqrt{2}$ units.

Each of the $\triangle ABD$ and $CAE$ being equilateral, each angle of each one of them is $60^{\circ}$.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ABD \sim \triangle CAE$ **[by AAA-similarity]**.

But, the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the ratio of the squares of their corresponding sides.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle CAE)}=\frac{AB^{2}}{CA^{2}}=\frac{x^{2}}{(x \sqrt{2})^{2}}=\frac{x^{2}}{2 x^{2}}=\frac{1}{2}
$$

Hence, $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABD)=\frac{1}{2} \times \operatorname{ar}(\triangle CAE)$.

### Example 14

If $D$ is a point on the side $AB$ of $\triangle ABC$ such that $AD:DB=3:2$ and $E$ is a point on $BC$ such that $DE \| AC$, find the ratio of the areas of $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DBE$. [CBSE 2008C]

#### Solution

Let $AD=3x \text{ cm}$ and $DB=2x \text{ cm}$.

Then, $AB=(AD+DB)=(3x+2x) \text{ cm}=5x \text{ cm}$.

In $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DBE$, we have
$\angle CAB=\angle EDB$ **[corresponding angles]**
and $\angle ACB=\angle DEB$ **[corresponding angles]**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DBE$ **[by AA-similarity]**

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBE)}=\frac{(AB)^{2}}{(DB)^{2}}=\frac{(5x)^{2}}{(2x)^{2}}=\frac{25 x^{2}}{4 x^{2}}=\frac{25}{4}
$$

$\Rightarrow \quad \operatorname{ar}(\triangle ABC): \operatorname{ar}(\triangle DBE)=25:4$.

### Example 15

In the given figure, $DE \| BC$ and $AD:DB=5:4$. Find the ratio $\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DFE): \operatorname{ar}(\triangle CFB)$. [CBSE 2000]

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_97258cbb4988c33e0722g-063.jpg?height=272&width=287&top_left_y=169&top_left_x=910)

#### Solution

Let $AD=5x \text{ cm}$ and $DB=4x \text{ cm}$. Then,
$AB=AD+DB=5x \text{ cm}+4x \text{ cm}=9x \text{ cm}$

In $\triangle ADE$ and $\triangle ABC$, we have

$\angle ADE=\angle ABC$ **[corresponding angles]**
$\angle AED=\angle ACB$ **[corresponding angles]**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC$ **[by AA-similarity]**

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{DE}{BC}=\frac{AD}{AB}=\frac{5x}{9x}=\frac{5}{9} \tag{i}
$$

In $\triangle DFE$ and $\triangle CFB$, we have

$\angle EDF=\angle BCF$ **[alternate interior angles]**
and $\angle DEF=\angle CBF$ **[alternate interior angles]**.

$\therefore \quad \triangle DFE \sim \triangle CFB$

$$
\begin{align*}
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle DFE)}{\operatorname{ar}(\triangle CFB)}=\frac{DE^{2}}{CB^{2}}=\frac{DE^{2}}{BC^{2}}=\left(\frac{DE}{BC}\right)^{2}=\left(\frac{5}{9}\right)^{2}=\frac{25}{81} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \operatorname{ar}(\triangle DFE): \operatorname{ar}(\triangle CFB)=25:81
\end{align*}
$$
