# Trigonometric Ratios

**Trigonometry** is that branch of mathematics which deals with the measurement of angles and the problems allied with angles.

---

## Trigonometric Ratios (T-Ratios) of an Acute Angle of a Right Triangle

Let $\angle BAC = \theta$ be an acute angle of a right-angled $\triangle ABC$. In right-angled $\triangle ABC$, let:
- **Base** $= AB = x$ units
- **Perpendicular** $= BC = y$ units
- **Hypotenuse** $= AC = r$ units

We define the following ratios, known as **Trigonometric Ratios** for $\theta$.

- **sine $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{perpendicular}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{y}{r}$, and is written as $\sin \theta$.
- **cosine $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{base}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{x}{r}$, and is written as $\cos \theta$.
- **tangent $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{perpendicular}}{\text{base}} = \frac{y}{x}$, and is written as $\tan \theta$.
- **cosecant $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{perpendicular}} = \frac{r}{y}$, and is written as $\operatorname{cosec} \theta$.
- **secant $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{base}} = \frac{r}{x}$, and is written as $\sec \theta$.
- **cotangent $\theta$**: $\frac{\text{base}}{\text{perpendicular}} = \frac{x}{y}$, and is written as $\cot \theta$.

---

## Reciprocal Relation

Clearly, we have:
- $\operatorname{cosec} \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}$
- $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}$

---

## Summary

Consider a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle A = \theta$.
Let $AB = x$, $BC = y$ and $AC = r$. Then,
- $\sin \theta = \frac{y}{r}$
- $\cos \theta = \frac{x}{r}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{y}{x}$
- $\operatorname{cosec} \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta} = \frac{r}{y}$
- $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} = \frac{r}{x}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta} = \frac{x}{y}$

Also, we have the reciprocal relations:
- $\operatorname{cosec} \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta}$
- $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta}$

## T-Ratios of an Angle are Well-Defined

### Theorem

*Show that the value of each of the trigonometric ratios of an angle does not depend on the size of the triangle. It only depends on the angle.*

**PROOF:**
Consider a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$.
Take a point $P$ on $AC$ and draw $PQ \perp AB$.
Then, $\triangle AQP$ is similar to $\triangle ABC$.

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_cc52b83dc44bbcf3c9f8g-02.jpg?height=244&width=324&top_left_y=1079&top_left_x=871)

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{AQ}{AB} = \frac{AP}{AC} = \frac{PQ}{CB}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{PQ}{AP} = \frac{CB}{AC} = \sin \theta .
$$

Similarly,
$$
\frac{AQ}{AP} = \frac{AB}{AC} = \cos \theta
$$
and
$$
\frac{PQ}{AQ} = \frac{CB}{AB} = \tan \theta .
$$
Similarly, if we produce $AC$ to $R$ and draw $RS \perp AB$ produced then $\triangle ASR$ is similar to $\triangle ABC$.

$$
\therefore \quad \frac{AS}{AB} = \frac{AR}{AC} = \frac{RS}{CB}
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \frac{RS}{AR} = \frac{CB}{AC} = \sin \theta
$$

Similarly,
$$
\frac{AS}{AR} = \frac{AB}{AC} = \cos \theta
$$
and
$$
\frac{RS}{AS} = \frac{CB}{AB} = \tan \theta .
$$
Hence, the trigonometric ratios of an angle do not depend on the size of the triangle. They only depend on the angle.

**REMARK:** Consider a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$. We know that in a right triangle, the **hypotenuse** is the longest side.

$$
\therefore \quad \sin \theta = \frac{BC}{AC} < 1 \text{ and } \cos \theta = \frac{AB}{AC} < 1 .
$$

Thus, $\sin \theta < 1$ and $\cos \theta < 1$.

### Power of T-Ratios

We write $(\sin \theta)^{2} = \sin^{2} \theta$; $(\sin \theta)^{3} = \sin^{3} \theta$; $(\cos \theta)^{3} = \cos^{3} \theta$; etc.

## Quotient Relation of T-Ratios

### Theorem 1

*For any acute angle $\theta$, prove that:*
1. $\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$
2. $\cot \theta = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$
3. $\tan \theta \cdot \cot \theta = 1$

**PROOF:** Consider a right-angled $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$. Let $AB = x$ units, $BC = y$ units and $AC = r$ units. Then,

1. $\tan \theta = \frac{y}{x} = \frac{(y / r)}{(x / r)}$ [dividing num. and denom. by $r$]
   $$
   = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}
   $$
   $\therefore \quad \tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$.

2. $\cot \theta = \frac{x}{y} = \frac{(x / r)}{(y / r)}$ [dividing num. and denom. by $r$]
   $$
   = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}
   $$
   $\therefore \quad \cot \theta = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$.

3. $\tan \theta \cdot \cot \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} \cdot \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta} = 1$.

---

## Summary

- $\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$
- $\tan \theta \cdot \cot \theta = 1$

## Square Relation

### Theorem 2

*For any acute angle $\theta$, prove that:*
1. $\sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta = 1$
2. $1 + \tan^{2} \theta = \sec^{2} \theta$
3. $1 + \cot^{2} \theta = \operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta$

**PROOF:** Consider a right-angled $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$. Let $AB = x$ units, $BC = y$ units and $AC = r$ units. Then, by **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have

$$
x^{2} + y^{2} = r^{2}
$$

1. $\sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta = \left(\frac{y}{r}\right)^{2} + \left(\frac{x}{r}\right)^{2} = \left(\frac{y^{2}}{r^{2}} + \frac{x^{2}}{r^{2}}\right)$
   $$
   = \frac{(x^{2} + y^{2})}{r^{2}} = \frac{r^{2}}{r^{2}} \quad [\because x^{2} + y^{2} = r^{2}]
   $$
   $$
   = 1 .
   $$
   $\therefore \quad \sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta = 1$.

2. $1 + \tan^{2} \theta = 1 + \left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^{2} = 1 + \frac{y^{2}}{x^{2}} = \frac{y^{2} + x^{2}}{x^{2}} = \frac{r^{2}}{x^{2}} \quad [\because x^{2} + y^{2} = r^{2}]$
   $$
   = \left(\frac{r}{x}\right)^{2} = \sec^{2} \theta .
   $$
   $\therefore \quad 1 + \tan^{2} \theta = \sec^{2} \theta$.

3. $1 + \cot^{2} \theta = 1 + \left(\frac{x}{y}\right)^{2} = 1 + \frac{x^{2}}{y^{2}} = \frac{x^{2} + y^{2}}{y^{2}} = \frac{r^{2}}{y^{2}} \quad [\because x^{2} + y^{2} = r^{2}]$
   $$
   = \left(\frac{r}{y}\right)^{2} = \operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta .
   $$
   $\therefore \quad 1 + \cot^{2} \theta = \operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta$.

---

## Summary

- $\sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta = 1$
- $1 + \tan^{2} \theta = \sec^{2} \theta$
- $1 + \cot^{2} \theta = \operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta$

## Solved Examples

### Example 1:

If $\sin A = \frac{8}{17}$, find other trigonometric ratios of $\angle A$.

#### Solution:

Let us draw a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$.
Then, $\sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{8}{17}$.
Let $BC = 8k$ and $AC = 17k$, where $k$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad AB^{2} = AC^{2} - BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad AB^{2} = (17k)^{2} - (8k)^{2} = 289k^{2} - 64k^{2} = 225k^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad AB = \sqrt{225k^{2}} = 15k
$$
Therefore:
- $\sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{8k}{17k} = \frac{8}{17}$
- $\cos A = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{15k}{17k} = \frac{15}{17}$
- $\tan A = \frac{\sin A}{\cos A} = \left(\frac{8}{17} \times \frac{17}{15}\right) = \frac{8}{15}$
- $\operatorname{cosec} A = \frac{1}{\sin A} = \frac{17}{8}$
- $\sec A = \frac{1}{\cos A} = \frac{17}{15}$
- $\cot A = \frac{1}{\tan A} = \frac{15}{8}$

### Example 2:

If $\cos A = \frac{9}{41}$, find other trigonometric ratios of $\angle A$.

#### Solution:

Let us draw a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$.
Then, $\cos A = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{9}{41}$.
Let $AB = 9k$ and $AC = 41k$, where $k$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = AC^{2} - AB^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = (41k)^{2} - (9k)^{2} = 1681k^{2} - 81k^{2} = 1600k^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC = \sqrt{1600k^{2}} = 40k
$$
Therefore:
- $\sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{40k}{41k} = \frac{40}{41}$
- $\cos A = \frac{9}{41}$ (given)
- $\tan A = \frac{\sin A}{\cos A} = \left(\frac{40}{41} \times \frac{41}{9}\right) = \frac{40}{9}$
- $\operatorname{cosec} A = \frac{1}{\sin A} = \frac{41}{40}$
- $\sec A = \frac{1}{\cos A} = \frac{41}{9}$
- $\cot A = \frac{1}{\tan A} = \frac{9}{40}$

### Example 3:

If $\tan A = \sqrt{3}$, find other trigonometric ratios of $\angle A$.

#### Solution:

Let us draw a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$.
Then, $\tan A = \frac{BC}{AB} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{1}$.
Let $BC = \sqrt{3}k$ and $AB = k$, where $k$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2} = k^{2} + (\sqrt{3}k)^{2} = k^{2} + 3k^{2} = 4k^{2}
$$
$$
\therefore \quad AC = \sqrt{4k^{2}} = 2k
$$
Therefore:
- $\sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{\sqrt{3}k}{2k} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$
- $\cos A = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{k}{2k} = \frac{1}{2}$
- $\tan A = \sqrt{3}$ (given)
- $\operatorname{cosec} A = \frac{1}{\sin A} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$
- $\sec A = \frac{1}{\cos A} = 2$
- $\cot A = \frac{1}{\tan A} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$

### Example 4:

If $\sec \theta = \frac{25}{7}$, find all trigonometric ratios of $\theta$.

#### Solution:

Let us draw a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$. Let $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$.
Then, $\sec \theta = \frac{AC}{AB} = \frac{25}{7}$.
Let $AC = 25k$ and $AB = 7k$, where $k$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = AC^{2} - AB^{2} = (25k)^{2} - (7k)^{2} = 625k^{2} - 49k^{2} = 576k^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC = \sqrt{576k^{2}} = 24k
$$
Therefore:
- $\sin \theta = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{24k}{25k} = \frac{24}{25}$
- $\cos \theta = \frac{1}{\sec \theta} = \frac{7}{25}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} = \left(\frac{24}{25} \times \frac{25}{7}\right) = \frac{24}{7}$
- $\operatorname{cosec} \theta = \frac{1}{\sin \theta} = \frac{25}{24}$
- $\sec \theta = \frac{25}{7}$ (given)
- $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta} = \frac{7}{24}$

### Example 5:

If $\cos \theta = \frac{3}{5}$, find the value of $\left(\frac{5 \operatorname{cosec} \theta - 4 \tan \theta}{\sec \theta + \cot \theta}\right)$.

#### Solution:

Let us draw a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$. Let $\angle A = \theta^{\circ}$.
Then, $\cos \theta = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{3}{5}$.
Let $AB = 3k$ and $AC = 5k$, where $k$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = AC^{2} - AB^{2} = (5k)^{2} - (3k)^{2} = 25k^{2} - 9k^{2} = 16k^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC = \sqrt{16k^{2}} = 4k
$$
Therefore:
- $\sec \theta = \frac{1}{\cos \theta} = \frac{5}{3}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{BC}{AB} = \frac{4k}{3k} = \frac{4}{3}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{1}{\tan \theta} = \frac{3}{4}$
- $\operatorname{cosec} \theta = \frac{AC}{BC} = \frac{5k}{4k} = \frac{5}{4}$

Now, evaluating the expression:
$$
\left(\frac{5 \operatorname{cosec} \theta - 4 \tan \theta}{\sec \theta + \cot \theta}\right) = \frac{\left(5 \times \frac{5}{4} - 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\right)}{\left(\frac{5}{3} + \frac{3}{4}\right)} = \frac{\left(\frac{25}{4} - \frac{16}{3}\right)}{\left(\frac{5}{3} + \frac{3}{4}\right)}
$$
$$
= \frac{\frac{75-64}{12}}{\frac{20+9}{12}} = \left(\frac{11}{12} \times \frac{12}{29}\right) = \frac{11}{29}
$$

### Example 6:

If $\sec \theta = \frac{5}{4}$, show that $\frac{(2 \cos \theta - \sin \theta)}{(\cot \theta - \tan \theta)} = \frac{12}{7}$.

#### Solution:

Consider a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle A = \theta$ and $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$.
$\sec \theta = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{base}} = \frac{AC}{AB} = \frac{5}{4} = \frac{5x}{4x}$ (say).
$\therefore \quad AC = 5x$ and $AB = 4x$, where $x$ is positive.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**,
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = AC^{2} - AB^{2} = (5x)^{2} - (4x)^{2} = 9x^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC = 3x
$$
Therefore:
- $\cos \theta = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{4x}{5x} = \frac{4}{5}$
- $\sin \theta = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{3x}{5x} = \frac{3}{5}$
- $\cot \theta = \frac{AB}{BC} = \frac{4x}{3x} = \frac{4}{3}$
- $\tan \theta = \frac{BC}{AB} = \frac{3x}{4x} = \frac{3}{4}$

Now, evaluating the expression:
$$
\frac{(2 \cos \theta - \sin \theta)}{(\cot \theta - \tan \theta)} = \frac{\left(2 \times \frac{4}{5} - \frac{3}{5}\right)}{\left(\frac{4}{3} - \frac{3}{4}\right)} = \frac{\left(\frac{8}{5} - \frac{3}{5}\right)}{\left(\frac{4}{3} - \frac{3}{4}\right)} = \frac{\left(\frac{5}{5}\right)}{\left(\frac{7}{12}\right)} = \frac{1}{\left(\frac{7}{12}\right)} = \frac{12}{7}
$$

### Example 7:

In a $\triangle ABC$ it is given that $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $AB : AC = 1 : \sqrt{2}$. Find the value of $\left(\frac{2 \tan A}{1 + \tan^{2} A}\right)$.

#### Solution:

Consider a $\triangle ABC$ in which $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$ and $AB : AC = 1 : \sqrt{2}$.
Let $AB = x$. Then, $AC = \sqrt{2}x$.

By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = AB^{2} + BC^{2} \Rightarrow BC^{2} = AC^{2} - AB^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC^{2} = (\sqrt{2}x)^{2} - (x)^{2} = 2x^{2} - x^{2} = x^{2}
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad BC = x
$$
$\therefore \quad \tan A = \frac{BC}{AB} = \frac{x}{x} = 1$.

So, the given expression is:
$$
\left(\frac{2 \tan A}{1 + \tan^{2} A}\right) = \left(\frac{2 \times 1}{1 + 1}\right) = \frac{2}{2} = 1
$$

### Example 8:

If $3 \tan \theta = 4$, evaluate $\frac{3 \sin \theta + 2 \cos \theta}{3 \sin \theta - 2 \cos \theta}$.

#### Solution:

Given $3 \tan \theta = 4 \Rightarrow \tan \theta = \frac{4}{3}$.
Given expression:
$$
\frac{3 \sin \theta + 2 \cos \theta}{3 \sin \theta - 2 \cos \theta}
$$
Dividing numerator and denominator by $\cos \theta$:
$$
= \frac{3 \tan \theta + 2}{3 \tan \theta - 2}
$$
$$
= \frac{\left(3 \times \frac{4}{3} + 2\right)}{\left(3 \times \frac{4}{3} - 2\right)} = \frac{4+2}{4-2} = \frac{6}{2} = 3
$$

### Example 9:

If $5 \cot \theta = 3$, find the value of $\left(\frac{5 \sin \theta - 3 \cos \theta}{4 \sin \theta + 3 \cos \theta}\right)$.

#### Solution:

Given $5 \cot \theta = 3 \Rightarrow \cot \theta = \frac{3}{5}$.
Given expression:
$$
\left(\frac{5 \sin \theta - 3 \cos \theta}{4 \sin \theta + 3 \cos \theta}\right)
$$
Dividing numerator and denominator by $\sin \theta$:
$$
= \frac{(5 - 3 \cot \theta)}{(4 + 3 \cot \theta)}
$$
$$
= \frac{\left(5 - 3 \times \frac{3}{5}\right)}{\left(4 + 3 \times \frac{3}{5}\right)} = \frac{\left(5 - \frac{9}{5}\right)}{\left(4 + \frac{9}{5}\right)} = \frac{\left(\frac{25-9}{5}\right)}{\left(\frac{20+9}{5}\right)} = \left(\frac{16}{5} \times \frac{5}{29}\right) = \frac{16}{29}
$$

### Example 10:

If $7 \sin^{2} \theta + 3 \cos^{2} \theta = 4$, show that $\tan \theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$. [CBSE 2008]

#### Solution:

Given:
$$
7 \sin^{2} \theta + 3 \cos^{2} \theta = 4
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 4 \sin^{2} \theta + 3 \sin^{2} \theta + 3 \cos^{2} \theta = 4
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 4 \sin^{2} \theta + 3(\sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta) = 4
$$
Since $\sin^{2} \theta + \cos^{2} \theta = 1$:
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 4 \sin^{2} \theta + 3 \times 1 = 4
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad 4 \sin^{2} \theta = 1 \Rightarrow \sin^{2} \theta = \frac{1}{4}
$$
Now, find $\cos^{2} \theta$:
$$
\therefore \quad \cos^{2} \theta = (1 - \sin^{2} \theta) = \left(1 - \frac{1}{4}\right) = \frac{3}{4}
$$
Now, find $\tan^{2} \theta$:
$$
\therefore \quad \tan^{2} \theta = \frac{\sin^{2} \theta}{\cos^{2} \theta} = \left(\frac{1/4}{3/4}\right) = \left(\frac{1}{4} \times \frac{4}{3}\right) = \frac{1}{3}
$$
$$
\text{Hence, } \tan \theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
$$

### Example 11:

If $\cot \theta = \frac{15}{8}$ then evaluate $\frac{(2 + 2 \sin \theta)(1 - \sin \theta)}{(1 + \cos \theta)(2 - 2 \cos \theta)}$. [CBSE 2009]

#### Solution:

Given expression:
$$
= \frac{(2 + 2 \sin \theta)(1 - \sin \theta)}{(1 + \cos \theta)(2 - 2 \cos \theta)}
$$
$$
= \frac{2(1 + \sin \theta)(1 - \sin \theta)}{2(1 + \cos \theta)(1 - \cos \theta)}
$$
Using the identity $(a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2$:
$$
= \frac{(1 - \sin^{2} \theta)}{(1 - \cos^{2} \theta)}
$$
Using the Pythagorean identities $\cos^2 \theta = 1 - \sin^2 \theta$ and $\sin^2 \theta = 1 - \cos^2 \theta$:
$$
= \frac{\cos^{2} \theta}{\sin^{2} \theta} = \cot^{2} \theta
$$
$$
= (\cot \theta)^{2} = \left(\frac{15}{8}\right)^{2} = \frac{225}{64}
$$
Hence, the value of the given expression is $\frac{225}{64}$.

### Example 12:

In $\triangle ABC$, right-angled at $B$, $AB = 5 \text{ cm}$ and $BC = 12 \text{ cm}$. Find the values of $\sin A$, $\sec A$, $\sin C$ and $\sec C$.

#### Solution:

In $\triangle ABC$, $\angle B = 90^{\circ}$, $AB = 5 \text{ cm}$ and $BC = 12 \text{ cm}$.
By **Pythagoras' theorem**, we have
$$
AC^{2} = (AB^{2} + BC^{2}) = \{(5)^{2} + (12)^{2}\} \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
= (25 + 144) \text{ cm}^{2} = 169 \text{ cm}^{2}
$$
$$
\therefore \quad AC = \sqrt{169 \text{ cm}^{2}} = 13 \text{ cm}
$$

![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/2025_09_25_cc52b83dc44bbcf3c9f8g-09.jpg?height=284&width=311&top_left_y=1331&top_left_x=886)

For T-ratios of $\angle A$, we have:
- **base** $= AB = 5 \text{ cm}$
- **perpendicular** $= BC = 12 \text{ cm}$
- **hypotenuse** $= AC = 13 \text{ cm}$

$\therefore \quad \sin A = \frac{BC}{AC} = \frac{12}{13}$ and $\sec A = \frac{AC}{AB} = \frac{13}{5}$.

For T-ratios of $\angle C$, we have:
- **base** $= BC = 12 \text{ cm}$
- **perpendicular** $= AB = 5 \text{ cm}$
- **hypotenuse** $= AC = 13 \text{ cm}$

$\therefore \quad \sin C = \frac{AB}{AC} = \frac{5}{13}$ and $\sec C = \frac{AC}{BC} = \frac{13}{12}$.
