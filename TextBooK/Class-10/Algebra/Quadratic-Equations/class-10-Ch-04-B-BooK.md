## Solving Quadratic Equations by Completing the Square

In fact, we can convert any quadratic equation to the form $(a x+b)^{2}-c^{2}=0$ and then we can easily find its roots.

---

## Solved Examples

### Example 1

Solve the equation $x^{2}-10 x-2=0$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& x^{2}-10 x-2=0 \\
\Rightarrow & x^{2}-10 x=2 \\
\Rightarrow & x^{2}-2 \times x \times 5+5^{2}=2+5^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $5^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & (x-5)^{2}=(2+25)=27 \\
\Rightarrow & x-5= \pm \sqrt{27}= \pm 3 \sqrt{3} \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & x-5=3 \sqrt{3} \text{ or } x-5=-3 \sqrt{3} \\
\Rightarrow & x=(5+3 \sqrt{3}) \text{ or } x=(5-3 \sqrt{3})
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **$(5+3 \sqrt{3})$** and **$(5-3 \sqrt{3})$** are the roots of the given equation.

### Example 2

Solve the equation $3 x^{2}-5 x+2=0$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 3 x^{2}-5 x+2=0 \\
\Rightarrow & 9 x^{2}-15 x+6=0 \quad \textit{[multiplying each term by 3]} \\
\Rightarrow & 9 x^{2}-15 x=-6 \\
\Rightarrow & (3 x)^{2}-2 \times 3 x \times \frac{5}{2}+\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{2}=-6+\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(3 x-\frac{5}{2}\right)^{2}=\left(-6+\frac{25}{4}\right)=\frac{(-24+25)}{4}=\frac{1}{4}=\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(3 x-\frac{5}{2}\right)= \pm \frac{1}{2} \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & 3 x-\frac{5}{2}=\frac{1}{2} \text{ or } 3 x-\frac{5}{2}=\frac{-1}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & 3 x=\left(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{5}{2}\right)=\frac{6}{2}=3 \text{ or } 3 x=\left(\frac{-1}{2}+\frac{5}{2}\right)=\frac{4}{2}=2 \\
\Rightarrow & 3 x=3 \text{ or } 3 x=2 \Rightarrow x=1 \text{ or } x=\frac{2}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **1** and **$\frac{2}{3}$** are the roots of the given equation.

### Example 3

Solve the equation $2 x^{2}+x-4=0$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 2 x^{2}+x-4=0 \\
\Rightarrow & 4 x^{2}+2 x-8=0 \quad \textit{[multiplying both sides by 2]} \\
\Rightarrow & 4 x^{2}+2 x=8 \\
\Rightarrow & (2 x)^{2}+2 \times 2 x \times \frac{1}{2}+\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}=8+\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(2 x+\frac{1}{2}\right)^{2}=\left(8+\frac{1}{4}\right)=\frac{33}{4}=\left(\frac{\sqrt{33}}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & 2 x+\frac{1}{2}= \pm\left(\frac{\sqrt{33}}{2}\right) \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & 2 x+\frac{1}{2}=\frac{\sqrt{33}}{2} \text{ or } 2 x+\frac{1}{2}=\frac{-\sqrt{33}}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & 2 x=\left(\frac{\sqrt{33}}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\right)=\frac{(\sqrt{33}-1)}{2} \text{ or } 2 x=\frac{-\sqrt{33}}{2}-\frac{1}{2}=\frac{-(\sqrt{33}+1)}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & x=\frac{(\sqrt{33}-1)}{4} \text{ or } x=\frac{-(\sqrt{33}+1)}{4}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **$\frac{-1+\sqrt{33}}{4}$** and **$\frac{-1-\sqrt{33}}{4}$** are the roots of the given equation.

### Example 4

By using the method of completing the square, show that the equation $4 x^{2}+3 x+5=0$ has no real roots.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 4 x^{2}+3 x+5=0 \\
\Rightarrow & 4 x^{2}+3 x=-5 \\
\Rightarrow & (2 x)^{2}+2 \times 2 x \times \frac{3}{4}+\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{2}=-5+\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(2 x+\frac{3}{4}\right)^{2}=\left(-5+\frac{9}{16}\right)=\frac{(-80+9)}{16}=\frac{-71}{16}<0
\end{aligned}
$$
But, $\left(2 x+\frac{3}{4}\right)^{2}$ cannot be negative for any real value of $x$.

So, there is no real value of $x$ satisfying the given equation.
Hence, the given equation has **no real roots**.

### Example 5

Solve the equation $10 x-\frac{1}{x}=3$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 10 x-\frac{1}{x}=3 \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x^{2}-1=3 x \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x^{2}-3 x=1 \\
\Rightarrow & 100 x^{2}-30 x=10 \quad \textit{[multiplying each side by 10]} \\
\Rightarrow & (10 x)^{2}-2 \times 10 x \times \frac{3}{2}+\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^{2}=10+\left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(10 x-\frac{3}{2}\right)^{2}=\left(10+\frac{9}{4}\right)=\frac{49}{4}=\left(\frac{7}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x-\frac{3}{2}= \pm \frac{7}{2} \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x-\frac{3}{2}=\frac{7}{2} \text{ or } 10 x-\frac{3}{2}=\frac{-7}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x=\left(\frac{7}{2}+\frac{3}{2}\right)=\frac{10}{2}=5 \text{ or } 10 x=\left(\frac{-7}{2}+\frac{3}{2}\right)=\frac{-4}{2}=-2 \\
\Rightarrow & 10 x=5 \text{ or } 10 x=-2 \Rightarrow x=\frac{5}{10}=\frac{1}{2} \text{ or } x=\frac{-2}{10}=\frac{-1}{5}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **$\frac{1}{2}$** and **$\frac{-1}{5}$** are the roots of the given equation.

### Example 6

Solve the equation $a^{2} x^{2}-3 a b x+2 b^{2}=0$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& a^{2} x^{2}-3 a b x+2 b^{2}=0 \\
\Rightarrow & a^{2} x^{2}-3 a b x=-2 b^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & (a x)^{2}-2 \times(a x) \times \frac{3 b}{2}+\left(\frac{3 b}{2}\right)^{2}=-2 b^{2}+\left(\frac{3 b}{2}\right)^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $\left(\frac{3 b}{2}\right)^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(a x-\frac{3 b}{2}\right)^{2}=\left(-2 b^{2}+\frac{9 b^{2}}{4}\right)=\frac{\left(-8 b^{2}+9 b^{2}\right)}{4}=\frac{b^{2}}{4}=\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(a x-\frac{3 b}{2}\right)= \pm \frac{b}{2} \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left(a x-\frac{3 b}{2}\right)=\frac{b}{2} \text{ or } \left(a x-\frac{3 b}{2}\right)=\frac{-b}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & a x=\left(\frac{b}{2}+\frac{3 b}{2}\right)=\frac{4 b}{2}=2 b \text{ or } a x=\left(\frac{-b}{2}+\frac{3 b}{2}\right)=\frac{2 b}{2}=b \\
\Rightarrow & x=\frac{2 b}{a} \text{ or } x=\frac{b}{a}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **$\frac{2 b}{a}$** and **$\frac{b}{a}$** are the roots of the given equation.

### Example 7

Solve the equation $x^{2}-(\sqrt{3}+1) x+\sqrt{3}=0$ by the method of completing the square.

#### Solution

We have
$$
\begin{aligned}
& x^{2}-(\sqrt{3}+1) x+\sqrt{3}=0 \\
\Rightarrow & x^{2}-(\sqrt{3}+1) x=-\sqrt{3} \\
\Rightarrow & x^{2}-2 \times x \times\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}+1}{2}\right)+\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}+1}{2}\right)^{2}=-\sqrt{3}+\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}+1}{2}\right)^{2} \quad \textit{[adding $\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}+1}{2}\right)^{2}$ on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & \left\{x-\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}\right\}^{2}=\left\{\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)^{2}}{4}-\sqrt{3}\right\} \\
\Rightarrow & \frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)^{2}-4 \sqrt{3}}{4}=\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}-1}{2}\right)^{2} \\
\Rightarrow & \left\{x-\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}\right\}= \pm \frac{(\sqrt{3}-1)}{2} \quad \textit{[taking square root on both sides]} \\
\Rightarrow & x-\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}=\frac{(\sqrt{3}-1)}{2} \text{ or } x-\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}=\frac{-(\sqrt{3}-1)}{2} \\
\Rightarrow & x=\frac{(\sqrt{3}-1)}{2}+\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}=\frac{2 \sqrt{3}}{2}=\sqrt{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{(-\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}+\frac{(\sqrt{3}+1)}{2}=\frac{2}{2}=1 \\
\Rightarrow & x=\sqrt{3} \text{ or } x=1
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, **$\sqrt{3}$** and **1** are the roots of the given equation.
