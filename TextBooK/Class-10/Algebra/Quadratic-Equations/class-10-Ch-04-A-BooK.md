## Quadratic Equations

---

## History

- **Babylonians** were the first to solve the quadratic equations of the form $x^{2}-px+q=0$.
- **Brahmagupta** (AD 598-665), an Indian mathematician, gave an explicit formula to solve a quadratic equation of the form $ax^{2}-bx=c$.
- An Arab mathematician, **Al-Khwarizmi**, in AD 800 also studied quadratic equations of various types.
- An ancient Indian mathematician **Shridharacharya** derived the well-known **Quadratic Formula** for solving a quadratic equation $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$ by the method of completing the square. This is being used as the standard formula for solving such an equation.

According to this formula, the roots of $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$ are given by $\alpha=\frac{-b+\sqrt{D}}{2a}$ and $\beta=\frac{-b-\sqrt{D}}{2a}$, where $D=(b^{2}-4ac)$ is called the **discriminant** of this equation.

---

### Definitions

**Quadratic Equation:** A quadratic equation in the variable $x$ is an equation of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$, where $a, b, c$ are real numbers and $a \neq 0$.

**Roots of a Quadratic Equation:** A real number $\alpha$ is called a **root** of the quadratic equation $ax^{2}+bx+c=0, a \neq 0$ if $a\alpha^{2}+b\alpha+c=0$.

**Note 1:** If $\alpha$ is a root of $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$ then we say that:
1. $x=\alpha$ **satisfies** the equation $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$.
2. $x=\alpha$ is a **solution** of the equation $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$.

**Note 2:** The roots of a quadratic equation $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$ are called the **zeros** of the polynomial $ax^{2}+bx+c$.

**Solving a Quadratic Equation:** Solving a quadratic equation means **finding its roots**.

## Solved Examples

### Example: 1

Which of the following are quadratic equations?
1. $x^{2}-5x+3=0$
2. $2x^{2}-3\sqrt{2}x+6=0$
3. $3x^{2}-2\sqrt{x}+8=0$
4. $2x^{2}-3=0$
5. $x+\frac{1}{x}=x^{2}$
6. $x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}=4\frac{1}{4}$

#### Solution:

1.  Clearly, $(x^{2}-5x+3)$ is a quadratic polynomial.
    $\therefore x^{2}-5x+3=0$ is a **quadratic equation**.
2.  Clearly, $(2x^{2}-3\sqrt{2}x+6)$ is a quadratic polynomial.
    $\therefore 2x^{2}-3\sqrt{2}x+6=0$ is a **quadratic equation**.
3.  $3x^{2}-2\sqrt{x}+8$ is not of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$.
    $\therefore 3x^{2}-2\sqrt{x}+8=0$ is **not a quadratic equation**.
4.  $2x^{2}-3=0$ is of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$, where $a=2, b=0$ and $c=-3$.
    $\therefore 2x^{2}-3=0$ is a **quadratic equation**.
5.  $x+\frac{1}{x}=x^{2} \Rightarrow x^{2}+1=x^{3} \Rightarrow x^{3}-x^{2}-1=0$.
    Since $(x^{3}-x^{2}-1)$ is a polynomial of degree 3, it is not quadratic.
    Hence, $x+\frac{1}{x}=x^{2}$ is **not a quadratic equation**.
6.  $x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}=\frac{17}{4} \Rightarrow 4x^{4}+4=17x^{2} \Rightarrow 4x^{4}-17x^{2}+4=0$.
    Clearly, $4x^{4}-17x^{2}+4$ is a polynomial of degree 4.
    $\therefore x^{2}+\frac{1}{x^{2}}=\frac{17}{4}$ is **not a quadratic equation**.

### Example: 2

Check whether the following are quadratic equations:
1. $(2x-1)(x-3)=(x+4)(x-2)$
2. $(x+2)^{3}=2x(x^{2}-1)$
3. $(x+1)^{3}=x^{3}+x+6$
4. $x(x+3)+6=(x+2)(x-2)$

#### Solution:

1.  We have $(2x-1)(x-3)=(x+4)(x-2)$
    $\Rightarrow 2x^{2}-7x+3=x^{2}+2x-8 \Rightarrow x^{2}-9x+11=0$.
    This is of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$, where $a=1, b=-9$ and $c=11$.
    Hence, the given equation is a **quadratic equation**.
2.  We have $(x+2)^{3}=2x(x^{2}-1)$
    $\Rightarrow x^{3}+8+6x(x+2)=2x^{3}-2x$
    $\Rightarrow x^{3}+6x^{2}+12x+8=2x^{3}-2x$
    $\Rightarrow x^{3}-6x^{2}-14x-8=0$.
    This is not of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$.
    Hence, the given equation is **not a quadratic equation**.
3.  We have $(x+1)^{3}=x^{3}+x+6$
    $\Rightarrow x^{3}+1+3x(x+1)=x^{3}+x+6$
    $\Rightarrow 3x^{2}+2x-5=0$.
    This is of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$, where $a=3, b=2$ and $c=-5$.
    Hence, the given equation is a **quadratic equation**.
4.  We have $x(x+3)+6=(x+2)(x-2)$
    $\Rightarrow x^{2}+3x+6=x^{2}-4$
    $\Rightarrow 3x+10=0$.
    This is not of the form $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$.
    Hence, the given equation is **not a quadratic equation**.

### Example: 3

For the quadratic equation $2x^{2}-5x-3=0$, show that:
1. $x=3$ is its solution.
2. $x=\frac{-1}{2}$ is its solution.
3. $x=4$ is not its solution.

#### Solution:

The given equation is $2x^{2}-5x-3=0$.

1.  On substituting $x=3$ in the given equation, we get
    $$
    \text{LHS} = 2 \times 3^{2}-5 \times 3-3 = (18-15-3) = 0 = \text{RHS}
    $$
    $\therefore x=3$ is a solution of $2x^{2}-5x-3=0$.
2.  On substituting $x=\frac{-1}{2}$ in the given equation, we get
    $$
    \begin{aligned}
    \text{LHS} &= 2 \times \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^{2}-5 \times \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)-3 \\
    &= \left\{2 \times \frac{1}{4} + 5 \times \frac{1}{2}-3\right\} \\
    &= \left\{\frac{1}{2}+\frac{5}{2}-3\right\} = 0 = \text{RHS}
    \end{aligned}
    $$
    $\therefore x= \frac{-1}{2}$ is a solution of $2x^{2}-5x-3=0$.
3.  On substituting $x=4$ in the given equation, we get
    $$
    \text{LHS} = 2 \times 4^{2}-5 \times 4-3 = (32-20-3) = 9 \neq 0
    $$
    Thus, $\text{LHS} \neq \text{RHS}$.
    $\therefore x=4$ is not a solution of $2x^{2}-5x-3=0$.

### Example: 4

Show that $\sqrt{2}$ and $-2\sqrt{2}$ are the roots of the equation $x^{2}+\sqrt{2}x-4=0$.

#### Solution:

The given equation is $x^{2}+\sqrt{2}x-4=0$.

Putting $x=\sqrt{2}$ in the given equation, we get
$$
\text{LHS} = (\sqrt{2})^{2}+(\sqrt{2} \times \sqrt{2})-4 = (2+2-4) = 0 = \text{RHS}
$$
$\therefore \sqrt{2}$ is a root of the given equation.

Putting $x=-2\sqrt{2}$ in the given equation, we get
$$
\text{LHS} = (-2\sqrt{2})^{2}+\sqrt{2} \times (-2\sqrt{2})-4 = (8-4-4) = 0 = \text{RHS}
$$
$\therefore -2\sqrt{2}$ is a root of the given equation.
Hence, $\sqrt{2}$ and $-2\sqrt{2}$ are the roots of the given equation.

## Solving a Quadratic Equation by Factorisation

Let the given quadratic equation be $ax^{2}+bx+c=0$, where $a \neq 0$. Let $(ax^{2}+bx+c)$ be expressible as the product of two linear expressions, say $(px+q)$ and $(rx+s)$, where $p, q, r, s$ are real numbers such that $p \neq 0$ and $r \neq 0$. Then,
$$
\begin{aligned}
ax^{2}+bx+c=0 & \Rightarrow (px+q)(rx+s)=0 \\
& \Rightarrow (px+q)=0 \quad \text{or} \quad (rx+s)=0 \\
& \Rightarrow x=-\frac{q}{p} \quad \text{or} \quad x=-\frac{s}{r}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 5

Solve: $(x+2)(3x-5)=0$.

#### Solution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(x+2)(3x-5)=0 & \Rightarrow x+2=0 \text{ or } 3x-5=0 \\
& \Rightarrow x=-2 \quad \text{or} \quad x=\frac{5}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, the roots of the given equation are $-2$ and $\frac{5}{3}$.

### Example: 6

Solve: $5x^{2}-8x=0$.

#### Solution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
5x^{2}-8x=0 & \Rightarrow x(5x-8)=0 \\
& \Rightarrow x=0 \text{ or } 5x-8=0 \\
& \Rightarrow x=0 \text{ or } x=\frac{8}{5}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $0$ and $\frac{8}{5}$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 7

Solve: $6x^{2}-x-2=0$ by the factorisation method.

#### Solution:

We write, $-x=-4x+3x$ as $(-4x) \times 3x=-12x^{2}=6x^{2} \times (-2)$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 6x^{2}-x-2=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 6x^{2}-4x+3x-2=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x(3x-2)+(3x-2)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (3x-2)(2x+1)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 3x-2=0 \text{ or } 2x+1=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{2}{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{-1}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\frac{2}{3}$ and $\frac{-1}{2}$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 8

Solve: $8x^{2}-22x-21=0$ by the factorisation method.

#### Solution:

We write, $-22x=-28x+6x$ as $8x^{2} \times (-21)=-168x^{2}=(-28x) \times 6x$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 8x^{2}-22x-21=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 8x^{2}-28x+6x-21=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 4x(2x-7)+3(2x-7)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (2x-7)(4x+3)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x-7=0 \text{ or } 4x+3=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{7}{2} \text{ or } x=\frac{-3}{4}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\frac{7}{2}$ and $\frac{-3}{4}$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 9

Solve: $6x^{2}+40=31x$.

#### Solution:

The given equation may be written as $6x^{2}-31x+40=0$.
We write, $-31x=-16x-15x$ as $6x^{2} \times 40=240x^{2}=(-16x) \times (-15x)$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 6x^{2}-31x+40=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 6x^{2}-16x-15x+40=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x(3x-8)-5(3x-8)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (3x-8)(2x-5)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 3x-8=0 \text{ or } 2x-5=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{8}{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{5}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\frac{8}{3}$ and $\frac{5}{2}$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 10

Solve: $4\sqrt{3}x^{2}+5x-2\sqrt{3}=0$. [CBSE 2013]

#### Solution:

Here, $4\sqrt{3} \times (-2\sqrt{3})=-24$, and we can split the middle term as $8x-3x$ since $8 \times (-3)=-24$ and $8+(-3)=5$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 4\sqrt{3}x^{2}+5x-2\sqrt{3}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 4\sqrt{3}x^{2}+8x-3x-2\sqrt{3}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 4x(\sqrt{3}x+2)-\sqrt{3}(\sqrt{3}x+2)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (\sqrt{3}x+2)(4x-\sqrt{3})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \sqrt{3}x+2=0 \text{ or } 4x-\sqrt{3}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{-2}{\sqrt{3}} \text{ or } x=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\left(\frac{-2}{\sqrt{3}} \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}}\right)=\frac{-2\sqrt{3}}{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example: 11

Solve: $4x^{2}-12x+9=0$.

#### Solution:

Here, $4 \times 9=36$. We need to find two numbers whose product is 36 and sum is -12. These are -6 and -6.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 4x^{2}-12x+9=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 4x^{2}-6x-6x+9=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x(2x-3)-3(2x-3)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (2x-3)(2x-3)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (2x-3)^{2}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x-3=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{3}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $x=\frac{3}{2}$ is the repeated root of the given equation.

### Example: 12

Solve the following equation by using factorisation method: $4x^{2}-4ax+(a^{2}-b^{2})=0$. [CBSE 2012, '15]

#### Solution:

We split the middle term $-4ax$ as $\{-2(a+b)x\} + \{-2(a-b)x\}$.
Note that $\{-2(a+b)\} \times \{-2(a-b)\} = 4(a^2-b^2) = 4 \times (a^2-b^2)$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 4x^{2}-4ax+(a^{2}-b^{2})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 4x^{2}-2(a+b)x-2(a-b)x+(a^{2}-b^{2})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x\{2x-(a+b)\}-(a-b)\{2x-(a+b)\}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \{2x-(a+b)\} \times \{2x-(a-b)\}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x-(a+b)=0 \text{ or } 2x-(a-b)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{(a+b)}{2} \text{ or } x=\frac{(a-b)}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\frac{(a+b)}{2}$ and $\frac{(a-b)}{2}$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 13

Solve the following equation by using factorisation method: $9x^{2}-6b^{2}x-(a^{4}-b^{4})=0$. [CBSE 2015]

#### Solution:

We may write, $-6b^{2} = 3(a^{2}-b^{2}) - 3(a^{2}+b^{2})$.
Also, $\{3(a^{2}-b^{2})\} \times \{-3(a^{2}+b^{2})\} = -9(a^{4}-b^{4})$.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 9x^{2}-6b^{2}x-(a^{4}-b^{4})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 9x^{2}+3(a^{2}-b^{2})x-3(a^{2}+b^{2})x-(a^{4}-b^{4})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 3x\{3x+(a^{2}-b^{2})\}-(a^{2}+b^{2})\{3x+(a^{2}-b^{2})\}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \{3x+(a^{2}-b^{2})\} \times \{3x-(a^{2}+b^{2})\}=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 3x+(a^{2}-b^{2})=0 \text{ or } 3x-(a^{2}+b^{2})=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=\frac{(b^{2}-a^{2})}{3} \text{ or } x=\frac{(a^{2}+b^{2})}{3}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $\frac{(b^{2}-a^{2})}{3}$ and $\frac{(a^{2}+b^{2})}{3}$ are the required roots of the given equation.

### Example: 14

Solve: $\frac{x+3}{x+2}=\frac{3x-7}{2x-3}$.

#### Solution:

By cross multiplication, we get:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& (x+3)(2x-3)=(x+2)(3x-7) \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 2x^{2}+3x-9=3x^{2}-x-14 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-4x-5=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-5x+x-5=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(x-5)+(x-5)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x-5)(x+1)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x-5=0 \text{ or } x+1=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=5 \text{ or } x=-1
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $5$ and $-1$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 15

Solve: $\frac{14}{x+3}-1=\frac{5}{x+1}, x \neq -3, -1$. [CBSE 2014]

#### Solution:

The given equation may be written as:
$$
\begin{aligned}

\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-5x+4=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-4x-x+4=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(x-4)-(x-4)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x-4)(x-1)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x-4=0 \text{ or } x-1=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=4 \text{ or } x=1
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $4$ and $1$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 16

Solve: $\frac{1}{(x+4)}-\frac{1}{(x-7)}=\frac{11}{30}, x \neq -4, 7$. [CBSE 2008, '10]

#### Solution:

We have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{1}{(x+4)}-\frac{1}{(x-7)}=\frac{11}{30} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{(x-7)-(x+4)}{(x+4)(x-7)}=\frac{11}{30} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{-11}{(x^{2}-3x-28)}=\frac{11}{30} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{-1}{(x^{2}-3x-28)}=\frac{1}{30} \quad \text{[On dividing both sides by 11]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x^{2}-3x-28)=-30 \quad \text{[By cross multiplication]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-3x+2=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}-2x-x+2=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(x-2)-(x-2)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x-2)(x-1)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x-2=0 \text{ or } x-1=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=2 \text{ or } x=1
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $2$ and $1$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 17

Solve: $\frac{1}{(a+b+x)}=\frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b}+\frac{1}{x}, ~[x \neq 0, x \neq -(a+b)]$. [CBSE 2013]

#### Solution:

We have:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{1}{(a+b+x)}=\frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b}+\frac{1}{x} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{1}{(a+b+x)}-\frac{1}{x}=\frac{1}{a}+\frac{1}{b} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{x-(a+b+x)}{x(a+b+x)}=\frac{b+a}{ab} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{-(a+b)}{x(a+b+x)}=\frac{(a+b)}{ab} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{-1}{x(a+b+x)}=\frac{1}{ab} \quad \text{[On dividing both sides by (a+b)]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(a+b+x)=-ab \quad \text{[By cross multiplication]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}+ax+bx+ab=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(x+a)+b(x+a)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x+a)(x+b)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x+a=0 \text{ or } x+b=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=-a \text{ or } x=-b
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $-a$ and $-b$ are the roots of the given equation.

Solve: $\frac{x-2}{x-3}+\frac{x-4}{x-5}=3\frac{1}{3}, x \neq 3, 5$. [CBSE 2014]

\Rightarrow \quad & 3(2x^{2}-14x+22)=10(x^{2}-8x+15) \quad \text{[By cross multiplication]} \\

\Rightarrow \quad & x=6 \text{ or } x=\frac{7}{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $6$ and $\frac{7}{2}$ are the roots of the given equation.

\end{aligned}

$$
\begin{aligned}

### Example: 21

Solve: $\frac{1}{(x+3)}+\frac{1}{(2x-1)}=\frac{11}{(7x+9)}, x \neq -3, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{-9}{7}$. [CBSE 2009C]

#### Solution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \frac{1}{(x+3)}+\frac{1}{(2x-1)}=\frac{11}{(7x+9)} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{(2x-1)+(x+3)}{(x+3)(2x-1)}=\frac{11}{(7x+9)} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & \frac{(3x+2)}{2x^{2}+5x-3}=\frac{11}{(7x+9)} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (3x+2)(7x+9)=11(2x^{2}+5x-3) \quad \text{[By cross multiplication]} \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 21x^{2}+41x+18=22x^{2}+55x-33 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}+14x-51=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x^{2}+17x-3x-51=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x(x+17)-3(x+17)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (x+17)(x-3)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x+17=0 \text{ or } x-3=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & x=-17 \text{ or } x=3
\end{aligned}
$$
Hence, $-17$ and $3$ are the roots of the given equation.

### Example: 22

Solve: $5^{(x+1)}+5^{(2-x)}=5^{3}+1$.

#### Solution:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& 5^{(x+1)}+5^{(2-x)}=5^{3}+1 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 5^{x} \cdot 5 + 5^{2} \cdot 5^{-x}=126 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 5 \cdot 5^{x} + \frac{25}{5^{x}}=126
\end{aligned}
$$
Let $y = 5^{x}$. The equation becomes:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 5y+\frac{25}{y}=126 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 5y^{2}-126y+25=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 5y^{2}-125y-y+25=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & 5y(y-25)-(y-25)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & (y-25)(5y-1)=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & y-25=0 \text{ or } 5y-1=0 \\
\Rightarrow \quad & y=25 \text{ or } y=\frac{1}{5}
\end{aligned}
$$
Substituting back $y = 5^{x}$:
$$
5^{x}=25 \Rightarrow 5^{x}=5^{2} \Rightarrow x=2
$$
$$
5^{x}=\frac{1}{5} \Rightarrow 5^{x}=5^{-1} \Rightarrow x=-1
$$
Hence, $2$ and $-1$ are the roots of the given equation.
