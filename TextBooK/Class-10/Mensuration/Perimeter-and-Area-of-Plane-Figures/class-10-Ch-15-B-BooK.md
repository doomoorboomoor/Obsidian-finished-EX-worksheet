# Perimeter and Area of Quadrilaterals [Formulae]

---

### 1. Area and Perimeter of a Rectangle

- (i) **Area** = $(length \times breadth)$ sq units.
- (ii) **Length** = $\frac{\text{area}}{\text{breadth}}$ and **Breadth** = $\frac{\text{area}}{\text{length}}$.
- (iii) **Perimeter** = $2(l+b)$ units.
- (iv) **Diagonal** = $\sqrt{l^{2}+b^{2}}$ units.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-001.jpg)

### 2. Area of 4 Walls of a Room

- **Area** = $[2(l+b) \times h)]$ sq units.

### 3. Area and Perimeter of a Square

- (i) **Area** = $a^{2}$ sq units.
- (ii) **Area** = $\left\{\frac{1}{2} \times (\text{diagonal})^{2}\right\}$ sq units.
- (iii) **Perimeter** = $4a$ units.
- (iv) **Diagonal** = $\sqrt{2} a$ units.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-002.jpg)

### 4. Area of Parallelogram and Rhombus

- (i) **Area of a parallelogram** = $\{(\text{base}) \times (\text{height})\}$ sq units.
- (ii) **Area of a rhombus** = $\left(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{product of diagonals}\right)$ sq units.

### 5. Area of a Trapezium

$$
\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{sum of parallel sides}) \times (\text{distance between them})
$$

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-003.jpg)

### 6. Area of a Quadrilateral

- (i) Let $ABCD$ be a quadrilateral with diagonal $AC$. Let $BL \perp AC$ and $DM \perp AC$. Let $BL = h_{1}$ and $DM = h_{2}$.
Then, **ar(quad. ABCD)** = $\left\{\frac{1}{2}\left(h_{1}+h_{2}\right) \times AC\right\}$ sq units.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-004.jpg)

- (ii) When diagonals of a quadrilateral are perpendicular to each other:
**ar(quad. ABCD)** = $\left(\frac{1}{2} \times \text{product of diagonals}\right)$ sq units.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-005.jpg)

## Solved Examples

### Example 1:

The length of a rectangular field exceeds its breadth by $8 \text{ m}$ and the area of the field is $240 \text{ m}^2$. Find the dimensions of the field.

#### Solution:

Let the breadth of the field be $x$ metres.
Then, its length $= (x+8) \text{ m}$.
$\therefore$ Area $= (x+8) \cdot x \text{ m}^2$.

$$
(x+8)x = 240 \Rightarrow x^2 + 8x - 240 = 0
$$

$$
\Rightarrow x^2 + 20x - 12x - 240 = 0 \Rightarrow x(x+20) - 12(x+20) = 0
$$

$$
\Rightarrow (x+20)(x-12) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 12 \quad [\because x \neq -20]
$$

$\therefore$ **Breadth** $= 12 \text{ m}$ and **Length** $= 20 \text{ m}$.

### Example 2:

Sum of the areas of two squares is $400 \text{ cm}^2$. If the difference of their perimeters is $16 \text{ cm}$, find the sides of the two squares. [CBSE 2013]

#### Solution:

Let the sides of the given squares be $a \text{ cm}$ and $b \text{ cm}$ respectively. Then,

$$
a^2 + b^2 = 400 \quad \cdots(\text{i})
$$

and $4a - 4b = 16 \Rightarrow a - b = 4$.

Squaring (ii) on both sides, we get

$$
(a-b)^2 = 16 \Rightarrow (a^2+b^2) - 2ab = 16
$$

$$
\Rightarrow 400 - 2ab = 16 \quad [\text{using (i)}]
$$

$$
\Rightarrow 2ab = 384 \Rightarrow ab = 192 \quad \cdots(\text{iii})
$$

Now, $(a+b)^2 - (a-b)^2 = 4ab$
$\Rightarrow (a+b)^2 - 16 = 4 \times 192$.
$\Rightarrow (a+b)^2 = 768 + 16 = 784 \Rightarrow a+b = \sqrt{784} = 28$.

On solving $a+b=28$ and $a-b=4$, we get $a=16$ and $b=12$.
Hence, the sides of the given squares are **16 cm** and **12 cm**.

### Example 3:

The length of the diagonal of a square is $24 \text{ cm}$. Find (i) the area of the square and (ii) its perimeter. [Given, $\sqrt{2} = 1.41$.]

#### Solution:

(i) Area of the square = $\left\{\frac{1}{2} \times (\text{diagonal})^2\right\}$ sq units.

$$
= \left(\frac{1}{2} \times 24 \times 24\right) \text{ cm}^2 = 288 \text{ cm}^2.
$$

(ii) Side of the square = $\sqrt{288} \text{ cm} = \sqrt{144 \times 2} \text{ cm}$

$$
= 12\sqrt{2} \text{ cm} = (12 \times 1.41) \text{ cm} = 16.92 \text{ cm}.
$$

$\therefore$ Perimeter of the square $= (4 \times 16.92) \text{ cm} = 67.68 \text{ cm}$.

### Example 4:

The longer side of a rectangular hall is $24 \text{ m}$, and the length of its diagonal is $26 \text{ m}$. Find the area of the hall.

#### Solution:

Let $ABCD$ be the hall in which $AB = 24 \text{ m}$ and $AC = 26 \text{ m}$.
By Pythagoras' theorem, we have

$$
BC = \sqrt{AC^2 - AB^2} \text{ units} = \sqrt{(26)^2 - (24)^2} \text{ m}
$$

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-006.jpg)

$$
= \sqrt{(26-24)(26+24)} \text{ m} = \sqrt{50 \times 2} \text{ m}
$$

$$
= \sqrt{100} \text{ m} = 10 \text{ m}
$$

Thus, length $= 24 \text{ m}$, breadth $= 10 \text{ m}$.
$\therefore$ Area of the hall $= (24 \times 10) \text{ m}^2 = 240 \text{ m}^2$.

### Example 5:

The length and the breadth of a rectangular park are in the ratio $8:5$. A path, $1.5 \text{ m}$ wide, running all around the outside of the park has an area of $594 \text{ m}^2$. Find the dimensions of the park.

#### Solution:

Let the length and the breadth of the park be $8x$ metres and $5x$ metres respectively.
Then, area of the park $= (8x \times 5x) \text{ m}^2 = (40x^2) \text{ m}^2$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-007.jpg)

Length of the park including the path $= (8x+3) \text{ m}$.
Breadth of the park including the path $= (5x+3) \text{ m}$.
Area of the park including the path $= (8x+3)(5x+3) \text{ m}^2$.
Area of the path $= \left[(8x+3)(5x+3) - 40x^2\right] \text{ m}^2 = (39x+9) \text{ m}^2$.

$\therefore 39x+9 = 594 \Rightarrow 39x = 585$

$$
\Rightarrow x = \frac{585}{39} = 15.
$$

$\therefore$ **Length** $= (8 \times 15) \text{ m} = 120 \text{ m}$,
**Breadth** $= (5 \times 15) \text{ m} = 75 \text{ m}$.

### Example 6:

A rectangular lawn, $75 \text{ m}$ by $60 \text{ m}$, has two roads, each $4 \text{ m}$ wide, running through the middle of the lawn, one parallel to length and the other parallel to breadth, as shown in the figure. Find the cost of gravelling the roads at ₹50 per $\text{m}^2$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-008.jpg)

#### Solution:

Area of the road $ABCD = (75 \times 4) \text{ m}^2 = 300 \text{ m}^2$.
Area of the road $EFGH = (60 \times 4) \text{ m}^2 = 240 \text{ m}^2$.
Clearly, area $PQRS$ is common to both the roads.
Area $PQRS = (4 \times 4) \text{ m}^2 = 16 \text{ m}^2$.
Area of the roads to be gravelled $= (300+240-16) \text{ m}^2 = 524 \text{ m}^2$.

Cost of gravelling the roads = ₹$(524 \times 50)$ = ₹$26200$.

### Example 7:

Find the area of the quadrilateral whose sides measure $9 \text{ cm}, 40 \text{ cm}, 28 \text{ cm}$ and $15 \text{ cm}$, and in which the angle between the first two sides is a right angle.

#### Solution:

Let $ABCD$ be the given quadrilateral in which $AB=9 \text{ cm}$, $BC=40 \text{ cm}$, $CD=28 \text{ cm}$, $DA=15 \text{ cm}$ and $\angle ABC = 90^{\circ}$.
By Pythagoras' theorem,
$$
AC = \sqrt{AB^2 + BC^2} \text{ units} = \sqrt{(9)^2 + (40)^2} \text{ cm} = \sqrt{1681} \text{ cm} = 41 \text{ cm}.
$$

Area of $\triangle ABC = \left(\frac{1}{2} \times AB \times BC\right) = \left(\frac{1}{2} \times 9 \times 40\right) \text{ cm}^2 = 180 \text{ cm}^2$.

In $\triangle ACD$,
Let $a = AC = 41 \text{ cm}$, $b = CD = 28 \text{ cm}$ and $c = DA = 15 \text{ cm}$.
$\therefore s = \frac{1}{2}(41+28+15) \text{ cm} = 42 \text{ cm}$.
$\therefore (s-a) = 1 \text{ cm}, (s-b) = 14 \text{ cm}$ and $(s-c) = 27 \text{ cm}$.
Area of $\triangle ACD = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)} \text{ sq units}$
$= \sqrt{42 \times 1 \times 14 \times 27} \text{ cm}^2 = (14 \times 3 \times 3) \text{ cm}^2 = 126 \text{ cm}^2$.

Area of the quadrilateral $ABCD = \text{area of } \triangle ABC + \text{area of } \triangle ACD$
$= (180+126) \text{ cm}^2 = 306 \text{ cm}^2$.

### Example 8:

In a four-sided field, the length of the longer diagonal is $120 \text{ m}$. The lengths of perpendiculars from the opposite vertices on this diagonal are $20 \text{ m}$ and $15 \text{ m}$. Find the area of the field.

#### Solution:

Let $ABCD$ be the field and $AC$ be its longer diagonal.
Let $BL \perp AC$ and $DM \perp AC$. Then,
$AC = 120 \text{ m}, BL = 20 \text{ m}$ and $DM = 15 \text{ m}$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-009.jpg)

Area of the field = $\left\{\frac{1}{2} \times (BL+DM) \times AC\right\} \text{ sq units}$
$= \left\{\frac{1}{2} \times (20+15) \times 120\right\} \text{ m}^2 = 2100 \text{ m}^2$.

### Example 9:

Find the area of a parallelogram one of whose sides measures $40 \text{ cm}$ and the corresponding height measures $19.5 \text{ cm}$.

#### Solution:

Area of the given parallelogram = $(\text{base} \times \text{height})$ sq units
$= (40 \times 19.5) \text{ cm}^2 = \left(40 \times \frac{195}{10}\right) \text{ cm}^2 = 780 \text{ cm}^2$.

### Example 10:

The adjacent sides of a parallelogram are $36 \text{ cm}$ and $27 \text{ cm}$ in length. If the distance between the shorter sides is $12 \text{ cm}$, find the distance between the longer sides.

#### Solution:

Longer side = $36 \text{ cm}$, shorter side = $27 \text{ cm}$.
Distance between the shorter sides = $12 \text{ cm}$.
Let the distance between the longer sides be $x \text{ cm}$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-010.jpg)

Area of the $\| \text{gm} = (\text{longer side} \times \text{distance between the longer sides})$
$= (\text{shorter side} \times \text{distance between the shorter sides})$
$\therefore 36 \times x = 27 \times 12 \Rightarrow x = \frac{27 \times 12}{36} = 9$.
$\therefore$ Distance between the longer sides = $9 \text{ cm}$.

### Example 11:

The diagonals of a rhombus are $48 \text{ cm}$ and $20 \text{ cm}$ long. Find (i) the area of the rhombus, and (ii) the perimeter of the rhombus.

#### Solution:

(i) Area of the rhombus
$= \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{product of diagonals}) \text{ sq units}$
$= \left(\frac{1}{2} \times 48 \times 20\right) \text{ cm}^2 = 480 \text{ cm}^2$.

(ii) We know that the diagonals of a rhombus bisect each other at right angles.
$\therefore OA = OC = 24 \text{ cm}, OB = OD = 10 \text{ cm}$ and $\angle AOB = 90^{\circ}$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-011.jpg)

$\therefore$ By Pythagoras' theorem, we have
$AB = \sqrt{OA^2 + OB^2} \text{ units} = \sqrt{(24)^2 + (10)^2} \text{ cm}$
$= \sqrt{676} \text{ cm} = 26 \text{ cm}$.

$\therefore$ Perimeter of the rhombus $= (4 \times 26) \text{ cm} = 104 \text{ cm}$.

### Example 12:

Find the area of a trapezium whose parallel sides are $35 \text{ cm}$ and $23 \text{ cm}$ long and the distance between them is $15 \text{ cm}$.

#### Solution:

Area of the trapezium
$= \frac{1}{2} \times (\text{sum of parallel sides}) \times (\text{distance between them})$
$= \left\{\frac{1}{2} \times (35+23) \times 15\right\} \text{ cm}^2 = 435 \text{ cm}^2$.

### Example 13:

Find the area of a trapezium $ABCD$ in which $AB \| DC, AB=77 \text{ cm}, BC=25 \text{ cm}, CD=60 \text{ cm}$ and $DA=26 \text{ cm}$. [HOTS]

#### Solution:

Draw $DE \| BC$ and $DL \perp AB$.
Then, $DE = BC = 25 \text{ cm}$.
$AE = (AB - EB) = (AB - DC) = (77-60) \text{ cm} = 17 \text{ cm}$.

![](file:///C:/Obsidian-finished-EX-worksheet_resources/TextBooK/Class-10/Mensuration/Perimeter-and-Area-of-Plane-Figures/class-10-Ch-15-B-BooK-012.jpg)

In $\triangle DAE$, we have
$a = AE = 17 \text{ cm}, b = DE = 25 \text{ cm}$ and $c = DA = 26 \text{ cm}$.
$\therefore s = \frac{1}{2}(17+25+26) \text{ cm} = 34 \text{ cm}$.
$(s-a) = 17 \text{ cm}, (s-b) = 9 \text{ cm}$ and $(s-c) = 8 \text{ cm}$.

$\therefore$ Area of $\triangle DAE = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)}$
$= \sqrt{34 \times 17 \times 9 \times 8} \text{ cm}^2$
$= (17 \times 3 \times 4) \text{ cm}^2 = 204 \text{ cm}^2$.

Also, area of $\triangle DAE = \frac{1}{2} \times AE \times DL \text{ sq units}$
$= \frac{1}{2} \times 17 \text{ cm} \times DL$.
$\therefore \frac{1}{2} \times 17 \text{ cm} \times DL = 204 \text{ cm}^2 \Rightarrow DL = \left(\frac{204 \times 2}{17}\right) \text{ cm} = 24 \text{ cm}$.

ar(trap. $ABCD$) = $\left\{\frac{1}{2}(AB+DC) \times DL\right\} \text{ sq units}$
$= \left\{\frac{1}{2}(77+60) \times 24\right\} \text{ cm}^2 = 1644 \text{ cm}^2$.
